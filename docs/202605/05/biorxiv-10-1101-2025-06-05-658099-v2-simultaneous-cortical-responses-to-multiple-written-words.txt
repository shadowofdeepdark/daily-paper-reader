ABSTRACT 16 

17 We have learned much about the brain regions that support reading by measuring 18 neuronal responses to single words, but we know little about how the brain processes multiple 19 words simultaneously. This fMRI study fills that gap by varying the number of English words 20 presented while holding the amount of visual stimulation constant. We adapted the 21 “simultaneous suppression” paradigm, which has demonstrated that the response to multiple 22 stimuli presented simultaneously is typically smaller than the sum of responses to the same 23 stimuli presented sequentially. On each trial, participants viewed a rapid sequence of three 24 frames. Each frame contained two character strings, most of which were pseudo-letters with 25 visual features matched to familiar letters. The experimental conditions differed in the number of 26 words in the sequence: zero words; one word; two words sequentially; or two words 27 simultaneously. Behavioral task accuracy was worse for detecting two words presented 28 simultaneously than sequentially. BOLD responses increased linearly with the number of words 29 presented in several reading-related regions of the left hemisphere: text-selective occipito-30 temporal regions, the superior temporal sulcus, the intraparietal sulcus, and the inferior frontal 31 sulcus. In all of those regions, responses did not differ between sequential and simultaneous 32 presentation of two words. Nonetheless, the sensitivity of ventral temporal text-selective regions 33 to the lexical frequencies of two words was attenuated by simultaneous presentation. To 34 account for these patterns of activity and task performance, we suggest that the reading 35 network can detect two strings of letters simultaneously, but there is interference during lexical 36 access. 37 38

INTRODUCTION 23To read this page, you must integrate information across many hierarchically arranged 4visual elements, from letters to words to sentences. Fluent reading requires a balance between 5holistic parallel processing of multiple elements and serial processing of chunks of text to avoid 6interference. Here, we investigate how reading-related brain areas process multiple words 7simultaneously. 8Most fMRI studies of visual word recognition have presented only one word at a time 9(e.g., Caffarra et al., 2021; Dehaene & Cohen, 2011; Yeatman & White, 2021). Others analyzed 10 activity during sentence reading (e.g., Carter et al., 2019; Henderson et al., 2015; Schuster et 11 al., 2015) but were designed for other questions. EEG and MEG studies have provided 12 important insights into the dynamics of multi-word processing (e.g., Dunagan et al., 2025; Fallon 13 & Pylkkänen, 2024; Flick et al., 2021; Schotter et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025), but fMRI offers 14 superior spatial resolution for specific reading-related regions. 15 Behavioral studies have raised deep questions about how efficiently readers can 16 process multiple words in parallel. The parafoveal preview benefit , for example, demonstrates 17 that while readers fixate on word N, they begin processing word N+1 (Schotter et al., 2012; 18 Vasilev & Angele, 2017). Some models of reading account for such effects by allowing parallel 19 processing of multiple words with divided attention (Engbert et al., 2005; Reilly & Radach, 2006; 20 Snell et al., 2018; Snell & Grainger, 2019). Other models assume that readers finish processing 21 the fixated word before shifting attention covertly to the next word(s) (Pollatsek et al., 2006; 22 Schotter et al., 2014). Several experimental tasks have also provided support for serial models 23 (White et al., 2018, 2020; Brothers et al., 2022; Campbell et al., 2024; Johnson et al., 2022). 24 Behavioral deficits might arise from limited processing capacity in the “visual word form 25 area” (VWFA), a left ventral occipito-temporal region that links the visual system and the 26 language system (Dehaene & Cohen 2011; Yeatman & White, 2021). White et al. (2019) argued 27 that a posterior VWFA sub-region can process two words at once in separable “channels”. In 28 contrast, activity in the anterior sub-region was affected by the lexical frequency of only one of 29 two words per trial, consistent with a processing ‘bottleneck’ during lexical access (see also 30 White et al. 2020). However, that study did not include a comparison condition with words 31 presented one at a time. 32 Here we leverage another neural correlate of processing capacity limits in vision: 33 

simultaneous suppression : a lower BOLD response to a set of stimuli flashed simultaneously 34

Ours is the first study to draw on this literature to 4investigate how the responses in text- and language-selective regions vary as a function of the 5number of words being read. 6Previously reported simultaneous suppression effects could be due to more stimulus 7onset and offsets in the sequential condition, each of which causes a transient neural response, 8rather than the competitive interactions between stimuli (Kupers et al., 2024). The first question is whether 11 BOLD responses (relative to baseline with no stimulation) increase steadily as the number of 12 words increases. The second question is whether the response to two words presented 13 simultaneously is as strong as the summed response to two words presented sequentially. We 14 also compare behavioral performance across those conditions, similar to previous studies that 15 have argued for limited processing capacity (e.g., Scharff et al 2011; White et al. 2026). 16 Following White et al. Zipf is a standardized measure of word frequency calculated through an adjustment of the 8log frequency per million words in the SUBTLEX-US corpus (Brysbaert & New, 2009; van 9Heuven et al., 2014). 10 Real words were presented in the Courier New, a standard monospaced serif font. The 11 other “filler” strings were presented in an unfamiliar, illegible false font called BACS-2 (Vidal et 12 al., 2017). These false font characters are designed to match the size, the symmetry, and the 13 numbers of strokes, junctions and terminations of Courier New. Both these fonts were scaled so 14 that the height of the letter ‘x’ was 0.41 dva. On average, the width of each character string was 15 2.65 dva, and the height was between 0.41 and 0.79 dva. The distance between centers of 16 neighboring letters was 0.55 dva. The stimuli were rendered in the same black color as the 17 fixation dot. 18 19 Trial sequence and task 20 The stimulus presentation on each trial consisted of three successive frames. Two 21 character strings were presented in each frame, one above and one below the fixation dot. The 22 centers of the character strings were 1.5 dva from the fixation dot. This display arrangement 23 differs from the conditions of natural English reading, but we chose it for two reasons: because 24 behavioral evidence for serial processing of words is strong with that arrangement (White et al., 25 2020), and because both words are equally near to fixation and clearly legible. Arranging the 26 words horizontally would better match the conditions of natural reading, but there is a strong 27 right>left hemifield asymmetry (Yeatman & White, 2021). Indeed, a strong bias to process 28 words on the right complicated the results of White et al (2019). Crucially, the VWFA is known to 29 respond strongly to words in a region spanning roughly the central 5 degrees of the visual field 30 (R. K. Le et al., 2017; White et al., 2023), including just above and below fixation (Rauschecker 31 et al., 2012). Thus, the population receptive fields (PRFs) of the voxels we analyze are known to 32 cover both of the stimulus positions. 33

9The first step in our analysis of the eye-tracking data was to determine which trials had 1data of acceptable quality. A whole run was excluded from the analysis of gaze positions if the 2median standard deviation of gaze positions 250 ms before stimulus presentation was greater 3than 1º. We chose that interval because participants were usually fixating stably just before the 4stimuli arrived. Rapidly changing position estimates during that interval are a sign of a poor data 5quality. 5 runs were excluded from the eye-tracking analysis for this reason (from a total of 3 6participants). 7Next, within included runs, each trial was deemed analyzable if, during the 650 ms of 8stimulus presentation, at least 80% of the gaze position samples were acceptable. 9Unacceptable samples are those with missing position values or positions >10º from the fixation 10 mark, which is implausible and a sign of calibration failure. After applying those criteria, we had 11 acceptable gaze data on 51% of trials. For 8 of the participants, more than 95% of trials were 12 acceptable, and the rest covered a wide range. 13 For the trials with acceptable gaze data, we detected fixation breaks as follows: We first 14 conducted a drift correction within each run. Specifically, across all trials in the run, we 15 computed the median horizontal and vertical gaze position in the 250 ms before stimulus onset. 16 Then, we defined the corrected fixation position as the across-trial median of those medians, 17 including only trials in which at least 90% of the eye position samples in the pre-stimulus interval 18 were acceptable. 19 Next, we analyzed the gaze position recorded during the 650 ms of stimulus 20 presentation on each trial, calculating the distance of each gaze sample from the corrected 21 fixation position. We defined a fixation break as a deviation of more than 1.5º from the corrected 22 fixation position for at least 100 ms, or if the participant blinked (as evidenced by pupil size 23 dropping to 0 for at least 100 ms during the stimuli). We also detected saccades using the 24 method described by Engbert & Mergenthaler (2006). Saccades were events in which the two-25 dimensional gaze position velocity exceeded, for at least 6 ms, an ellipse with horizontal and 26 vertical radii equal to 4 times the horizontal and vertical median-based standard deviations, 27 respectively. Those standard deviations were calculated in the interval from 250 ms prior to 28 stimulus onset until stimulus offset. Saccades had to be >1º and <15º in amplitude. We also 29 categorized individual trials as fixation breaks if the vertical distance of the saccade exceeded 30 1.5º. 31

11 BOLD Response Estimation 1We estimated BOLD responses (beta weights) to each single trial with GLMSingle in 2Python (Prince et al., 2022). The result of this analysis is a beta weight for each trial, for each 3cortical surface node in each participant. We first resampled the BOLD time-series from 1.5s to 41s. GLMSingle optimizes the hemodynamic response function for each node in the surface 5space and performs denoising using fractional ridge regression to remove correlated noise. For 6the main experiment, the design matrix consisted of a unique column for each stimulus 7condition (zero, one, two-sequential or two-simultaneous words), uniquely coding trials in which 8the top word appeared in the first or second frame, and uniquely coding the frame in which the 9words in the 1-word or 2-simultaneous conditions appeared. For the visual category localizer, 10 each stimulus category received its own column. For the language localizer, there were two 11 columns: sentence trials and nonsense trials. 12 13 ROI Definition 14 We sought to define nine regions of interest (ROIs) in each hemisphere in each 15 participant. There are a range of explanations for this effect, but it is generally agreed that low-3frequency words require more time and/or effort for the word recognition system (Taylor et al. 42013; Gagl et al. 2022). If the VWFA acts as the “orthographic lexicon,” high-frequency words 5evoke lower responses there because of more rapid lexical access (Kronbichler et al., 2004; 6Wimmer & Ludersdorfer, 2018). 33 The limited-capacity parallel model ( Figure 6B ) assumes that mFus-words processes 1two words in parallel, but with a cost: two words presented simultaneously interfere with each 2other in the sense that both of their activations are slowed . The simulations for sequential trials 3are identical to the serial model, producing a clear lexical frequency effect ( Figure 6B , top two 4panels). However, for the simultaneous trials, the slope of evidence accumulation for all words 5(i.e., activation over time) is shallower. That is the effect of the processing capacity limit, which 6could be accounted for by mutual inhibition among candidate lexical units that are activated by 7both words. Thus, neither of the words fully reach threshold before processing is interrupted, 8regardless of their frequency, and there is no difference between the total BOLD responses on 9high- and low-frequency trials ( Figure 6B , bottom right panel, purple bars). Like the serial 10 model, this model also correctly predicts that the responses to both high- and low-frequency 11 words in the simultaneous condition are similar to the response to high-frequency words in the 12 sequential condition. 13 Thus, it is possible to account for the lexical frequency effects with two different models. 14 We did not quantitatively fit these simple models to the data, but we offer each as a proof of 15 concept for a mechanistic account of activity in text-selective regions. Importantly, both models 16 assume that that there is some kind of processing capacity limit. One assumes that there is a 17 serial bottleneck in lexical identification, but that two high-frequency words can be at least 18 partially processed in sequence within a single frame. The other assumes that there is parallel 19 activation for two words at once, but with interference that manifests as a significant slowing of 20 evidence accumulation. Figure 6 shows that both models predict similar patterns as we 21 observed in the data, but they are not identical. The parallel model, given its assumption that the 22 interference is so great that no words reach their activation thresholds in the simultaneous 23 condition, predicts 0 lexical frequency effect in that condition. The serial model, given the 24 parameter settings that allow one word to reach its activation threshold on each trial and 25 another to begin processing, will always predict a small lexical frequency effect in the 26 simultaneous condition, because of the higher threshold for low-frequency words that is reached 27 for at least one word. However, our data do not clearly distinguish between the serial model’s 28 prediction of a small effect and the parallel model’s prediction of 0 effect in the simultaneous 29 condition. Thus, we are not attempting to draw any conclusions from the minor differences in the 30 model outputs for the two models. 31 Future studies could explore stimulus and task conditions for which these two models 32 make clearly different predictions. DISCUSSION 12 We first established that in ventral temporal text-selective regions, and in frontal and 13 temporal language-processing regions, BOLD response magnitudes steadily increased with the 14 number of words presented, from 0 to 1 to 2. Behaviorally, participants were less likely to detect 15 both of two words that were presented simultaneously than sequentially, suggesting a16 processing capacity limit (Scharf et al., 2011; White et al., 2020; White et al., 2026). The neural 17 correlate of that capacity limit could have presented as a weaker BOLD response to two words 18 presented simultaneously as compared to sequentially, as occurs for other types of stimuli in 19 visual cortex (Kaiser et al., 2014; Kastner, Weerd, et al., 2001; Miller et al., 1993; Shim et al., 20 2013). However, we did not observe statistically significant simultaneous suppression in any of 21 our regions of interest nor in a whole-cortex analysis. These data demonstrate that text-22 selective regions can respond to two written words simultaneously. 23 However, there is a distinction between simply detecting two letter strings and identifying 24 them as specific words in the mental lexicon. We also found that in ventral temporal text-25 selective areas, the response was higher for two low-frequency words than two high-frequency 26 words, but less so in the simultaneous condition. The statistical evidence in this analysis was 27 modest and must be followed up with further research. Our preliminary interpretation is that 28 lexical access was impaired by the simultaneous presentation, resulting in less sensitivity to the 29 words’ frequencies. We proposed two simple models to account for those effects, either 30 assuming serial processing or parallel processing with interference ( Figure 6 ). 31

35 We now consider four factors of our empirical design that may explain the absence of a 1neural simultaneous suppression effect: 21. Visual Stimulation: We equated the numbers of characters and stimulus onsets and 3offsets across conditions. Had we not done so, but simply presented a variable number of words 4without the ‘filler’ false font strings, we may have found a simultaneous suppression effect. 5However, such an effect could be due to nonlinear temporal integration of responses to stimulus 6transients, rather than competition for limited resources (Kupers et al. 2024). 72. Stimulus Duration: Each word was presented for 183 ms, which is short enough to 8minimize goal-directed eye movements within a frame. The interstimulus interval was 233 ms, 9roughly the average fixation duration during reading (Rayner et al., 2016). This interval is also 10 not far from the 250 ms used in classic simultaneous suppression studies (e.g., Kastner et al., 11 1998; Kastner & Ungerleider, 2001; McMains & Kastner, 2011). However, there is some 12 evidence that the effect increases as stimulus durations increase (Kupers et al., 2024). We 13 chose not to make the time between frames any longer because that could invite eye 14 movements. But we cannot definitively rule out shifts of covert attention between words within a 15 single frame (see Figure 6A ). If subjects attempted that strategy, however, they were not 16 entirely successful, because task accuracy was impaired on simultaneous trials. Nonetheless, 17 we might have found stronger simultaneous suppression had the presentation durations been 18 even shorter, to prevent (partial) sequential processing of simultaneously presented words. 19 3. Stimulus Location: Unlike many past studies (e.g., Kastner et al. 1999; Kupers et al. 20 2024), we presented just two stimuli, each 1.5º from fixation. Both were inside the large 21 population receptive fields of voxels in the ventral temporal cortex that cover roughly the central 22 5º of the visual field (Le et al., 2017). Therefore, we expected a strong effect in ventral temporal 23 cortex (Kastner, De Weerd, et al., 2001; Kupers et al., 2024). To minimize the distance between 24 words, we presented them above and below fixation. If anything, we would be less likely to find 25 simultaneous suppression with the words presented to the left and right of fixation, which would 26 be more familiar to English readers. 27 4. Top-down attention: While early studies found the strongest simultaneous 28 suppression effect when the stimuli are ignored (Kastner et al., 1998; S. McMains & Kastner, 29 2011), it is also present when the stimuli are attended (Scalf et al., 2011). As our question is 30 whether two words can be read simultaneously, we made our stimuli task-relevant. Voluntary 31 effort to read amplifies activity in the VWFA, possibly via top-down signals from language 32

The parallel stage is consistent with an 23 existing model of reading that proposes that multiple words are processed in parallel at the 24 stage of activating letter or bigram detectors (Grainger et al., 2014, 2016; Snell et al., 2018; 25 Snell & Grainger, 2019).
