## Abstract 45 

Speech perception often takes place in environments with competing sensory inputs, both within 46 

the auditory modality and across modalities; for example, following a conversation in a noisy 47 

cafe while simultaneously reading a menu. This study examined the extent to which dividing 48 

attention between auditory and visual modalities (bimodal divided attention) influences linguistic 49 

context processing across hierarchical levels during continuous speech perception in noise. 50 

Electroencephalographic (EEG) responses were recorded while participants listened to 51 

audiobook stories in multitalker babble as a secondary task, concurrently performing a52 

demanding primary visual task that imposed either low or high cognitive load. Behaviorally, 53 

speech comprehension accuracy was significantly lower under high-load than low-load dual-task 54 

conditions. Multivariate temporal response function (mTRF) encoding models were used to 55 

predict EEG responses from information-theoretic measures (entropy and surprisal) indexing 56 

linguistic context at sublexical, word-form, and sentence levels. Significant neutral tracking was 57 

observed at the word-form and sentence levels, but not the sublexical level. Critically, neutral 58 

tracking of sentence-level linguistic representations was significantly reduced under high 59 

compared to low load, with effects emerging at latencies beyond 200 ms. In contrast, neutral 60 

tracking of word-form-level representations was unaffected by dual-task load. mTRF analyses 61 

further revealed that neutral tracking of acoustic features was not modulated by dual-task load. 62 

These findings indicate that bimodal divided attention selectively disrupts cortical 63 

representations of sentence-level linguistic context, while lower-level processing remains 64 

relatively preserved. Such impairments in higher-level linguistic processing may contribute to 65 

reduced speech comprehension during multitasking in noisy environments. 66

## Introduction 72 

In everyday life, speech perception often unfolds amid competing sensory inputs, both within the 73 

auditory modality and across other modalities; for example, following a conversation in a noisy 74 

cafe while simultaneously reading a menu. Such complex listening environments place 75 

substantial demands on perceptual and cognitive resources and can lead to communication 76 

breakdowns. Successful speech perception under these conditions requires listeners to allocate 77 

attention to the auditory modality while managing demands from nonauditory inputs, to 78 

segregate target speech from background noise, and to engage selective attention mechanisms 79 

that enhance target speech while suppressing interfering sounds (Shinn-Cunningham, 2008). The 80 

present study investigated the neural mechanisms underlying the effects of dividing attention 81 

between modalities—specifically audition and vision (i.e., bimodal divided attention)—on the 82 

processing of natural continuous speech in noise. 83 

Speech processing entails the extraction and encoding of acoustic information from the 84 

speech signal and its progressive transformation into linguistic representations of increasing 85 

complexity (Brodbeck & Simon, 2020; Hickok & Poeppel, 2007). A key mechanism proposed to 86 

support this transformation is the use of linguistic context across multiple hierarchical levels to 87 

facilitate the interpretation of incoming speech input (Kuperberg & Jaeger, 2016; Ryskin & 88 

Nieuwland, 2023). Research on linguistic context processing has traditionally relied on 89 

paradigms employing highly controlled, constructed stimuli, such as isolated words (e.g., 90 

Ganong, 1980; Giovannone & Theodore, 2021; Gwilliams et al., 2018; Lam et al., 2017; Sohoglu 91 

et al., 2024) or sentences (e.g., Connine et al., 1991; Hsin et al., 2023; Hsin & Lee, 2024; Obleser 92 

& Kotz, 2011; Sarrett et al., 2020; Strauß et al., 2013; Xie, 2025). Over the past decade, 93 

however, there has been a growing shift toward paradigms that use natural, continuous speech, 94

Of relevance to the present study, Brodbeck et al. (2022) asked participants to listen to 97 

continuous narrative speech (i.e., audiobook stories) under quiet listening conditions while neural 98 

responses were recorded. They examined how linguistic context at three hierarchical levels 99 

modulated neural responses to phonemes. The sublexical-level context incorporated local 100 

phoneme context (i.e., the preceding four phonemes) to predict upcoming phonemes. The word-101 

form-level context, grounded in the cohort model of word recognition (Marslen-Wilson, 1987), 102 

incorporated word statistics (i.e., the probability of words with a given phoneme sequence in the 103 

lexicon) to predict upcoming phonemes. The sentence-level context extended the word-form 104 

model by additionally incorporating sentence context (i.e., the four preceding words) to estimate 105 

word-level statistics. The authors found that linguistic context at all three levels significantly 106 

modulated neural responses to phonemes. Effects for all context levels were observed in the 107 

superior temporal gyrus, with the sentence-level context additionally engaging more ventral 108 

regions of the temporal lobe. In terms of temporal dynamics, all context levels modulated early 109 

neural responses within the first 100 ms; however, only the sentence-level context influenced 110 

later responses, emerging after approximately 150 ms and persisting up to ~500 ms. Together, 111 

these findings suggest that continuous speech processing involves the parallel use of linguistic 112 

context across multiple hierarchical levels, including sublexical-, word-form-, and sentence-level 113 

representations (see also, e.g., Brodbeck et al., 2024; Karunathilake et al., 2025). 114 

However, because this and other related work have focused on speech processing under 115 

auditory-only attention, it remains unclear whether and how bimodal divided attention modulates 116 

linguistic context processing across these hierarchical levels. Addressing this issue is essential 117

electroencephalography (EEG) in conjunction with an audiovisual dual-task paradigm. Bimodal 121 

divided attention was operationalized by requiring participants to perform a demanding primary 122 

visual task under either low or high cognitive load while concurrently listening to continuous 123

with an auditory single-task condition, in which participants focused exclusively on the speech 125 

stimuli while disregarding concurrent visual stimuli. 126 

Xie et al. (2023) replicated the core findings from Brodbeck et al. (2022), demonstrating 127 

that linguistic context at sublexical, word-form, and sentence levels significantly modulated 128 

neural responses to phonemes. Crucially, neural responses reflecting these context effects were 129 

stronger in the auditory single-task condition than in either dual-task condition, with no 130 

significant difference observed between the low- and high-load dual-task conditions. Although 131 

divided attention across modalities attenuated context-related neural responses, these effects 132 

were not eliminated under dual-task conditions. Moreover, the magnitude and pattern of 133 

attention-related modulation was comparable across all three levels of linguistic context. 134 

Collectively, these findings suggest that bimodal divided attention may reduce—but does not 135 

abolish—the use of linguistic context during continuous speech processing and that its influence 136 

appears relatively uniform across hierarchical levels of linguistic representations. 137 

Notably, however, this study examined speech processing exclusively under quiet 138 

listening conditions. As a result, it remains unclear to what extent additional processing demands 139 

imposed by background noise further influence the attentional modulation of linguistic context 140

2018 versus divided attention in Xie et al., 2023). Accordingly, the present study aims to 154 

investigate how bimodal divided attention modulates linguistic context processing across 155 

multiple hierarchical levels during continuous speech perception in noise. 156

manipulate bimodal divided attention. Participants performed a demanding primary visual task 158 

under either low or high cognitive load while concurrently listening to continuous speech in 159 

noise as a secondary task. We compared the two dual-task conditions (low versus high load) to 160 

assess the effects of bimodal divided attention on linguistic context processing. We tested the 161 

hypothesis that sentence-level context processing would be more susceptible to attentional 162 

modulation than word-form and sublexical context processing, owing to its reliance on tracking 163

## Materials & Methods 167 

Participants 168 

The study included 24 younger adults (20 females) aged 18-23, recruited from the Tallahassee, 169 

Florida, area. The sample size was determined based on previous research on bimodal attention 170 

and its effects on the neural processing of speech (e.g., Gennari et al., 2018; Kasper et al., 2014; 171 

Xie et al., 2023). All participants had normal hearing and normal or corrected-to-normal vision. 172 

Normal hearing was defined as having pure-tone air-conduction thresholds  ≤ 25  dB hearing 173 

level at octave frequencies from 0.25 to 8  kHz in both ears, assessed using an Interacoustics 174 

Equinox 2.0 PC-based audiometer (Interacoustics, Middelfart, Denmark). All participants were 175 

native speakers of English; four reported speaking a second language (three in Spanish and one 176 

in Hindi). No participant reported a history of head trauma. The study protocol was approved by 177 

the Institutional Review Board at Florida State University (IRB#: STUDY00003674). Written 178 

informed consent was obtained from all participants, who received monetary compensation for 179 

their participation. 180

that work for additional details. 183

speech in noise. Visual stimuli were blue squares appearing at one of eight positions surrounding 186

Electrophysiological data acquisition and preprocessing 235 

Acquisition 236 

The experiment took place in a dimly lit, sound-attenuating booth. Participants were seated 237 

comfortably throughout the tasks. Electroencephalography (EEG) signals were recorded at a 238 

sampling rate of 5 kHz using an Easycap recording cap (www.easycap.de) equipped with 64 239 

actiCAP active electrodes (Brain Products, Gilching, Munich, Germany). Electrode placement 240 

followed the extended 10-20 system (Oostenveld & Praamstra, 2001), with the Fpz site serving 241 

as the ground and Fz as the reference. All electrode impedances were maintained below 25 k Ω.242 

EEG data were acquired using a Brainvision actiCHamp Plus amplifier linked to BrainVision 243 

Recorder software V. 1.25.0001. 244 

Preprocessing 245 

The raw EEG data were preprocessed offline using custom scripts implemented in MNE-Python 246 

(Gramfort et al., 2013) and EEGLAB (Delorme & Makeig, 2004). The raw data were re-247 

referenced to the average of electrodes TP9 and TP10, followed by band-pass filtering between 1 248 

and 15 Hz using a Hamming-windowed sinc FIR filter with default EEGLAB settings. The data 249 

were then segmented into 66-second epochs, time-locked to the onset of the corresponding story 250 

segments, and downsampled to 500 Hz. Independent component analysis was applied to the 251 

epoched data pooled across both task conditions for each participant using the Infomax 252 

algorithm. Artifact-related components, specifically those associated with muscle activity and 253

Assessing neural tracking of visual and auditory stimuli 256 

The multivariate temporal response function (mTRF) approach (Crosse et al., 2016; Ding & 257 

Simon, 2012), implemented via the Eelbrain Python toolkit (Brodbeck et al., 2023) was used to 258 

assess neural tracking of visual and auditory stimuli. This technique employs time-delayed 259 

multiple regression to predict EEG signals from stimulus-derived predictor variables. The 260 

predictive power of each predictor set (e.g., visual features) was interpreted as an index of the 261 

strength of the corresponding neural representation. Examples of these predictors are shown in 262 

Figure 1. Visual and acoustic predictors were estimated following procedures similar to those 263 

described in Xie et al. (2023), while linguistic predictors were directly adopted from that work. A 264 

brief overview of these predictors is provided below; for additional methodological details, see 265 

Xie et al. (2023). To enhance computational efficiency, both EEG data and predictor variables 266 

were downsampled to 100 Hz before the mTRF analysis. Additionally, only the first 56 seconds 267 

of each trial were included for the mTRF analysis to match the shortest trial duration. 268

The visual predictor consisted of a one-dimensional time series with an impulse at the onset and 275 

offset of each blue square. To account for potential neural activity related to participants’ 276 

responses, an additional one-dimensional time series was included, containing impulses at time 277 

points when participants responded to the visual stimuli. 278 

Acoustic predictors were designed to capture the spectro-temporal properties of both the 279 

target speech and background noise. These predictors were derived from high-resolution 280 

gammatone spectrograms spanning 256 frequency bands from 20 to 10,000 Hz. An envelope 281 

spectrogram predictor was obtained by summing the 256-band spectrogram into eight 282 

logarithmically spaced frequency bands. Additionally, an onset spectrogram predictor was 283 

created by applying an auditory edge detection model (Brodbeck et al., 2020; Fishbach et al., 284 

2001) to each band of the 256-band spectrograms, and then summing them into eight 285 

logarithmically spaced frequency bands. 286 

Linguistic predictors 287 

Linguistic predictors were computed only for the target speech, comprising information-theoretic 288 

measures—surprisal and entropy—of phoneme sequences using sublexical, word-form, and 289 

sentence models (briefly described below). These measures were expressed as time series with 290 

impulses of varying magnitude at each phoneme onset. Phoneme sequences and their onsets were 291 

obtained from the speech stimuli through forced alignment with the Montreal Forced Aligner 292 

(McAuliffe et al., 2017). 293 

Sublexical model . The sublexical model uses a 5-gram model (Heafield, 2011) trained 294 

on phoneme sequences from all sentences in the SUBTLEX-US corpus (Keuleers et al., 2010), 295 

without word boundary removed. It predicts each current phoneme from the four preceding ones 296

and was applied to target phoneme sequences to generate sublexical surprisal and entropy 297 

predictors. Sublexical surprisal represents the level of probabilistic surprisal of the current 298 

phoneme given its four-phoneme context, while sublexical entropy captures the uncertainty 299 

about the next phoneme based on the same context. These predictors are limited to simple 300 

phoneme sequence statistics and do not account for word boundaries (Brodbeck et al., 2022; 301 

Vitevitch & Luce, 1999, 2016). 302 

Word-form model. The word-form model is grounded in the cohort model of word 303 

recognition (Brodbeck et al., 2018b; Marslen-Wilson, 1987). Each word was assigned a prior 304 

probability based on its count frequency in the SUBTLEX corpus (Keuleers et al., 2010), with a 305 

default value of 1 for words not present in the corpus. For each word in the target speech, the 306 

model begins with the full lexicon and progressively removes words that do not match the 307 

observed phoneme sequence as each phoneme is processed. The evolving probability 308 

distribution over the lexicon at each phoneme position was used to compute word-form surprisal 309 

and entropy predictors. Word-form surprisal reflects the level of probabilistic surprisal of the 310 

current phoneme given the preceding phonemes within the word, while word-form entropy 311 

represents the uncertainty of all words currently in the cohort that match the observed phoneme 312 

sequence. The lexicon was constructed by combining pronunciations from the Montreal Forced 313 

Aligner English dictionary and the Carnegie Mellon University Pronouncing Dictionary 314 

(http://www.speech.cs.cmu.edu/cgi-bin/cmudict). 315 

Sentence model. The sentence model applies the same procedure to the word-form 316 

model to compute sentence surprisal and entropy predictors, with one key difference: the prior 317 

probability of each word was defined from the four preceding words, using a lexical 5-gram 318 

model (Heafield, 2011) trained on the whole SUBTLEX-US corpus (Keuleers et al., 2010). 319

Estimation of neural tracking 325 

This study examined to extent to which neutral tracking of visual information and the acoustic 326 

and linguistic properties of target speech differ across the two task conditions. For each 327 

participant and condition, a forward encoding mTRF model was applied, using the previously 328 

defined predictors to model EEG responses at individual electrodes. Models were estimated 329 

using a boosting algorithm with 5-fold cross-validation, implemented in Eelbrain. The mTRFs 330 

were parameterized with 50-ms Hamming basis functions spanning the stimulus-EEG lag from -331 

100 to 500 ms, and all predictors were jointly optimized via coordinate descent to minimize ℓ2332 

loss. 333 

To implement 5-fold cross-validation, the data (n = 15 trials) were partitioned into 5 folds 334 

(3 trials per fold). In each iteration, one fold was held out for testing, while the remaining folds 335 

rotated as the validation set, yielding four mTRFs per testing fold. These mTRFs were averaged 336 

to produce a single mTRF for that iteration, which was then used to predict EEG in the held-out 337 

(testing) fold. Predicted EEG responses from all five testing folds were concatenated to estimate 338 

a per-electrode measure of model performance, i.e., the proportion of the variation in the actual 339 

EEG response that is explained by the model (i.e., % explained variance). Final mTRFs per 340 

electrode were obtained by averaging the mTRFs across all testing folds. 341

and entropy; and sentence  level processing by removing sentence surprisal and entropy. The 351 

change in % explained variance (i.e., Δ% explained) between the full and reduced models was 352 

used to index the strength of neutral tracking for the corresponding predictor(s). 353 

Statistical analysis 354 

All statistical analyses were performed in Eelbrain, unless otherwise noted. 355 

First, we examined the impact of task condition (low vs. high load) on behavioral 356 

performance and on neural tracking of visual, acoustic, and linguistic properties, using one-sided 357 

paired t-tests with an alpha level of .05. Effect sizes (Cohen’s d) and Bayes Factors (BF) were 358 

also estimated. Additional details are provided below. 359 

Behavioral performance was indexed by three measures: visual accuracy, visual reaction 360 

time (RT), and auditory accuracy. Visual accuracy was calculated as the hit rate (i.e., correct 361 

responses to targets) minus the false alarm rate (i.e., incorrect identification of non-targets as 362 

targets). Visual RT was defined as the median RT for hits only. Auditory accuracy was 363 

expressed as the percentage of correctly answered story comprehension questions. The analyses 364

The Δ% explained values were averaged across task conditions and tested against zero using a 368

## Discussion 564 

The present study examined the extent to which bimodal divided attention modulates linguistic 565 

context processing at sublexical, word-form, and sentence levels during continuous speech 566 

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted May 1, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.28.721419 doi: bioRxiv preprint

perception in multi-talker babble. Consistent with the hypothesis, increasing dual-task load 567 

selectively reduced neural tracking of sentence-level linguistic information (Figure 3), whereas 568 

no significant effects were observed at the sublexical or word-form levels (Figure 4). 569 

Furthermore, dual-task load did not significantly alter neural tracking of acoustic features in 570 

continuous speech (Figure 5 and 6), even though both behavioral performance (Figure 2) and 571 

neural indices (Figure 7) clearly indicated that the high-load condition imposed greater 572 

processing demands. 573 

Hierarchical levels of linguistic context processing in quiet and noise 574 

This study largely replicated prior findings demonstrating the parallel use of linguistic context 575 

across multiple hierarchical levels (e.g., Brodbeck et al., 2022, 2024; Karunathilake et al., 2025; 576 

Xie et al., 2023). However, unlike previous work, the current study did not observe significant 577 

cortical tracking of sublexical information. A key methodological difference is that earlier 578 

studies presented continuous speech stimuli under quiet listening conditions (e.g., Brodbeck et 579 

al., 2022, 2024; Karunathilake et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2023), whereas the present study 580 

introduced background noise. Prior research has shown that neural responses associated with 581 

sublexical context processing were relatively weaker than those observed at word-form and 582 

sentence levels (Brodbeck et al., 2022). Consequently, sublexical context processing may be 583 

more vulnerable to noise interferences, potentially accounting for the absence of significant 584 

neural tracking of sublexical information in the current study. 585 

The central question of the present study was whether and how these multiple levels of 586 

linguistic context processing are differentially affected by bimodal divided attention. Our results 587 

showed that increasing dual-task load selectively attenuated neural tracking of sentence-level 588 

linguistic information, whereas sublexical or word-form context processing remained unaffected. 589 

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted May 1, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.28.721419 doi: bioRxiv preprint

The absence of dual-task load effects at the sublexical or word-form levels is consistent with 590 

previous findings reported by Xie et al. (2023), who examined continuous speech perception 591 

under quiet listening conditions. In contrast, the selective reduction in sentence-level tracking 592 

observed here diverges from their results, which showed no dual-task load effects at this level. 593 

One possible explanation is that relative to sublexical or word-form levels, sentence-level 594 

context processing depends on the tracking and integration of linguistic information over longer 595 

timescale and therefore draws more heavily on domain-general cognitive resources (Alexander 596 

& Brown, 2018; Miller & Cohen, 2001; Shain et al., 2022). When background noise is 597 

introduced, these cognitive demands are likely amplified, rendering sentence-level processing 598 

more susceptible to the effects of increased dual-task load. 599 

Our observation that higher-level linguistic processing is selectively reduced under 600 

increased dual-task demands appear to align with prior work using highly controlled, laboratory-601 

based stimuli. For instance, studies examining lexical context effects on word identification 602 

within the Ganong paradigm (Ganong, 1980) have shown that increasing cognitive load via dual-603 

task manipulations resulted in only modest changes in the lexical context effect among younger 604 

adults with normal hearing (Derawi et al., 2022; Mattys & Scharenborg, 2014). By contrast, 605 

previous research has demonstrated that neural indices of sentence-level context processing (e.g., 606 

the N400) were substantially attenuated when attention was diverted away from the language 607 

stimuli (e.g., Erlbeck et al., 2014; Hubbard & Federmeier, 2021). 608 

Locus of effects of bimodal divided attention on continuous speech processing 609 

At the behavioral level, increased dual-task load clearly impaired listeners’ performance on 610 

auditory comprehension questions for continuous speech, both in noise in the current study and 611 

in quiet in Xie et al. (2023). This raises the question of where, within the speech-processing 612

hierarchy, bimodal divided attention exerts its primary influence on behavior. Across both 613 

studies, dual-task load did not significantly modulate cortical tracking of acoustic features or 614 

lower-level linguistic representations at the sublexical or word-form level, with the only neural 615 

effects observed at the sentence level in the current study. Notably, these sentence-level effects 616 

emerged in relatively late neural responses, occurring after approximately 220 ms. Together, 617 

these findings indicate that behavioral declines under dual-task conditions predominantly reflect 618 

disruptions to later, post-perceptual stages of continuous speech processing (Xie et al., 2023). 619 

Moreover, the sentence-level effects observed in the present study under noise conditions 620 

suggest that, when background noise is present, reduced behavioral performance may partly stem 621 

from limitations in higher-order cognitive processes involved in probabilistic word-sequence 622 

processes, such as semantic integration. Finally, the interpretation of the dual-task effects— 623 

namely, that dual-task interference in continuous speech perception primarily affects higher-624 

order processing stages—is broadly consistent with the load theory of attention (Lavie, 2005; 625 

Lavie & Tsal, 1994). According to this framework, increases in cognitive load, such as those 626 

introduced by the visual working memory demands of our dual-task paradigm (also in Xie et al., 627 

2023), preferentially impact later stages of stimulus processing, including memory and 628 

behavioral output (Lavie, 2005; Murphy et al., 2016; however see for example, Sörqvist et al., 629 

2012). 630 

Selective versus divided attention in continuous speech processing 631 

Prior research on auditory selective attention in continuous speech perception has shown that 632 

neural tracking of linguistic representations at sublexical or sentence levels is robust for attended 633 

speech but largely absent for unattended speech (Brodbeck et al., 2018a; Broderick et al., 2018). 634 

In contrast, findings from the present study, together with those of Xie et al. (2023), indicate that 635

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted May 1, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.28.721419 doi: bioRxiv preprint

under bimodal divided attention, neural tracking at sublexical, word-form, and sentence levels is 636 

attenuated but not abolished. Notably, the effects of bimodal divided attention emerged relatively 637 

late, occurring after 200 ms. The effects of bimodal divided attention were even more limited 638 

when comparing low- and high-load dual-task conditions. 639 

As proposed by Xie et al. (2023), these differences in attentional modulation between 640 

selective and divided attention may reflect distinct underlying mechanisms (Johnson & Zatorre, 641 

2006; Loose et al., 2003). Selective attention costs are often attributed to “filter” mechanisms 642 

that prioritize task-relevant information while suppressing irrelevant inputs (Broadbent, 1958; 643 

Lachter et al., 2004). In contrast, the costs associated with divided attention are more likely to 644 

arise from limitations in executive control required to coordinate concurrent task demands, rather 645 

than from direct competition for shared sensory resources (Katus & Eimer, 2019; Loose et al., 646 

2003). Nonetheless, future research is needed to more fully characterize the mechanisms that 647 

differentiate continuous speech processing under selective versus divided attention. 648 

Implications for domain-general cognitive processes in linguistic prediction 649 

It remains an open question to what extent linguistic prediction during speech and language 650 

comprehension relies on domain-general cognitive processes (Ryskin et al., 2020; Ryskin & 651 

Nieuwland, 2023). The present findings, demonstrating divided attention effects on linguistic 652 

context processing in continuous speech perception, together with prior evidence from studies of 653 

selective attention, indicate that certain aspects of linguistic prediction may engage domain-654 

general cognitive mechanisms. This appears particularly likely for predictive processes operating 655 

over longer timescales (e.g., sentence-level context) and under acoustically challenging 656 

conditions, such as in the presence of background noise. However, it remains unclear whether 657 

these cognitive processes directly modulate linguistic prediction itself or instead influence 658

downstream measures that index predictive processing. Future research will be necessary to 659 

dissociate these possibilities and to more precisely characterize the role of domain-general 660 

cognitive processes in linguistic prediction during real-world speech comprehension. 661 

Study limitations 662 

The present study employed a fixed segment of multi-talker babble as the noise and presented at 663 

a single signal-noise ratio of 5 dB. Prior research has demonstrated that the cognitive demands 664 

associated with speech perception in noise vary with both noise types (e.g., Koelewijn et al., 665 

2012; Xie et al., 2015) and signal-to-noise ratio (e.g., Zekveld et al., 2011, 2026). Hence, it 666 

remains unclear whether the findings observed here extend to other types of background noise or 667 

to more negative or favorable signal-to-noise ratios. In addition, the extent of dual-task costs may 668 

depend on the specific characteristics of the tasks. For instance, dual-task interference may be 669 

reduced when an auditory task is paired with a visual-object task rather than a visual-spatial, 670 

such as the visuospatial n-back task used in the present study (Wahn & König, 2017). Future 671 

work should therefore examine whether the effects of bimodal divided attention on continuous 672 

speech processing vary across different combinations of auditory and visual tasks. 673 

## Conclusion 674 

This study suggests that bimodal divided attention may selectively disrupt cortical tracking of 675 

sentence-level linguistic representations, while leaving sublexical and word-form processing 676 

relatively unaffected during continuous speech perception in noise. Such impairments in higher-677 

level linguistic processing may, in turn, contribute to the observed reductions in behavioral 678 

speech comprehension during multitasking in noisy environments. 679
