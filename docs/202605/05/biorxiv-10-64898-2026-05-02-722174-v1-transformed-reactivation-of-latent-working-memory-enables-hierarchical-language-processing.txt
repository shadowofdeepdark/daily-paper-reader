Abstract 1

Human language comprehension requires tracking words across intervening material 2

to construct grammatical s tructures , yet two fundamental questions remain 3

unresolved: whether maintenance of earlier words relies on sustained neural activity 4

or activity -silent working memory mechanisms, and whether memory retrieval during 5

integration engages domain -general or lan guage -selective networks. Using 6

magnetoencephalography with time -resolved decoding, we tracked main -clause 7

subjects as participants heard sentences with embedded clauses (e.g. , "The dog, who 8

ch ases the cat, jumps over the mud. "). The subject representation ("dog") decayed to 9

baseline during the embedded clause but reactiv ated after the main -clause verb 10 

("jumps"), with transformed rather than reinstated neural codes. Critically, 11 

reactivation emerged first in right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex before engaging 12 

frontotemporal language regions, and reactivation strength was modulated b y13 

syntactic structure and predicted comprehension accuracy. These findings demonstrate 14 

activity -silent working memory maintenance, structure -dependent retrieval, and 15 

cooperative function of domain -general and language -selective networks during 16 

hierarchical language proce ssing. 17

Introduction 24 

The human capacity for language depends on the ability to construct hierarchical 25 

structures that link grammatically related elements across intervening material  1,2 .26 

Resolving such dependencies requires keeping earlier words in memory while 27 

processing subsequent material, and later retrieved at appropriate points to en able 28 

flexible integration  3,4 . However, how memory supports dynamic integration during 29 

sentence comprehension at the  neural  level remains largely unexplored  5,6 . Long -30 

distance dependencies, such as the subject -verb dependency between "the reporter "31 

and "admitted " in "The reporter, who the senator attacked, admitted the error. ",32 

provide a natural test case for investigating the neural implementation of working 33 

memory (WM) in sentence processing. 34 

Previous studies on hierarchical structure building during sentence processing 35 

have yielded important insights into the timing and localisation of syntact ic 36 

operations. Research in this area has identified event -related potential components, 37 

such as the Syntactic Positive Shift / P600, in response to syntactic violations  7–10 ,38 

neural oscillations associated with structural processing  11 –13 , and demonstrated that 39 

syntactic operations can be decoded from neural activity  14 –16 . Yet these approaches do 40 

not directly reveal how content -specific linguistic representations are maintained 41 

across embedded structure and subsequently retrieved for integration. 42 

How linguistic content is maintained in WM during sentence processing 43 

remains unresolved, but research  in visual and auditory WM  has identified two 44 

competing mechanisms  about how the brain stores information over time . Persistent -45 

activity theory proposes that WM representations are sustained th rough ongoing 46 

neural firing, maintaining information in an active state  17 –21 . In contrast, the activity -47 

silent theory suggests that information can be stored in short -term synaptic plasticity 48 

without elevated firing  to reduce interference  between multi ple items  22 –24 . These 49 

accounts make distinct predictions for hierarchical sentence processing. Persistent -50 

activity models predict sustained neural representation of ear lier linguistic elements 51 

throughout the embedded clause, whereas activity -silent theory predicts a loss of 52

Results 109 

Behavioural performance in sentence -picture matching task 110 

Participants (N=34,  20 females, aged 21.0 ± 3.0, mean ± SD) listened to English 111 

sentences and judge d whether a subsequent probe picture matched the sentence 112 

meaning (Fig. 1A). Each sentence consisted of a main clause with a subject -verb -113 

prepositional phrase structure, with either a subject -relative (SR) or object -relative 114 

(OR) clause embedded between th e main -clause subject and verb (e.g., SR: "The dog, 115 

who chases the cat, jumps over the mud "; OR: "The dog, who the cat chases, jumps 116 

over the mud "). The main -clause subject was drawn from a set of four animals (dog, 117 

cat, fox, goat), enabling multivariate d ecoding of subject identity across trials. The 118 

embedded -clause noun was selected from the remaining three animals, and the main -119 

clause verb was either "jumps over " or "steps into ", yielding 48 unique sentences. All 120 

the sentence constituents were counterbal anced across conditions (see full details in 121 

Methods and Supplementary Table 1). 122 

Participants showed high overall accuracy in the behavioural task (mean = 123 

92.6%, SD = 5.2%). A two -way repeated -measures ANOVA revealed main effects of 124 

both sentence -picture c ongruency and syntax on accuracy (Match > Mismatch: F(1,33) 125 

= 9.28, p = 0.005, ηp² = 0.22; SR > OR, F(1,33) = 22.4, p < 0.001, ηp² = 0.40) and 126 

reaction times (Match < Mismatch: F(1,33) = 14.1, p = 0.001, ηp² = 0.30; SR < OR, 127 

F(1,33) = 18.7, p < 0.001, ηp² = 0.36). Overall, congruent sentence –picture pairs 128 

produced higher accuracy and shorter RTs, and subject -relative sentences were 129 

processed more easily than object -relative sentences, consistent with previous 130 

psycholinguistic findings  45 –47 .131

Neuronal sources underlying WM encoding and reactivation 180 

We next examined which cortical regions were involved in the WM encoding and 181 

reactivation during sentence comprehension. To address this, single -trial MEG data 182 

were est imated in source space using minimum -norm estimation (MNE) constrained 183 

to the individual cortical surface. Then we applied a searchlight approach to identify 184 

the cortical regions that carried content -specific information (for details see Methods) .185 

Source -level searchlight analyses revealed that subject encoding recruited 186 

bilateral temporal cortex and inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), with peak decoding 187 

accuracy at approximately 220 ms after subject onset (Fig. 3B). During reactivation, 188 

content -specific represe ntations initially emerged in right dorsolateral prefrontal 189 

cortex (DLPFC), peaking at approximately 6 10 ms after verb onset (Fig. 3C, left 190 

panel), and subsequently engaged temporal cortex and IFG around 950 ms (Fig. 3C, 191 

right panel). This temporal progres sion suggests that verb -triggered WM retrieval is 192 

initiated in the prefrontal control network and subsequently propagates to IFG -193 

temporal regions for content representation and subject -verb integration. 194

Syntactic structure modulates WM reactivation 199 

Having established the spatiotemporal profile of subject reactivation during subject -200 

verb integration, we next examined whether this process is modulated by the syntactic 201 

structure of the embedded clause. MEG epochs were separated into subj ect -relative 202 

(SR) and object -relative (OR) conditions (e.g., SR: "The dog, who chases the cat, 203 

jumps over the mud. ", OR: "The dog, who the cat chases, jumps over the mud. "), and 204 

SVM decoding analyses were performed separately for each condition. 205 

In the SR condition, subject reactivation emerged 640 ms after verb onset and 206 

remained robust for approximately 780 ms (Fig. 4A, left panel: 0.64 –1.42 s, p = 4 207 

10 -5, cluster -based permutation test). In the OR condition, reactivation occurred later 208 

(~850 ms) and wa s shorter (~290 ms) in duration (Fig. 4A, right panel: 0.85 –1.14 s, p209 

= 0.002, cluster -based permutation test). When comparing the decoding accuracy 210 

averaged over the reactivation window identified previously (0.61 –1.36 s; see Fig. 211 

2B), we found significan tly stronger reactivation in SR than OR sentences (Fig. 4B; 212 

paired t -test, t(33) = 2.19, p = 0.036, Cohen's d = 0.38). 213 

Behavioural performance showed a similar pattern. A subset of trials was 214 

constructed as integration -mismatch trials, in which the probe p icture depicted the 215 

embedded -clause noun, rather than the main -clause subject, performing the main -216 

clause action, while all other aspects of the sentence remained unchanged. This 217 

manipulation selectively violated the correct subject –verb integration (see F ig. 4C for 218 

illustration). Accuracy on integration -mismatch trials was higher for SR than OR 219 

sentences  (Fig. 4C; paired t -test, t(33) = 3.21, p = 0.003, Cohen's d = 0.55), mirroring 220 

the neural reactivation effect. Together, these results indicate that the m agnitude and 221 

timing of verb -triggered subject reactivation are modulated by the syntactic structure 222 

of the embedded clause, with consequences for comprehension accuracy. 223

Discussion 226 

In this study, we used time -resolved MEG decoding t o track how the neural 227 

representation of the main -clause subject is maintained and reactivated across 228 

hierarchical ly embedded structure s (e.g., "The dog, who chases the cat, jumps over 229 

the mud. Three key findings emerged: (1) subject ("dog") representation s decayed to 230 

baseline across the embedded clause and reactivated at the main -clause verb, 231 

consistent with activity -silent maintenance; (2) encoding and reactivation patterns did 232 

not cross -generalise, indicating that retrieval reflects a transformed rather than 233 

reinstated representation; and (3) reactivation emerged first in right DLPFC before 234 

spreading to frontotemporal regions, suggesting domain -general networks contribute 235 

to core language functions during hierarchical sentence processing .236 

How WM supports language comprehension , particularly the processing of 237 

long -distance dependencies and syntactic integration, has long been a central question 238 

in psycholinguistics and cognitive neuroscience  34,35,49,50 . The neural mechanisms 239 

underlying this capacity provide the foundation for the recursive structure of human 240 

language and the generation of unlimited expressions, while also constraining the 241 

structural comp lexity that can be processed, such as the depth of hierarchical 242 

embedding  1,3,4 . Numerous behavioural studies have examined how increased WM 243 

load or interference affects lang uage processing difficulty  47,51 –53 , yet the underlying 244 

neural mechanisms remain poorl y understood. To advance our understanding of the 245 

neurobiology of language comprehension, we combined MEG with MVPA to 246 

investigate how linguistic information is maintained in WM during sentence 247 

processing. 248 

Our results revealed that decoding accuracy for th e main -clause subject returned 249 

to baseline during processing of embedded clauses and reactivated by the main -clause 250 

verb (Fig. 2) . This pattern is consistent with the activity -silent theory of WM, in 251 

which encoded representations do not persist through sus tained neural firing but 252 

instead transition into a latent state  24,48,54,55 . Converging evidence from brain 253 

stimulation studies and computational modelling further suggests that inform ation in 254

Critically, this mechanism of activity -silent storage enables more efficient storage of 257

where multiple constituents must be held in memory as new words are continuously 260

Although word representation became activity -silent during maintenance, it 262 

remained accessible in WM for later integration. The main -clause verb, which 263 

introduces semantic and syntactic constraints, served as a retrieval cue that triggered 264 

subject reactivation  50,62 –64 . This cue -driven reac tivation is consistent with previous 265 

demonstrations using task -relevant cues  65,66  and impulse perturbation  54,56,67 .266 

Critically, WM reactivation in sentence pro cessing was not determined simply by 267 

recency or memory trace strength. In subject -relative clauses (e.g., "The dog, who 268 

chases the cat, jumps over the mud."), the embedded -clause noun ("cat") was closer to 269 

the main -clause verb ("jumps") and thus constitute d a stronger recent competitor  52 ,270 

yet reactivation of the more distant main -clause subject ("dog") was stronger than in 271 

object -relative clauses (e.g., "The dog, who the cat chases, jumps over the mud.", Fig. 272 

4). This pattern demonstrated that memory retrieval was guided primarily by syntactic 273 

structure rather than by recency  68 .274 

Having established structure -dependent WM reactivation at the syntactic 275 

integration point , we next asked whether this reactivated representation preserves the 276 

same format as the original encoding. Temporal generalisation analysis showed that 277 

encoding and reactivation patterns did not cross -generalise (Fig. 3A), indicating that 278 

WM retrieval instead engaged a distinct representational state. One plausible 279 

interpretation is that encoding and retrieval privilege different aspects of the same 280 

linguistic item. D uring encoding, processing is dominated by word -form information 281 

such as phonological features, whereas during retrieval, processing may operate 282 

primarily on conceptual information that is relevant for integration with the verb  64,69 .283

Beyond the transformation dri ven by different cognitive demands at encoding 284 

and retrieval, the subject representation may be further reshaped by the intervening 285 

context during maintenance. The contrast between sentence structures directly 286 

supports this prediction. In subject -relative clauses, the main -clause subject retains its 287 

syntactic role throughout, whereas in object -relative clauses it is temporarily 288 

reassigned as the object of the embedded verb. Consistent with this structural 289 

difference , verbs following subject -relative clauses trigger ed stronger WM 290 

reactivation , a pattern paralleled by higher integration accuracy (Fig. 4). These 291 

findings further suggest that the function of WM in sentence comprehension is not 292 

merely to preserve prior representations, but to utilise them within a processing 293 

memory system that supports their transformation and retrieval during online 294 

integration  5,6 .295 

A central debate in the cognitive neuroscience of language concerns whether 296 

WM operations supporting sentence comprehension are implemented within 297 

language -selective regions or rely on domain -general WM networks. Prior work has 298 

argued that language WM is primarily located in a left -hemisphere frontotemporal 299 

language network (i.e. left inferior frontal gyrus and left superior /middle temporal 300 

gyrus), that is functionally distin ct from domain -general regions such as DLPFC 301 

> 31,33,35

. However, t he present findings argue against this dissociation . During long -302 

distance integration, content -specific information was decodable in DLPFC prior to 303 

its emergence in classical language regions. This finding result suggests that domain -304 

general WM network contribute directly to core linguistic computation as in non -305 

linguistic WM tasks  33,70 . The observed right laterali sation suggests that memory 306 

retrieval during sentence comprehensi on extends beyond the left -dominant language -307 

selective network , consistent with evidence that right hemisphere regions support 308 

executive functions essential for language processing  71 –74 .309 

The role of DLPFC in reactivation can be further understood in the cont ext of 310 

ongoing debates about its contribution to WM. While early work emphasised DLPFC 311 

as a storage site based on sustained activity during maintenance  18,19 , recent evidence 312

Our findings support a model in which WM for hierarchical sentence processing 327 

relies on activity -silent maintenance, structure -dependent retrieval, and coordi nated 328 

engagement of domain -general and language -selective networks. The transformation 329 

of word representations across hierarchical structure indicates that WM during 330 

language comprehension is not a passive storage buffer but an active system that 331 

continuou sly reshapes representation in accordance with evolving integration 332 

demands. These results raise several questions for future investigation. First, we 333 

employed controlled sentences structures with fixed embedded types. Future research 334 

using more naturalist ic materials could test whether similar maintenance and 335 

reactivation dynamics generalise across diverse syntactic constructions and 336 

embedding depths. Second, the transformation of representation s during reactivation 337 

bear resemblance to attention mechanisms in transformer -based language models, 338 

e.g., GPT -2 79,80 . Investigating the alignment between neural reactivation in human 339 

brain and attention mechanisms in language models could reveal shared or different 340 

principles underly ing biological and artificial language processing. Finally, individual 341

Auditory stimuli (sentences) 677 

In the experiment we constructed 48 unique sentences (see Supplementary Table 1, 678 

combining four animals ( "dog ", "cat ", "fox ", "goat ") as main -clause subjects with two 679 

main -cla use verb phrases ( "jumps over the mud ", "steps into the mud ")).  Besides, the 680 

set of natural sentences comprised subject -relative clauses (SR; "who chases ... ") and 681 

object -relative clauses (OR; "who … chases "), in which the relative clause intervened 682 

betwee n the main -clause subject and verb to form a long -distance dependency. The 683 

animal within the relative clauses was also chosen from the four options ( "dog ", "cat ",684 

"fox ", "goat ") but was never identical to the main -clause subject. This resulted in 48 685 

unique sentences in total (4 main -clause subjects × 2 main -clause verb phrases × 2 686 

relative -clause types × 3 relative -clause nouns). 687

Experimental procedure 721 

Participants were seated in a dimly lit magnetically shielded room under the MEG 722 

gantry (60° upright angle) and viewed stimuli on a rear -projection screen positioned 723 

100 cm from their eyes. Visual stimuli were presented by a PROPixx DLP LED 724 

projector (VPi xx Technologies, Canada), yielding approximately 41 pixels per visual 725 

degree. The experimental task was controlled using Psychtoolbox -3 running in 726 

MATLAB (MathWorks). Before the main experiment, participants completed a 727 

resting -state recording of at least 3 min while maintaining fixation. In addition, an 728 

empty -room recording of at least 3 min was acquired on the same day. 729 

Each trial started with a white fixation cross (1.0° of visual angle) at the centre 730 

of a black screen (RGB: [0, 0, 0]) , lasting for 1.0 –1.4 s. An English sentence with an 731 

embedded clause (e.g., "The dog, who chases the cat, jumps over the mud. ") was then 732 

presented auditorily while the fixation cross remained on the screen. Auditory stimuli 733 

were delivered via a MEGIN/Natus sound system usin g transducers and air -734 

conduction tubes. After sentence offset, the fixation cross was displayed for another 735 

1.0 –1.4 s. Subsequently, a cartoon illustration (31.0°×10.7° visual angle) was 736 

presented on a black background (Fig. 1A). Participants then indicate d whether the 737 

illustration matched the sentence by pressing a button (right index/middle finger). The 738 

experiment consisted of twelve blocks, with each block containing 48 sentences and 739 

taking approximately 6 min to finish. Following each block, participant s were given a 740 

rest period of at least  1 min.  In total, the MEG recording took about 1.5 h. Thirty -one 741 

participants completed all 12 blocks, one participant completed 10 blocks and two 742 

participants completed 8 blocks due to early dropout. 743

Source reconstruction 838 

Individual T1 -weighted structural MRIs were processed with FreeSurfer to 839 

reconstruct cortical surfaces and to compute a boundary element model (BEM) using 840 

the watershed algorithm  91 . MEG sensor locations were co -registered to each 841 

participant's MRI, and a cort ical constrained source space was defined on the 842 

reconstructed surfaces. Forward solutions were computed including all planar 843 

gradiometers and magnetometers. The noise covariance matrix was estimated from 30 844 

s of empty -room recordings acquired on the exper imental day, using a shrinkage 845 

estimator and subsequent regularisation for magnetometers and gradiometers (mag = 846 

0.2, grad = 0.2; rank based on the MEG info). This covariance matrix was used to 847 

spatially pre -whiten magnetometers and gradiometers prior to i nverse modelling, 848 

thereby accounting for differences in sensor scaling and noise levels. A minimum -849 

norm inverse operator was then constructed in MNE -Python, with source orientations 850 

constrained to be normal to the cortical surface. This inverse operator wa s applied to 851 

single epochs to obtain dynamic statistical parametric maps (dSPM source estimates) 852 

for each trial  92 . A surface -based source space co nsisting of 8196 vertices ( 4098 per 853 

hemisphere) was generated for each participant ( Fig. 1C ). 854
