Abstract 23 

Continuous speech evolves around vowels, the centerpieces of individual syllables. Vowels vary in 24 linguistic and acoustic salience: Linguistically, stressed syllables are more salient than unstressed 25 syllables: Stress patterns convey critical lexico-semantic and prosodic information, and their regularity 26 defines the speech meter. Acoustically, English vowel intensity cues lexical stress but also marks 27 salient syllables irrespective of stress status. Recent evidence demonstrates rapid neural analysis of 28 vowel intensity and identity during perception of continuous speech. Here, we probe how these 29 processes integrate lexical stress and metrical regularity. We recorded EEG while participants (n=26) 30 listened to children's stories with either an irregular, speech-like meter, or a regular poetic meter. 31 Stress and meter modulated cortical encoding of vowels throughout processing: Preparatory activity 32 preceded vowel onsets in an irregular meter only, and early sensory responses were enhanced for 33 unstressed vowels, suggesting additional resource allocation during processing of uncertain and less 34 discriminable speech sounds. In contrast, later processing (300-500ms) was stronger for stressed 35 syllables and in irregular meters, suggesting a combined effect of uncertainty and informational 36 content. Finally, responses were stronger for small intensity rises within metrically predicted stressed 37 vowels than in all other conditions. In the time-frequency domain, the spectral profile of neural phase-38 locking corresponded to spectral signatures of individual evoked responses, syllable and stress rates 39 in the stimuli. Overall, our findings reveal rapid neural integration of stress and metrical expectations 40 in neural processing of continuous speech. These dynamics may underlie the perceptual benefits of 41 metrically regular speech, such as poetry and song lyrics. 42 43 keywords: speech, lexical stress, prosody, EEG, speech perception, speech comprehension, envelope 44 tracking 45

Introduction 46 

Verbal communication hinges on the ability to extract meaning from a continuous and rapidly varying 47 speech signal. However, the information content of speech is not uniform in time. Rather, speech is 48 organized in syllabic units of varying informational content and acoustic salience. Lexically stressed 49 syllables are more informative than unstressed syllables, especially with regard to vowel identity 50 (Altman & Carter, 1989; McAllister, 1991). Furthermore, the alternation of stressed and unstressed 51 syllables defines the speech meter. In regular meters, this alternation is predictable. Such regularities 52 are not only aesthetically pleasing and form the basis for poetry, but also provide large-scale 53 contextual predictions of the stress status, and thus informational content, of individual vowels (Beier 54 & Ferreira, 2018). 55 56 Multiple lines of evidence show that lexical stress is an integral part of the speech signal. For instance, 57 lexical stress supports recognition of individual speech sounds (Slowiaczek, 1990; van Donselaar et al., 58 2005). To accommodate such effects, it has been suggested that stress and phonemes are rapidly 59 recognized in parallel and independently of each other during perception (Reinisch et al., 2010; Schild 60 et al., 2014). Metrical regularity was also found beneficial for further integration of speech content, 61 including syllable segmentation (Nazzi & Ramus, 2003) and lexico-semantic access (Rothermich et al., 62 2012; Zora et al., 2023). Moreover, deviations from metrical patterns are remembered better, 63 providing a prosodic means of emphasis (Kimball, 2018). A large body of electrophysiology studies 64 using M/EEG have found phase-locking between neural signals in auditory cortices and the speech 65 signal in the range of 1 -10 Hz (Boucher et al., 2019; Kolozsvári et al., 2021; Lizarazu et al., 2019; Peelle 66 et al., 2013). This frequency range covers the typical rate at which syllables and stress occur in speech. 67 It was thus hypothesized that this effect reflects neural tracking of syllables, with stronger neural 68 responses to stressed syllables (Gross et al., 2013). However, these analyses typically aggregate across 69 the entire speech signal. It is thus not clear whether phase-locking at syllable and stress rates is driven 70 by stressed or unstressed syllables. More generally, it is not known how this effect may be affected by 71 metrical predictability. 72 73 Acoustically, English lexical stress is indexed by the combination of multiple cues, realized 74 predominantly on the vowel nucleus, most importantly vowel intensity (Kandylaki et al., 2022). That 75 is, English stressed vowels are louder and longer than unstressed vowels (Fry, 1955, 1958; Lehiste, 76 1971; Van Santen, 1992). Critically, vowel intensity is not only overall higher for stressed syllables but 77 

also serves as a bottom-up acoustic cue to acoustic salience within each stress category. A reliable 78 acoustic correlate of vowel intensity are rising edges in the speech envelope (“peakRate” events). 79 Recent electrophysiology work revealed that dedicated neural populations in the human speech 80 cortex encode peakRate events and their magnitude, independently and in parallel to vowel onsets 81 (Oganian et al., 2023; Oganian & Chang, 2019). However, it is not known how these representations 82 are affected by the stress status of individual vowels. 83 84 Here, we aim to identify the neural timescales for the effects of acoustic salience, lexical stress, and 85 metrical predictability on neural responses to vowels in continuous speech. We capitalize on analytic 86 approaches that allow time-resolved modeling of the neural signal as a function of speech input 87 (Crosse et al., 2024; Holdgraf et al., 2017), disentangling between the effects of multiple concurrent 88 properties of the speech signal. We hypothesize that acoustic salience and lexical stress are recognized 89

Stimulus materials: Seuss stories 96 

The speech stimuli consisted of four children's stories in two different meter conditions varying in 97 predictability (Fig. 1a): metrically predictable (mp+) and metrically less predictable (mp-). The mp+ 98 

condition consisted of two Dr. Seuss (Scholastic Books) stories: "And to Think That I Saw It on Mulberry 99 Street" and "McElligot's Pool". Stimulus annotation 119 

Speech segmentation 120 

The audio recordings were time-aligned to their respective orthographic transcriptions using the 121 Montreal Forced Aligner (McAuliffe et al., 2017) (Version 3.9). Prior to alignment, all audio files were 122 downsampled to 16 kHz to match the acoustic model’s feature generation specifications. Alignment 123 employed the pretrained english_us_arpa acoustic model and its associated pronunciation dictionary. 124 For any out-of-vocabulary word, phonetic transcriptions were generated using MFA’s Grapheme-to-125 Phoneme (G2P) model. The alignment process relied on a Hidden Markov Model-Gaussian Mixture 126 Model (HMM-GMM) framework with speaker adaptation to improve boundary precision. Resulting 127 phonemes and word-level timestamps were exported as Praat TextGrids for subsequent acoustic 128 analysis (Boersma, 2001). To ensure temporal accuracy, all segment boundaries were manually 129 inspected and refined by two independent coders (inter-rater reliability, cohen’s kappa > 0.8). The 130 coders also inserted syllable -level boundaries where appropriate. All adjustments were based on 131 detailed visual examination of the waveform and wide -band spectrogram, ensuring consistent and 132 high -quality annotations. 133 134 

Stress 135 

Following boundary verification, the stress status of each syllable was manually annotated in Praat. A 136 three-tier coding structure was employed to maintain high data quality and inter-rater consistency: A 137 senior researcher established the annotation guidelines and performed gold-standard coding on a 138 training set to calibrate the other raters. Two additional trained coders each processed 50% of the 139 remaining dataset. To assess inter-rater reliability (IRR), a shared subset of 10% of the data was 140 annotated by all three coders. Agreement was calculated using Cohen’s Kappa (or interrater 141 reliability), with any discrepancies resolved through consensus discussions led by the master coder. 142 143 

peakRate events 144 

peakRates were computed following Oganian and Chang (2019), using publicly available code 145 (https://github.com/yoganian/peakRate). In brief, we extracted the broad amplitude envelope of the 146 speech signal, rectified it and low-pass filtered it at 10Hz, and calculated the derivative of this 147 envelope. For each syllable, when more than one peakRate occurred, only the one closest to voicing 148 onset was retained for analysis. We defined Inter-PeakRate intervals as the time from each peakRate 149 to the subsequent peakRate, regardless of the stress status of the following syllable. We expected 150 longer intervals in stressed syllables, reflecting longer stressed vowel durations. 151 152 

Sentence onset 153 

Sentence onset was manually annotated at the acoustic onset of each sentence. 154

Story 159 pairings (MP- and MP+) and order of presentation were counterbalanced across participants to control 160 for potential order effects. For each condition, peakRates were coded in two ways: 190 as a Binary predictor marking the occurrence of a peakRate event, and as a Magnitude predictor 191 indicating its strength. Finally, we included a Sentence Onset predictor to account 194 for large responses typically observed at the beginning of speech following silence. Results 250 

Acoustic analysis: peakRate is an acoustic cue to vowel onset and lexical stress 251 

We first aimed to characterize the relationship between acoustic edges in the speech envelope 252 (peakRate events) and syllabic stress. Based on prior literature (MacIntyre et al., 2022; Oganian & 253 Chang, 2019), we hypothesized that peakRate events would mark both stressed and unstressed 254 syllables, with larger peakRate magnitudes, a proxy of vowel intensity, and longer intervals to the 255 following peakRate in stressed syllables. Overall, we found that each story contained between 790 256 and 1000 peakRate events, marking ~90% of all syllables across stories. As expected, mean peakRate 257 magnitudes were higher for stressed syllables (Fig. 1b: mp+: mean = 0.084 (SD = 0.044), mp-: 0.083 258 (SD = 0.045)) than for unstressed syllables (mp+: mean = 0.065 (SD = 0.039), mp-: 0.067 (SD = 0.039)) 259 in both conditions (ME of stress: F(1, 3715) = 150.20, p < 0.001, ME of meter and interaction effect p 260 > .5). In contrast, inter-peakRate intervals were not affected by stress or meter (all main effects and 261 interactions p > .5, Figure 1c). This was likely because our child-directed stimulus materials contained 262 fewer reductions than spontaneous speech (Fitzroy & Breen, 2020). Overall, this analysis confirmed 263 peakRate as a reliable acoustic cue to syllable occurrence and lexical stress. The differences in 264 peakRate magnitude between stressed and unstressed syllables also indicate that any analysis of 265 stress effects must include peakRate magnitude as a covariate, to isolate categorical effects of stress 266 perception from continuous effects of vowel intensity, reflected by peakRate magnitude. 267 

TRF model fit: Independent tracking of vowels and peakRate events 268 

To isolate the independent evoked neural activity in response to vowels, peakRate events and 269 sentence onsets as a function of stress and meter, we fitted temporal response function (TRF) models 270 to the continuous speech data. Model prediction accuracies on a held-out test set were significantly 271 above 0 (mean model r = 0.0385, t(25) = 6.65, p<0.001). Crucially, the full model (mean r = 0.39, SEM 272 = 0.006) including all predictors outperformed a reduced model (mean r = 0.3, SEM = 0.004) that 273 contained predictors for sentence onsets only (t(25) = 2.19, p = .04), confirming ongoing neural 274 tracking of vowel onset and intensity. 275

TRF time courses 277 

TRF model weights revealed distinct timecourses of neural responses for sentence onsets, vowel 278 onsets, peakRate events, and peakRate magnitudes (Fig. 2a-d). All four were localized to a central 279 cluster, consistent with sources in bilateral auditory and speech cortices (Fig. 2a-d, right column). We 280 first tested these TRF time courses against zero, to identify time windows with significant neural 281 responses driven by each feature. Evoked responses to vowel onsets and peakRates showed an early 282 bimodal shape, with peaks around 50 and 150 ms (Fig 2b-c). This early bimodal double-peak response 283 was followed by a sustained negativity at 200 - 600 ms. This shape was distinctly different from the 284 dynamics of responses to sentence onsets, which showed the N100-P200 complex (Figure 2a) post-285 event. A comparison of TRFs for peakRate event onsets and peakRate magnitudes (Fig. 2d) showed 286 that the amplitude of all three key TRF responses (early bimodal and late sustained) scaled up with 287 larger peakRate magnitudes, i.e., responses were stronger for acoustically more salient syllables. Discussion 404 

We isolate the effects of acoustic salience (i.e., magnitude of acoustic edges), linguistic salience (i.e., 405 lexical stress) and the regularity of stress patterns (i.e., meter) on neural responses to continuous, 406 storybook-like speech. We hypothesized that stress may be detected early on in a syllable with direct 407 effects on processing of vowels and acoustic edges. In line with our expectations, temporal receptive 408 field (TRF) analyses show effects of both lexical stress and meter throughout neural responses to 409 vowels, including preparatory activity prior to vowel onset, early sensory responses (0-150ms), and 410 late markers of integrational processes (300-600ms). Furthermore, we find that neural responses to 411 low magnitude acoustic edges are enhanced within stressed vowels, even more so within a regular 412 meter. Time-frequency analyses show that evoked responses to acoustic edges in the envelope are 413 temporally more precise within unstressed than within stressed syllables, unlike previously assumed 414 (Gross et al., 2013) . In sum, our results demonstrate rapid integration of acoustic salience and lexical 415 stress and speak in favor of parallel processing of acoustic, segmental and metrical information during 416 speech perception. 417 418 Linguistic and psycholinguistic literature have debated the relative timing and relation between 419 identification of individual speech sounds and lexical stress information. Behavioral (Cutler & Foss, 420 1977) and eye tracking evidence (Reinisch et al., 2010) points towards early recognition of stress 421 concurrently with segmental information. However, electrophysiological evidence for this is missing, 422 not least due to methodological issues associated with modeling of neural responses driven by 423 complex speech acoustics. Here, we find that lexical stress affects neural responses to vowels within 424 100 ms post vowel onset. Critically, we find stronger early auditory evoked activity for unstressed than 425 for stressed vowels. While this finding seemingly contradicts the dominant view that stressed vowels 426 should drive stronger neural responses, it cannot be explained by acoustic differences between 427 stressed and unstressed vowels, as those are accounted for in the TRF model. Rather, we propose that 428 it might reflect additional effort in sensory encoding - possibly because recognition of vowel identity 429 is harder for unstressed vowels (Calhoun, 2010). Alternatively, a reduced response to stressed vowels 430 may be due to their higher predictability from preceding speech context. For instance, it has been 431 found that the N100 auditory evoked component is reduced in regular and predictable contexts and 432 enhanced for unexpected sounds (Schwartze et al., 2013). 433 434 Early integration of vowel and stress information is further supported by preparatory pre-vowel 435 activity in metrically less predictable stories (MP-). Multiplex facilitatory effects of predictive 436 processing have been observed across sensory domains, including language processing (Ryskin & 437 Nieuwland, 2023). In speech, the benefits of prediction and expectation rely both on preparatory 438 activity ahead of a stimulus (Goldstein et al., 2025), as well as ensuring ease of sensory processing 439 (Ahissar et al., 2001) and later semantic integration (Frank et al., 2015). Here, we find that 440 suprasegmental (stress) uncertainty can drive preparatory activity for individual speech segments, 441

This shows that anticipatory processes are not confined to segmental and lexico-semantic 443 processing but are pervasive across levels of speech analysis and provides evidence for rapid online 444 integration between phonemic and prosodic structure during comprehension. 445 446 In addition to integration across linguistic levels, our findings support early integration of acoustic and 447 linguistic and information. Scaling of neural responses to acoustic edges (peakRate events) with their 448 magnitude was equivalent across stress and meter conditions in the first 100 ms. However, this scaling 449 disappeared for stressed vowels in metrically predictable contexts. Rather, responses were strong 450 even for edges of small magnitude. Neural responses to peakRate events are the primary source of 451 speech envelope tracking (Oganian et al., 2023). The extent to which envelope tracking is a purely 452 bottom-up auditory process or is subject to top-down modulation, such as intelligibility, has been 453 subject to extensive research (Peelle et al., 2013). Our findings show that envelope tracking is 454 supported by suprasegmental linguistic representations, enhancing tracking of smaller modulations in 455 the envelope when they are expected to occur. 456 457 Early sensory responses to both vowels and peakRate events were followed by a late negativity around 458 300-500 ms after event onset, with topography and latency resembling the N400 component for 459 semantically less expected words (Rabovsky et al., 2018). For vowels, this late negativity was stronger 460 in metrically less predictable and stressed syllables. Numerically, however, both effects seem to reflect 461 that this negativity was absent for unstressed vowels in metrically predictable contexts. Arguably, 462 predictable unstressed vowels are easiest to identify as less informative and may thus be assigned less 463 processing effort, similar to findings of an enhanced N400-like component to metrically unexpected 464 words (Magne et al., 2007, 2016). While this past work was on violations of metric expectations, our 465 stimuli did not contain a violation. Rather, they showed varying levels of metrical predictability. This 466 speaks for continuous and graded predictions of upcoming stress patterns, akin to semantic 467 expectations (Lau et al., 2008). Interestingly, for peakRates, this late effect was modulated by 468 peakRate magnitude for both unstressed and stressed vowels, but at different latencies. Namely, it 469 occurred later in unstressed than in stressed vowels, suggesting delayed integrative activity for 470 unstressed but acoustically salient syllables. This speaks for a continuing role of acoustic salience even 471 at later stages of syllable processing. 472 473 Complementary to our findings in the time domain, analyses of neural phase locking speak for 474 increased temporal precision in neural tracking of peakRate events within unstressed vowels. It is 475 known that evoked responses to peakRate events in the speech envelope directly map onto neural 476 phase locking, with stronger phase locking to acoustically more salient events (Oganian et al., 2023). 477 While TRF weights reflect the average response amplitude and temporal dynamics, neural phase-478 locking is a measure of temporal coherence in neural response shape and latency across trials. Here, 479 we tested how stress and meter affect the temporal coherence of neural phase-locking to peakRate 480 events. In our data, the phase-locked frequencies closely correspond to temporal modulation 481 frequencies present in speech signal acoustics, lending additional support to an evoked response 482 account of neural phase-locking to speech. Specifically, spectral peaks in phase-locking reflect the 483 shape of the evoked response (6-8Hz), the syllable rate (~4 Hz) and the stress rate (~2 Hz) in our speech 484 stimulus. In the metrically less predictable stories, phase-locking at 6-8 Hz, and thus temporal precision 485 of evoked responses, was higher for stressed than for unstressed syllables. We propose that this reflects a shift in resource allocation: A predictable 488 meter eases the processing of stressed syllables, allowing for more precise processing of unstressed 489 syllables. In contrast, without metrical regularity the focus remains on the more informative stressed 490 syllables. At the syllable rate, phase locking was evident in all conditions, except for stressed syllables 491 in metrically predictable stories (where it, however, did appear at the stress rate). While further 492 studies are necessary to assess the factors that determine this complex relationship, it does show that 493 neural phase-locking depends not only on acoustic salience, but also on the interaction between 494 linguistic salience and the predictability of the speech signal. 495 496 Ample evidence has shown that metrical regularity has beneficial effects on speech processing 497 including acoustic (Moon et al., 2020), vowel (Pearson et al., 2021), and semantic information 498 (Rothermich et al., 2012). Behaviorally, beneficial effects of metrical regularity have been documented 499 for infant-directed materials and for adults (Cox et al., 2022; Cutler, A., 1991; Lenc et al., 2023; Pérez-500 Navarro et al., 2022) . The present findings extend this literature by providing insight into the neural 501 mechanisms underlying this facilitation both in early sensory processing and in later integration. 502 Future research could examine whether atypical neural processing of metrical predictability 503 contributes to observed behavioral deficits in language processing, such as those associated with 504 developmental language disorders (Lense et al., 2021) and aging, and further explore its potential role 505 in supporting language acquisition across development. 506 507 

Conclusion 508 

We provide evidence for concurrent and rapid neural analysis and integration of acoustic, segmental 509 (vowel) and suprasegmental (stress) patterns in continuous speech. We find that the current speech 510 input is interpreted in context of suprasegmental metrical regularities and offer a possible neural basis 511 for the beneficial effects of metrical regularities in speech processing. 512 513
