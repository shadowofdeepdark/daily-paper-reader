# Abstract 31 

Human speech is inherently social. Yet our understanding of the neural substrates 32 

underlying continuous speech perception relies largely on neural responses to 33 

monologues, leaving substantial uncertainty about how social interactions shape the 34 

neural encoding of speech. Here, we bridge this gap by study ing how EEG responses 35 

to speech change when the input includes a social element. In Experiment 1 , we 36 

compared the neural encoding of synthesised undirected monologues, directed 37 

monologues, and dialogues . In Experiment 2, we extended this by using podcast s,38 

addressing the additional challenges of real speech dialogue, such as dysfluenc y.39 

Using temporal response function analyses, we show that the presence of a social 40 

component strengthens the cortical tracking of the speech envelope , despite identical 41 

acoustic properties . Neural responses to synthesised speech showed a strong 42 

correlation with those for real speech podcasts, with a stronger alignment emerging 43 

for more socially -relevant speech material. In addition, we demonstrate that robust 44 

neural indices of sound and lexical -level processing can be derived using real podcast 45 

recordings despite the presence of dysfluencies . Finally, we present a simulat ion to 46 

put to the test the robustness of temporal response function analys es under increasing 47 

levels of dysfluency . Together, these findings highlighting the impact of social 48 

element s in shaping auditory neural processing , providing a framework for future 49 

investigation and analysis of social speech listening and speech interaction .50 

# Significance Statement 51 

Human speech is rarely produced or processed in a social vacuum. Yet, our 52 

understanding of continuous speech neurophysiology mostly comes from experiments 53 

involving speech monologue s. This study reveals how social context modulates the 54 

neural encoding of speech. We directly contrast neural signals recorded when 55 

participants listened to monologues and dialogues , using controlled material from 56 

speech synthesis and real podcast recordings. We found that the social element 57 

amplifies the neural encoding of speech features , reflecting greater engagement. We 58 

also show strong correlation between synthetic and real podcast neural responses, 59 

scaling with social relevance . Finally, we demonstrate that lexical processing can be 60 

measured robustly even amid natural dysfluencies. These insights advance our 61 

understanding of speech neurophysiology, informing future research on social speech. 62

# Introduction 63 

To understand speech , our brains must rap idly process sounds into linguistic meaning 64 

(Friederici, 2011 ; Poeppel et al., 2012 ). Th e neural basis of this phenomenon has been 65 

studied extensively using controlled paradigms involving targeted manipulations 66 

(Carreiras et al., 2005 ; Kutas & Federmeier, 2011 ; Osterhout & Holcomb, 1993 ), and 67 

more recently with listening tasks involving more realistic continuous streams of 68 

speech (Di Liberto & Ip, 2025 ; Di Liberto et al., 2021 ; Gwilliams et al., 2023 ). That work 69 

utilised a variety of neural recording technologies , including electro - and magneto -70 

encephalography (EEG and MEG ) (Défossez et al., 2023 ; Destoky et al., 2019 ),71 

functional magnetic resonance imaging and functional near -infrared spectroscopy 72 

(Butler et al., 2020 ; Chen et al., 2020 ; Formisano et al., 2008 ), and intracranial 73 

electrical recordings (e.g., electrocorticography and stereotactic EEG ) (Bernabei et al., 74 

2021 ; Hamilton et al., 2021 ; Mesgarani et al., 2014 ). Overall, this research has led to 75 

a new understanding of the sound -to -meaning transformation, providing detailed 76 

insights into the spatial and temporal dynamics that unfold throughout that process 77 

(Kocagoncu et al., 2017 ; Uusvuori et al., 2008 ). However, the necessary experimental 78 

constraints have led to listening tasks centred around audiobook -style , pre -recorded 79 

speech material , while ignor ing one of the key functions of speech: the social element. 80 

Recent research has explor ed speech interaction, for example by measuring brain -to -81 

brain synchrony using simultaneous neural recordings from interacting individuals 82 

(Kinreich et al., 2017 ; Schwartz et al., 2024 ). However, th os e interactive experiments 83 

are far removed from previous speech listening research , as other factors at play (e.g., 84 

social anxiety, familiarity, turn -taking) mak e it difficult to relate findings across the two 85 

domains . To achieve this and advance the study of social speech neurophysiology ,86 

we require a mechanistic understanding of how specific social factors impact the 87 

neural processing of speech , which requires careful experimental control . The present 88 

study takes a step in that direction, by extending the conventional audiobook -style 89 

listening paradigm to stimuli with progressively higher social relevance . In doing so, 90 

we investigate how challenges of real speech (e.g., dysfluencies ) impact our ability to 91 

measure the neural processing of speech and language (Di Liberto & Ip, 2025 ;92 

Mousavi et al., 2024 ).93

We present results from two EEG experiments . The first experiment compares the 94 

neural encoding of speech while participants listen ed to pre -recorded monologues and 95 

dialogues (Experiment 1 ). Dialogue typically involves turn -taking, mutual feedback, 96 

and shared communicative goal s (Garrod & Pickering, 2004 ), while monologue s lack 97 

immediate (auditory) reciprocity . Nevertheless , monologues can also include a social 98 

dimension by being directed to a listener , as opposed to undirected monologues , when 99 

they are not aimed at any particular listener . Using the temporal response function 100 

analysis (TRF), this study tests if social relevance impact s the neural processing of 101 

speech during listening . This leads to our first hypothesis: 102 

Hypothesis 1 : Social relevance enhances the cortical encoding of speech, 103 

leading to stronger enc oding for directed than undirected monologues .104 

Synthesised speech was used to control for acoustic and lexical differences across 105 

conditions, ensuring that differences in the EEG would be due to social relevance as 106 

opposed to other factors such as prosody, word rate, or volume level (see Methods ).107 

To generalise the results from Experiment 1 to the case of real human speech, we108 

also conducted a second experiment using real dialogue recordings from popular 109 

podcasts (Experiment 2 ). The material included speech from different registers to 110 

capture a broader range of speech types, increasing generalisability . Furthermore, real 111 

spontaneous includes dysfluency (Shriberg, 2001 ), which can challenge natural 112 

speech comprehension , for example by hampering syntactic parsing (Ferreira & 113 

Bailey, 2004 ), contribut ing to processing delays that impact word predictability (Corley 114 

et al., 2007 ). Here, we investigate the possibility of studying speech material including 115 

dysfluencies, testing if robust EEG measurements of speech processing can be 116 

derived at varying levels of dysfluency , with the following hypotheses: 117 

118 

Hypothesis 2 : The social element of speech leads to a consistent neural 119 

signature, which generalises across synthesised and real speech dialogue. 120 

Hypothesis 3: Neural measurements of speech encoding are robust to 121 

dysfluency in the speech material .122

Stimulus features 252 

The present study examines the neural encoding of speech and language features 253 

during speech listening . The same features and analyses were used across the two 254 

experiments .255 

Sound envelope and derivative . The sound envelope was calculated as the 256 

instantaneous magnitude of the Hilbert transform of the sound -pressure waveform. 257 

The speech envelope captures the slow amplitude modulations of the speech signal, 258 

which have been shown to be robustly reflected in the EEG signal (Crosse et al., 259 

2016a ; Ding & Simon, 2012 ; Lalor & Foxe, 2010 ). The envelope -related features 260 

assessed in this study comprised the sound envelope and half -way rectified envelope 261 

derivative (calculated from the envelope at the original sampling rate with the MATLAB 262 

diff function , and by replacing negative values with zero ). Matching the processed EEG 263 

signal, t he se acoustic features were then down sampled to 64 Hz .264 

Word onsets . The forced alignment software DARLA (Reddy & Stanford, 2015 ) was 265 

used to produce a word -by -word time -align ment between each speech segment and 266 

its transcript. The onset time of all words in the speech stimuli was then coded into 267 

sparse vectors of zeros and ones with the same length and sampling rate as the other 268 

features , where ones represent the onset time of a word , consistent with previous 269 

studies (Broderick et al., 2018 ; Chalehchaleh et al., 2025 ; Di Liberto et al., 2021 ).270

Temporal response function analysis 299 

A system identification approach was employed to determine how specific stimulus 300 

features map onto EEG activity. This approach, known as the Temporal Response 301 

Function (TRF) (Ding & Simon, 2012 ; Edmund C. Lalor, 2009 ), utili ses regulari sed 302 

ridge linear regression (Crosse et al., 2016) to estimate a filter , or model, that best 303 

captures how the brain converts input stimulus features into the resulting neural 304

responses. One of the two metric s derived from the TRF is the weights of the model 305 

over time. The brain's response to a speech stimulus is not instantaneous; rather, it 306 

unfolds over time when speech is heard. Therefore, the brain's electrical activity is 307 

affected not just at the moment the sound occurs, but also over a subsequent time 308 

period. This delayed and extended influence is captured in the time lags of the TRF 309 

weights between the stimulus and the EEG response. These time lags can be 310 

analysed both spatially (across the scalp) and temporally (in terms of response 311 

delays). 312 

As is typical (Crosse et al., 2016b ; Crosse et al., 2021 ), t o evaluate the model's ability 313 

to generali se and avoid overfitting, we used leave -one -out cross -validation across 314 

trials . In this process, TRF models were trained on all but one trial and then used to 315 

predict the EEG signal for the left -out trial. The TRF model performance is then 316 

assessed by computing Pearson’s correlation between the predicted EEG signal and 317 

the actual EEG signal on the left -out fold at each scalp electrode . This constitutes the 318 

second metric derived from the TRF: the prediction correlation, or the EEG variance 319 

explained by a specific set of speech feature(s) . The EEG variance captured by 320 

speech features is interpreted as how well the model was able to map the encoding of 321 

speech features in the brain signals. Fitting the model with one speech feature results 322 

in a univariate model, while fitting the model while more than one speech feature 323 

results in a multivariate model. 324

Statistical analyses 353 

Parallel statistical procedures were applied to the datasets from the two experiments. 354 

Statistical analyses were performed on EEG prediction -correlation values obtained 355 

from the TRF models. For each participant and condition, prediction correlations were 356 

averaged across all EEG electrodes to obtain subject -level values, and these values 357 

were used in all statistical tests. To determine whether the TRF models captured 358 

meaningful neural tracking of the speech features, prediction correlations were tested 359 

against zero using one -sample t-tests. 360 

To compare experimental conditions, repeated -measures statistical tests were used 361 

because all conditions were recorded from the same participants. When three levels 362 

of social relevance (undirected monologue, directed monologue, and dialogue) were 363 

compared, a one -way repeated -measures ANOVA was applied to the subject -level 364 

averages. When the omnibus test was significant, pairwise comparisons between 365 

conditions were performed using Wilcoxon signed -rank tests. 366

# Discussion 629 

Speech communication is an ideal testbed for investigating human cognition, as it 630 

involves a multitude of processes involved in the transformation of sound to meaning .631 

Such processes encompass mechanisms such as statistical prediction , attention and 632 

cognitive effort . The possibility of extending that work to social speech opens up 633 

opportunities for probing a whole range of processes that are typically excluded from 634 

monologue listening research, such as social role and speaker familiarity, 635 

communication accommodation, social anxiety, and many others (Di Liberto & Ip, 636 

2025 ). However, this opportunity comes with the important challenge of having to 637 

isolate the phenomena of interests from a much richer and harder -to -control set of 638 

behaviours and neural processes. One key for disentangling those processes and 639 

phenomena is to pinpoint how exactly these socially -relevant factors impact the spatial 640 

and temporal patterns reflecting the neural processing of speech and language . Here, 641 

we take a first step in that direction by means of a principled step -by -step approach ,642 

determining the impact of including a social element in speech listening tasks 643 

(excluding interaction in our scope of investigation) .644 

The results from Experiment 1 indicate that social relevance substantially enhances 645 

the neural tracking of speech . Importantly, this effect cannot be explained by 646 

differences in low -level acoustic or speech -structure properties across stimulus 647 

categories. To assess this possibility, we quantified several stimulus characteristics, 648 

including duration, root -mean -square (RMS) amplitude, mean fundamental frequency 649 

(F0), F0 variability, and word rate. No systematic differences were observed between 650 

conditions, nor between monologue and dialogue stimuli. These findings indicate that 651 

the observed neural effects are unlikely to be driven by basic acoustic or speech -rate 652 

differences and instead reflect modulation by socially relevant aspects of the stimuli. 653 

The increase d encoding emerged specifically for the speech envelope, but not for 654 

word -level processing (EEG prediction correlation metric; Fig ure 1A ). Previous work 655 

on selective attention has also shown similar increases in envelope tracking. 656

Interestingly, however, the increased speech tracking in selective attention tasks also 657 

corresponded with changes in the TRF patterns, especially with a modulation of the 658 

TRF -P2 component (Carta et al., 2024 ; Power et al., 2012 ; Vanthornhout et al., 2018 ),659 

while the increased envelope tracking here does not correspond to changes in the 660 

temporal or spatial dynamics of the TRF ( Fig ure 1D ). As such, our results are 661 

compatible with a general increased engagement with the task and stimulus , but with 662 

important differences from the widely studied selective attention phenomenon . It 663 

should be noted that we also detected sensitivity to the social relevance of speech in 664 

the beta band , specifically for word onset encoding. This result supports the potential 665 

and relevance of measuring cortical tracking at higher EEG rates (even though 666 

stronger speech tracking typically emerges in the delta and theta bands). While we did 667 

not have specific hypotheses pertaining to the EEG beta band in this study, we note 668 

that studying speech tracking at different frequency rates may provide an additional 669 

dimension for more clearly disentangl ing the cortical tracking of distinct speech 670 

features , and should be considered (without forgetting about the risks that come with 671 

filtering operations) (De Cheveigné & Nelken, 2019 ).672 

While Experiment 1 isolated the impact of social relevance on speech tracking , our 673 

design in Experiment 2 takes us a step further by measuring neural dynamics in more 674 

naturalistic settings, wherein spontaneous speech introduces additional complexities .675 

The results in Experiment 2 (Figure 2) demonstrated temporal response 676 

characterisations that are not only consistent with previous monologue -listening 677 

literature (Broderick et al., 2018 ; Di Liberto et al., 2021 ; Heilbron et al., 2022 ), but also 678 

with the neural speech encoding results obtained in Experiment 1 ( Figure 3 ). Our 679 

results indicated similar spatial and temporal patterns for synthesised and real speech 680 

recordings; the EEG encoding of speech features , including the speech envelope and 681 

word onset , was highly correlated across synthesised and real speech record ings ,682 

achieving correlations of up to 0.92 for word onset TRFs . This is quite remarkable, 683 

considering that the two experiments involved completely different speech material 684 

and distinct participant cohorts. The fact that we observed a consistent increase in that 685 

similarity with social relevance is also a strong indication that the impact of social 686 

relevance on the spatial and temporal patterns of speech is consistent across material 687 

and participants. Further work is needed to better characterise and test the boundaries 688 

of consistency in neural tracking of speech features across synthesised and real 689

From this analysis, two key conclusions emerge. First, dysfluencies do not pose a 703 

problem up to a certain threshold, which may eliminate the need for manual transcript 704 

cleaning when using LLMs. Second, it is essential to quantify and report the dysfluency 705 

profile - both type and frequency - used in a given experiment, particularly when 706 

generating new stimuli. This task is relatively straightforward in listening experiments, 707 

where all participants are exposed to identical stimuli. In contrast, speech interaction 708 

paradigms, such as hyperscanning studies, introduce greater methodological 709 

complexity. A central challenge is that individual participants often exhibit unique 710 

dysfluency profiles (Befi -Lopes et al., 2014 ; Pistono et al., 2024 ), which can 711 

differentially affect measures of lexical expectations - both in terms of LLM -based 712 

estimation and neural encoding (Goldstein et al., 2022 ). This variability reinforces the 713 

need for systematic dysfluency measurement and individuali sed reporting to ensure 714 

robust and interpretable findings. Finally, it is worth noting that the observed sensitivity 715 

of LLMs to dysfluency opens new avenues for research focused on its impact on the 716 

neural encoding of speech. Future investigations could specifically examine this effect 717 

in participant cohorts with speech production challenges, such as individuals who 718 

stutter or those experiencing social anxiety. Such studies would not only deepen our 719 

understanding of speech processing under naturalistic conditions but also offer 720 

insights into the neural mechanisms underlying communicative difficulties. 721
