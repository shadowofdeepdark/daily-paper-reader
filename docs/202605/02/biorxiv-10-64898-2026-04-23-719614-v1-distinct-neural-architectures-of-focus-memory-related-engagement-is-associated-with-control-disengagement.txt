Abstract 1Prevailing models propose that sustained focus is a product of top-down control that amplifies 2task-relevant representations while suppressing distraction. We challenge this view, 3demonstrating that the neural signatures of mental focus differ across cognitive modes. In two 4neuroimaging studies, we used a paradigm that creates conflict between external task goals and 5self-generated thought, to identify the neural correlates of task focus in contexts varying in 6memory engagement. In Study 1, arithmetic and comprehension tasks were associated with 7distinct neural architectures of task focus: arithmetic-related focus recruited visual–motor regions, 8while comprehension-related focus was characterized by the recruitment of memory systems and 9reduced activation of control regions. Study 2 extended these findings, with both externally 10 directed comprehension and internal memory retrieval associated with a shared neural profile of 11 reduced control engagement, despite reliance on distinct memory systems. Furthermore, focus 12 covaried with activity in heteromodal semantic regions across both reading and listening, 13 suggesting semantic engagement is a primary marker of being on task during comprehension. 14 Our findings indicate that sustained focus during memory-guided cognition is characterized by 15 mutual disengagement between control and memory networks. This discovery motivates a 16 departure from traditional executive-control accounts, since task focus in meaningful contexts is 17 associated with the recruitment of representational structures in long-term memory rather than 18 active top-down supervision. 19 

Significance Statement 20 Does concentration always require effort? While traditional models suggest that sustained focus 21 is a product of top-down executive supervision, we show that the brain employs fundamentally 22 different architectures for focus depending on the cognitive mode. In two neuroimaging studies, 23 focus was associated with distinct neural profiles – reduced engagement of memory systems in 24 arithmetic, and control deactivation alongside the activation of distinct memory systems in 25 comprehension and autobiographical recall. Heteromodal semantic regions were associated with 26 focus during reading and listening, consistent with an association between semantic network 27 engagement and task focus during comprehension. These findings indicate that sustained 28 attention occurs across multiple neural configurations linked to different cognitive modes, 29 challenging the view that top-down supervision is a universal requirement. 30 31

Introduction 34 35 Task focus is conventionally viewed as a product of deliberate, top-down control (1-3). Yet, the 36 brain can sustain remarkable focus during immersive memory recall with minimal apparent effort, 37 challenging the assumption that task focus always depends on executive regulation. Prior work 38 demonstrates that task engagement sharpens neural responses in relevant cortical regions and 39 suppresses competing information by modulating the connectivity between control, sensory, and 40 default mode networks (DMN; e.g., 4-6). However, this account cannot explain forms of sustained 41 cognition that unfold independently of control networks. Here, we test whether the brain can 42 maintain focus through the self-organizing dynamics of memory systems, without continuous 43 executive oversight. 44 45 A classic executive-failure hypothesis proposes that mind-wandering during externally oriented 46 tasks such as reading reflects a breakdown in top-down control. When frontal–parietal control 47 systems fail to prioritize task-relevant information, there is insufficient suppression of internally 48 generated content within the DMN (7, 8). The fronto-parietal control network (FPCN) maintains 49 goal-relevant representations and typically responds more strongly when demands increase (9-50 11). Through its subregions, FPCN aligns functionally with either DMN or the dorsal attention 51

3network, thereby supporting internal and external modes of cognition (12-17). This perspective 52 predicts that higher focus should coincide with stronger control engagement. 53 54 The DMN, conversely, is classically assumed to deactivate in attention-demanding contexts (18, 55 19), and to activate during unconstrained, self-generated thought (20, 21). Yet a growing 56 literature shows that DMN activity can accompany goal-directed cognition when memory retrieval 57 or semantic comprehension is required (22, 23). Some cognitive states might also be sustained 58 via memory-based systems: dorsomedial and core DMN subsystems are recruited during both 59 text comprehension and autobiographical recall respectively, with stronger core DMN activation 60 observed when participants are more focused on memory recall than comprehension (23, 24). 61 These patterns indicate that semantic engagement might be a common mechanism supporting 62 focus across internally and externally oriented cognition (25), and that memory networks are 63 positively associated with task focus in some cognitive modes. 64 65 This perspective motivates a re-evaluation of the link between difficulty, control, and focus. 66 Classical theories posit that harder tasks, which demand more attention, elicit greater control 67 engagement and less mind-wandering (8, 20, 26). However, studies of reading comprehension 68 reveal the opposite: people report more off-task thought when texts are difficult (27-30). Thus, 69 sustained focus on comprehension can be achieved in the absence of control when texts are 70 easy to follow, motivating the hypothesis that semantic engagement may be key to cognitive 71 focus during reading or listening, rather than attentional mechanisms that prioritize relevant 72 sensory inputs. 73 74 To test these ideas, we conducted two functional MRI studies examining how large-scale brain 75 networks support sustained focus across internal and external cognitive modes. In Study 1, 76 participants performed externally oriented tasks -- arithmetic or sentence comprehension -- with 77 each trial preceded by autobiographical memory cues, creating competition between task goals 78 and self-generated thought. Trial-by-trial focus ratings allowed us to model how fluctuations in 79 attention shape neural activity and network coupling. Study 2 extended and replicated these 80 effects by comparing sentence comprehension and autobiographical memory retrieval tasks, 81 presented either alone or in competition, across spoken and written modalities. This design 82 enabled us to test whether similar mechanisms are associated with focus across semantic and 83 memory-based states, and whether modality influences these processes. Across tasks, we define 84 focus as the stability of task-relevant mental representations. While traditional models equate 85 focus with top-down supervision, we propose that focus within semantic contexts reflects 86 successful immersion in meaning-based representations. 87 88 Across analyses, we observed distinct neural pathways associated with focus depending on 89 cognitive context. In Study 1, focus in arithmetic involved reduced engagement of memory 90 regions, whereas comprehension-related focus involved deactivation of control regions. Study 2 91 showed that both comprehension and autobiographical memory retrieval were associated with 92 greater reductions in control engagement at higher levels of focus, with comprehension 93 additionally associated with reduced activity in core DMN regions. Heteromodal semantic regions 94 were associated with higher focus across reading and listening tasks, indicating that 95 representational rather than sensory systems covary with sustained comprehension. Conjunction 96 analyses across studies 1 and 2 confirmed consistent semantic activation and control 97 deactivation during focused comprehension. Finally, bidirectional connectivity analyses revealed 98 mutual decoupling between control and semantic regions during focused comprehension, 99 consistent with a pattern in which memory-based systems coincide with more stable cognitive 100 states in the absence of executive engagement. Together, these findings challenge traditional 101 executive-control accounts, indicating that in some cognitive modes, sustained thought reflects 102 the engagement of memory architectures rather than top-down supervision. 103

Study 1: Differential focus effects during semantic and non-semantic tasks 107 

108 Study 1 examined how task focus is sustained across semantic and non-semantic contexts to test 109 the hypothesis that these distinct modes of cognition are sustained in different ways. In a within-110 subjects design (N=34), participants performed comprehension (semantic) and arithmetic (non-111 semantic) tasks that varied in difficulty (Easy vs. Hard; see Materials and Methods). Prior to 112 scanning, participants generated personal thoughts linked to cue phrases (e.g., “Lottery win”) to 113 induce internally oriented, memory-based distraction. These cues were designed to trigger 114 autobiographical retrieval during fMRI while participants either read factual sentences or solved 115 arithmetic problems (serial addition). After each trial, participants rated their task focus on a 1–7 116 scale (1 = thinking about other things; attention fully diverted from task, 7 = thoughts fully on task; 117 Fig. 1A). 118 119 

Behavioral results: A repeated-measures ANOVA examining task focus ratings explored the 120 effects of task (comprehension/arithmetic) and difficulty (easy/hard). This revealed main effects of 121 task ( F(1, 33) = 54.08, p < .001, η2 = .62), difficulty ( F(1, 33) = 27.47, p < .001, η2 = .45), and a 122 task-by-difficulty interaction ( F(1, 33) = 58.60, p < .001, η2 = .64) (Fig. 1B). Post-hoc Bonferroni-123 corrected t-tests showed that participants were more focused on easy than hard comprehension 124 (t(33) = 3.348, Bonferroni-corrected p = .002), and more focused on hard than easy arithmetic 125 (t(33) = −7.01, Bonferroni-corrected p < .001). 126 127 This behavioral dissociation suggests that the mechanisms sustaining task focus differ across 128 domains. In the arithmetic task, participants achieve higher focus during harder trials by actively 129 suppressing mind-wandering to meet task demands. In contrast, comprehension relies on 130 engagement of the semantic system: focus is higher in easier texts, where visual-semantic 131 processing more effectively stabilizes attention on the material. 132 133

Parametric effects of task focus in comprehension and arithmetic: To identify neural 150 responses associated with subjective fluctuations in focus, we implemented a univariate general 151 linear model (GLM; see Materials and Methods). Task difficulty was modelled categorically, while 152 trial-by-trial ratings of task focus were entered as a parametric regressor across all trials. This 153 approach isolated neural activity that covaried with moment-to-moment variation in focus, 154 independent of mean activation differences between easy and hard conditions (see below; Fig. 155 2). Effects of difficulty in comprehension and arithmetic: Next, we examined whether the neural 224 effects of task focus overlapped with those of difficulty within each domain. In the arithmetic task, 225 regions of motor cortex associated with increased focus also showed greater activation for 226 difficult relative to easy trials, while regions linked to lower focus (characterized by more off-task 227 thought) overlapped with DMN areas that were more active during easy trials (Fig. 2D). This 228 alignment suggests that focus and difficulty in the arithmetic task depend on shared mechanisms: 229 sustaining attention requires engagement of visual–motor systems and suppression of default 230 mode activity, both of which intensify as task demands increase. In contrast, regions associated 231 with increased focus in comprehension overlapped with those more active during easy than hard 232 trials in posterior temporal cortex (Fig. 2C). This implies that sustained focus in reading is not a 233 function of increased effort and might instead reflect successful engagement of temporal lobe 234 semantic regions. 235 236

Conjunctions of focus effects across Study 1 and Study 2 336 

337 Conjunction analyses examined whether the patterns of activation and deactivation during 338 focused comprehension overlapped across datasets. We also asked whether focused 339 comprehension and recall recruited regions that were disengaged by task focus in non-semantic 340 tasks, such as arithmetic. Conjunction analyses used a z-statistic threshold of 2.6 and a FWE 341 threshold of p < 0.05, in line with previous studies (33, 34). Results are shown in Fig. 4. 342 343 The conjunction of focused comprehension across Studies 1 and 2 (Fig. 4A) revealed common 344 areas of increased activation in left vOT and MTG/STG, and deactivation in bilateral anterior and 345 posterior cingulate, paracingulate gyrus, precuneus, frontal pole, MFG and right SFG, AG/SMG. 346 When participants were more focused on comprehension, there was a reliable pattern of 347 increased activation in lateral semantic regions, and decreased activation in the core DMN and 348 executive regions. 349 350 A conjunction of negative focus during arithmetic in Study 1 and positive focus in comprehension 351 (across modalities in Study 2; Fig. 4B) revealed overlap in left anterior temporal lobe and right 352 precentral/postcentral gyrus and supplementary motor cortex. We compared this conjunction map 353 with a meta-analytic map from Neurosynth (32) for the term ‘semantic’ (1031 contributing 354 studies), which revealed overlap in left ATL (Fig. 4G). Therefore, semantic regions are engaged 355 during focused comprehension, and are suppressed during arithmetic, which does not require 356 access to the semantic store. 357 358 The conjunction of negative focus during arithmetic in Study 1 and positive focus during recall in 359 Study 2 (Fig. 4C) further revealed bilateral precuneus, right precentral/postcentral gyrus, SPL, 360 and supplementary motor cortex, and left parahippocampal gyrus/occipitotemporal regions. The 361 left parahippocampal gyrus, occipitotemporal cortex and precuneus overlapped with the 362 Neurosynth meta-analytic map for ‘episodic’ (488 contributing studies). These results indicate that 363 regions associated with episodic memory are engaged during focused recall and suppressed 364 when participants focus on arithmetic. 365 366

Effects of focus on functional connectivity 391 

392 Using conjunctions that highlighted the distinct roles of control and semantic memory systems in 393 task focus as seeds, we performed psychophysiological interactions (PPI) across the whole brain 394 to investigate variation in connectivity as a function of focus across tasks. We seeded control, 395 salience and default mode network regions that deactivated during more focused comprehension 396 across studies (blue regions in Fig. 4D). We also seeded memory-related regions in ATL (Fig. 397 4G), which overlapped with the meta-analytic maps for ‘semantic’; activation in this site increased 398 with focus during comprehension and decreased for arithmetic. We constructed separate PPI 399 models for these two seeds and examined the parametric effect of task focus on connectivity in 400 Studies 1 and 2 separately. The PPI models included all the regressors in the basic GLM model, 401 a PPI term for each focus effect, as well as the time series of the seed (for details, See Materials 402 and Methods), using the generalized psychophysiological interaction (gPPI) approach (35). The 403 regressors were not orthogonalized. The two datasets revealed similar patterns, and we focus on 404 effects observed reliably across both studies below (see SI Appendix Fig. S5 for all PPI results). 405 406 The first seed, showing deactivation in focused comprehension in medial and frontal regions (Fig. 407 4D), showed greater functional segregation with bilateral MTG/ITG, and left lateral frontal cortex 408 (IFG, precentral gyrus, MFG) when comprehension focus was higher (see Fig. 4E). The overlap 409 (Fig. 4F) of these PPI results with the conjunction map for focus effects in comprehension (brain 410 map in Fig. 4A) demonstrates that control and core DMN areas become increasingly decoupled 411 from regions supporting focused comprehension, suggesting this pattern of disengagement 412 occurs when semantic systems maintain attention with minimal interference. 413 414 The second seed, in left ATL, showed greater activation when participants reported higher focus 415 during comprehension (Fig. 4G). As focus increased, the ATL became more functionally 416 segregated from areas in bilateral MFG, frontal pole, precuneus, right anterior 417 cingulate/paracingulate gyrus, SFG, and SMG across Studies 1 and 2 (Fig. 4H). Masking the 418 conjunction focus map with this PPI highlighted the same restricted set of regions largely in 419 control networks (Fig. 4I), which showed deactivation during focused comprehension. These 420 results suggest that focus is associated with functional segregation of language and semantic 421 regions from control and core DMN networks. Together with the first seed, these results 422 demonstrate bidirectional disengagement between semantic and control regions during focused 423 comprehension, regardless of which network is seeded. 424 425 

Discussion 426 427 Our findings across two independent studies challenge accounts in which sustained focus is 428 primarily a product of executive control. Instead, the neural correlates of task focus were found to 429 vary across cognitive modes, with meaning-based contexts associated with reduced control-430 network activity and greater functional segregation between control and memory systems. 431 Participants performed externally oriented tasks (comprehension or arithmetic) preceded by 432 autobiographical cues, creating competition between task goals and self-generated thought, and 433 rated their focus after each trial. In Study 1, focus during arithmetic was associated with visual– 434 motor regions, while focused comprehension was associated with greater engagement of 435 semantic regions and reduced activity in control and salience networks. In Study 2, 436 comprehension and autobiographical memory showed convergent patterns: focused cognition 437 coincided with engagement of memory systems and reduced control involvement. In both studies, 438 control and memory systems showed reciprocal separation when participants were more focused. 439

13 440 Functional separation of control and memory systems may be associated with a semantic “flow” 441 state that is relatively independent of executive control. In this view, coherent thought may be 442 sustained through intrinsic dynamics within memory systems. Rather than requiring continuous 443 external regulation of goal-relevant representations by control networks, these systems may 444 contain structural constraints—such as semantic associations—that allow cognition to proceed 445 along well-established pathways (36-38). Thus, being “on task” during comprehension may reflect 446 a handover from executive control to self-organizing memory systems, with thought sustained by 447 the internal structure of the text’s meaning. 448 449 Executive networks are typically engaged during demanding tasks, amplifying relevant 450 information and suppressing distraction (9, 11, 39-41). Executive-failure accounts propose that 451 lapses of attention arise when such control is insufficient, allowing internally generated thought to 452 intrude (7, 8). Yet stronger control recruitment does not always predict better performance: more 453 erratic, error-prone (“out-of-the-zone”) behavior can be associated with greater control activation 454 (42). Our findings refine this perspective. During arithmetic, higher focus was associated with 455 visual–motor regions, whereas during semantic comprehension and memory retrieval it coincided 456 with reduced engagement of control and salience networks, indicating distinct neural routes to 457 focused cognition. 458 459 Behavioral studies indicate that the link between executive control and mind-wandering is more 460 nuanced than simple executive-failure accounts suggest. In non-semantic tasks, mind-wandering 461 typically decreases as difficulty increases, consistent with greater control demands (20, 43). In 462 contrast, in semantic tasks such as reading, greater difficulty can increase mind-wandering (30, 463 44, 45), potentially because disruption to semantic prediction and integration promotes internally 464 generated thought. Under such conditions, semantic memory may decouple from sensory input, 465 allowing internally driven associations to dominate over text-related processing. These 466 dissociations suggest that the mechanisms supporting sustained attention differ across cognitive 467 modes, depending on whether cognition is control-driven or representation-driven. 468 469 Our neural data help to explain these behavioral patterns and show they cannot be reduced to 470 task difficulty alone. Focused comprehension was associated with reduced engagement of 471 control and salience networks, together with weaker functional coupling between these regions 472 and lateral temporal cortex. If this pattern merely reflected lower executive demands, similar 473 effects would be expected across tasks. Instead, arithmetic showed a partially opposing profile, 474 with higher focus linked to greater engagement of visual–motor systems and stronger 475 deactivation of memory-related regions. Within comprehension, parametric analyses further 476 isolated trial-by-trial variation in focus independent of easy and hard task conditions, indicating 477 that these neural signatures tracked attentional state rather than difficulty per se. Across studies, 478 comprehension and autobiographical retrieval converged in linking higher focus to reduced 479 control involvement despite differing task goals and stimulus contexts. Together, these findings 480 suggest that sustained attention depends on cognitive mode: in some contexts, focus is linked to 481 increased goal-directed processing, whereas in memory-guided cognition it coincides with 482 reduced executive involvement and greater reliance on representational systems. This pattern 483 points to a third attentional mode—neither passive mind-wandering nor effortful control—in which 484 memory representations sustain coherent thought through a flow-like state of control 485 disengagement. 486 487 Sustained comprehension across both reading and listening engaged the same heteromodal 488 semantic and language regions in superior and middle temporal gyri. This modality-invariance 489 indicates that task focus depends on representational coherence in long-term memory rather than 490 attentional mechanisms tied to a specific sensory channel. Anterior STG was implicated in 491 sustained focus in comprehension across modalities and in off-task thought during arithmetic, 492 suggesting that this region underpins both task-relevant semantic retrieval and mind-wandering 493

The 501 dorsomedial DMN, linked to semantic cognition (49-52), was more engaged during focused 502 comprehension across modalities, consistent with its role in conceptual coherence (36-38). In 503 contrast, the medial temporal and core DMN subsystems were more engaged during focused 504 autobiographical memory retrieval: the MT subsystem is implicated in episodic memory and 505 scene construction (34, 53, 54), while core DMN activates during autobiographical memory (23) 506 and self-relevant cognition (21, 55). Despite these differences, control-network suppression 507 overlapped across conditions: dorsomedial prefrontal cortex showed deactivation with higher 508 focus during reading, listening, and autobiographical retrieval, irrespective of distracting stimuli. 509 This convergence suggests that although distinct DMN subsystems support different forms of 510 memory retrieval, focused memory-based cognition consistently involves reduced executive 511 involvement. 512 513 Several limitations should be noted. The present studies focused on arithmetic, comprehension, 514 and autobiographical retrieval, and broader sampling of cognitive domains will be needed to 515 determine how widely these modes generalize. Because fMRI provides correlational measures of 516 neural activity, the observed patterns of network engagement and decoupling do not by 517 themselves establish causal mechanisms (56, 57). In addition, self-reported focus likely reflects 518 multiple processes, including motivation, effort, and subjective absorption (58-60). Future work 519 combining causal perturbation methods and temporally resolved imaging could clarify how control 520 and memory systems interact across tasks. 521 522 The ability to maintain coherent, goal-directed thought without continuous executive supervision 523 may be highly adaptive. Executive control is cognitively demanding and likely metabolically costly 524 (61, 62); by leveraging the self-organizing properties of long-term memory, the brain may sustain 525 focus on meaningful information such as language or autobiographical memories more efficiently. 526 This perspective suggests that when past experience supports cognition, focus may often involve 527 fluent processing within representational systems, with top-down control serving primarily as 528 intermittent guidance or error correction rather than a continuous driver of thought. 529

Task Design and procedure. 556 

557 Both Studies 1 and 2 were adapted from ref. 23. In Study 1, we employed a within-subjects 2 × 2 558 design manipulating task (Comprehension vs. Arithmetic) and difficulty (Easy vs. Hard). 559 Participants generated thoughts linked to cue phrases (e.g., “Lottery win”) outside the scanner. 560 Inside the scanner, participants retrieved personal thoughts from cue phrases for 3 s, and then 561 either read factual sentences or added up numbers for 9 s (see Fig. 1A for task illustration). After 562 each comprehension or arithmetic trial, participants provided ratings on a scale from 1 (i.e., 563 thinking about other things) to 7 (i.e., focusing on the meaning of the sentence or the ongoing 564 addition) within 2 s, which measured the extent to which participants were focused on the current 565 task. 566 567 Study 2 took place across two days. On day 1, participants generated personal memories linked 568 to cue words (e.g., gift) outside the scanner. On day 2, inside the scanner, participants brought to 569 mind their detailed personal memories related to a written and spoken autobiographical memory 570 cue word for 2 s. Following the task instruction (i.e., COMPREHEND or RECALL) presented for 1 571 s, participants were asked to either comprehend the sentences (presented visually or auditorily), 572 or recall their memories for the cue word for an average of 9 s. The experiment consisted of the 573 following conditions: (a) visual sentences; (b) auditory sentences; (c) retrieve personal memories 574 while ignoring visual sentences; (d) retrieve personal memories while ignoring auditory 575 sentences; (e) retrieve personal memories (no distracting sentence presented; see Fig. 1C for 576 task illustration). After each trial, task focus ratings on a scale of 1 (i.e., not at all focused) to 4 577 (i.e., highly focused) were collected within 2 s to index the extent to which participants were able 578 to focus on each task condition. For more information, refer to SI Appendix, Supplemental 579 Methods. 580 581
