Abstract 18 

Although humans excel at speech recognition, recognition accuracy can vary widely due 19 

to differences in background environments as well as the speaker’s voice quality, intonation, and 20 

pitch. Predicting when speech recognition will succeed or fail, however, remains an ongoing 21 

challenge in hearing research. Here we characterize recognition abilities across a wide range of 22 

natural conditions using digits spoken by many male and female talkers of multiple ages with 33 23 

unique backgrounds. Across this diverse set of sounds, speech recognition is most strongly 24 

influenced by the spectrum and modulation statistics of the noise. Yet, articulatory features of the 25 

speech, including fundamental and formant frequencies, show categorically distinct modulatory 26 

effects on accuracy across age, gender, and words. We then show that a low-dimensional model of 27 

sound, based on computations in the auditory midbrain, accounts for participants’ single-trial 28 

recognition behavior across voices, words and backgrounds. Thus, speech-in-noise perception 29 

across extremely diverse natural conditions depends largely on a simple set of spectrotemporal 30 

statistics likely encoded by central neural populations. 31

Introduction 32 

Listening in the everyday world is seldom a simple task, since background sounds 33 

frequently distort or mask sounds of interest 1. Yet, the human auditory system is surprisingly 34 

robust to noise, allowing us to hear one another in a noisy restaurant filled with many voices or 35 

while walking down a city street with a cacophony of traffic and construction sounds. Recognizing 36 

speech in noise is a major challenge for individuals with hearing impairment, language processing 37 

differences, and even machine systems. Thus, understanding what speech and background factors 38 

impact the acuity, robustness, and efficiency of human hearing under natural distortions is of 39 

critical scientific and clinical interest. Recognition abilities vary based on the relative intensity of 40 

the foreground to the background, but even at a fixed signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), recognition 41 

accuracy varies widely across different backgrounds 2,3 . The details of the speech – the specific 42 

talker, phonemes, their quality, intonation, and pitch – impact speech recognition as well 4-7 , and 43 

these effects are likely background-dependent. Accurately accounting for the moment-by-moment 44 

contributions of the background, foreground, and interaction between them is an ongoing 45 

challenge for understanding the perception of speech in natural listening environments. Here, using 46 

a combination of human perceptual experiments and computational modeling, we aim to 47 

characterize foreground effects on speech recognition and foreground-background interactions on 48 

the timescale of single words. 49 

Many previous studies have examined the specific sound features that impact speech 50 

perception, such as the fundamental frequency, consonants, phoneme-level features and other 51 

talker characteristics 8-12 . Energetic masking, where the spectrum of the noise overlaps with or 52 

exceeds the energy of the competing speech, is a critical factor influencing speech perception 13 .53 

Since many environmental sounds are rich with transient acoustic events and are nonstationary, 54 

times of decreased power can allow speech to stand out, in a phenomenon known as glimpsing 55 14,15 . Informational factors, such as spectrotemporal modulations that underlie word identity and 56 

contextual cues that influence word uncertainty 13,16,17 , along with spatial cues 18-21 all also 57 

influence speech perception in noise. One limitation of many previous speech-in-noise studies is 58 

that they have not focused on the interactions between speech and natural background features and 59 

often have not focused on single-trial predictions. Here we aim to address these limitations by 60 

using highly diverse natural backgrounds and speech from multiple talkers to characterize the 61 

mechanisms underlying speech-in-noise perception. 62 

Theories of auditory processing have proposed that humans rely on statistical sound 63 

information for perception and that statistics related to the spectral and modulation profiles of 64 

speech and stationary natural sounds can explain many aspects of human perception 2,22,23 . Mid-65 

level regions of the auditory pathway, such as the inferior colliculus, are specifically sensitive to 66 

both the spectral and modulation statistics of natural sounds 24-27 and neural activity in these 67 

subcortical regions can well describe both the identity of individual sound stimuli and categorical 68 

information about the sound type 28-30 . Additionally, models based on spectrotemporal sound 69

Here we evaluate the hypothesis that spectrotemporal modulations in natural background 72 

sounds interfere with the representation of speech articulatory features in noise. Using both 73 

psychophysical measurements and biologically plausible modelling techniques, we find that the 74 

ability to recognize speech varies widely depending on the foreground word, the speaker’s voice, 75 

and the background statistics. Some backgrounds give rise to consistent impairment in speech 76 

recognition, regardless of the foreground, while in some cases the background and foreground 77 

interact, and both determine listeners’ speech recognition accuracy. These interactions are linked 78 

to specific speech articulatory features, including fundamental and formant frequencies, which 79 

cluster across words as well as the talker’s age and gender. However, we find that the detailed 80 

behavioral effects can be directly explained on a sound-by-sound basis across words, talkers, and 81 

backgrounds by a model of central auditory representations of the foreground and background 82 

modulation statistics. 83

Results 84 

To determine how voice features and background statistics together impact speech 85 

recognition, we used a block randomized design and tested digit recognition abilities of n=10 86 

participants using foreground English speech produced by many distinct speakers (n=326) mixed 87 

with many natural and perturbed backgrounds (9,900 mixtures; see Methods). 88 

For a specific background, we mix foregrounds from different speakers producing the same 89 

word (Fig. 1A left) and the same speaker producing different words (Fig. 1A, right). By comparing 90 

across these trials, we can quantify how features of a speaker’s voice impact recognition in noise 91 

and how features of the word impact recognition in noise. By comparing multiple backgrounds 92 

where the speaker (Fig. 1B left) or word (Fig 1B, right) is fixed, we can also determine to what 93 

extent foreground features interact with the spectral and temporal statistics of these different 94 

backgrounds. Participants were tasked with recognizing three digits (Fig. 1C) within natural 95 

background sounds (Fig. 1D, Original, OR) and manipulated variants which isolate the spectrum 96 

(Fig. 1D, Spectrum Matched, SM) or the modulations (Fig. 1D, Modulation Matched, MM). 97 

Sounds were block randomized across backgrounds and their variants and balanced across 4 98 

different speaker populations (Adult Male, AM, n=111; Child Male, CM, n=50; Adult Female, AF, 99 

n=114; Child Female CF, n=51) (Fig. 1E). Altogether, participants’ recognition accuracy was 100 

measured across a wide range of natural conditions, allowing us to characterize and model the 101 

effects of different background statistics and foreground articulatory features. 102 

Accuracy variation is associated with foreground articulatory features 103 

Participants listened to sounds over headphones (Fig. 2A) and were instructed to identify 104 

the digits in the sound mixture via keyboard entry (e.g., 7-9-2). In previous work, we found that 105 

digit recognition accuracy varied extensively across backgrounds depending on their spectrum and 106 

modulation statistics 2. Here we additionally find that accuracy varies based on the foreground 107 

speaker group (Fig. 2B1-3) and across digits (Fig. 2C1-3). Comparing the OR backgrounds, 108 

accuracy for babble-8 and factory backgrounds was the lowest, while accuracy for fire and white 109 

noise was the highest among the 11 backgrounds (see Methods). Additionally, by comparing 110 

accuracy, across OR, SM, and MM conditions, we can attribute performance to spectral or 111 

modulation statistics of the background. Across backgrounds, the voice group effects are 112 

influenced by the background spectrum and modulation cues, however they do not appear to be 113 

purely the result of one or the other. For babble-8 backgrounds, children’s speech is more 114 

recognizable than adult speech both for the OR background (15±2%, 36±3%, , AM, AF vs 59±2%, 115 

63±3%, , CM, CF; mean±sem) and when the background modulations are whitened (SM, Fig. 116 

2B2) (72±2%, 74±2%, , AM, AF vs 86±2%, , 85±1%, , CM, CF; mean±sem). This suggests that 117 

the background spectrum alone is sufficient to cause voice group effects. At the same time, even 118 

when the spectra are matched across backgrounds (MM, Fig. 2B3) the voice group effects still 119 

differ across backgrounds (15±2%, 34±3%, , AM, AF vs 52±2%, , 44±3%, , CM, CF; mean±sem), 120

Subband modulations preserve articulatory feature representations 224 

Next, we test whether spectrotemporal summary statistics can account for speech 225 

articulatory features among a wide range of male and female talkers of multiple ages. Here, we 226 

use a biologically motivated, hierarchical model of the auditory midbrain containing 227 

spectrotemporal receptive fields that allow for measuring the modulation statistics for each spoken 228 

digit (Fig. 5A, See Methods). The model output statistic, the midbrain modulation power spectrum 229 

(mMPS), accounts for the sound’s power distribution in three acoustic dimensions: frequency, 230 

temporal and spectral modulation. To visualize how well articulatory features are preserved in this 231 

statistic, we applied t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE) to the mMPS to represent 232 

each digit in a low dimensional 2D space. 233 

In the embeddings, we find that different digits and voice groups are well differentiated 234 

(Fig 5B, left) and there is substantial variability in the clustering across digits. For instance, the 235

Subband modulation masking explains variability in recognition accuracy across words and voices 243 

To determine whether the mMPS can account for both foreground- and background-driven 244 

changes in recognition accuracy, we next apply a previously developed, generalized perceptual 245 

regression (GPR) approach 2 (Fig. 6A; see Methods). Here the background and foreground mMPS 246 

(Fig. 6A, left) and their interaction (point-wise products) are dimensionality reduced using 247 

principal components analysis (PCA). The resulting neural model embeddings (Fig. 6A, middle), 248 

here with 15 principal components each, are then used as input features to a logistic regression 249 

model that predicts whether the participant identifies the digit correctly (see Methods). 250 

We evaluated cross-validated predictions for individual participants’ recognition accuracy 251 

across a digit projection (DP) where the data and predictions are averaged across speakers and a 252 

voice projection (VP) where the data and predictions are averaged across digits (Fig 6B, shown 253 

for one participant). GPR accurately replicates the overall patterns in recognition accuracy across 254 

different voice groups, spoken digits, and backgrounds. For instance, in the case of the original 255 

sounds (Fig 6B, top left), the GPR scores replicate the pattern of low accuracy for babble-8, factory, 256 

city and water for most digits, as well as trends across different voices and backgrounds where 257 

AM/AF have a lower accuracy than CM/CF for babble-8, factory, city, and water backgrounds. 258 

The model also captures accuracy differences in SM (Fig 6B, middle) and MM (Fig 6B, bottom) 259 

backgrounds, which are driven by changes in the background spectrum or modulation content. 260 

Together, these predictions suggest that spectrotemporal modulations, and the interaction between 261 

foreground and background mMPS features, are sufficient to reconstruct human perceptual 262 

performance in the task. For this individual participant, the GPR explains 73% of the perceptual 263 

variance across digits (Fig. 6 C1 and C2, white dots) and 78% of the perceptual variance across 264 

voice groups (Fig. 6 D1 and D2, white dots). 265 

Across participants, the variance explained by the model for the digit projection ranged 266 

between 66.0-73.2% (69.4 ± 0.8%, mean ±sem; Fig. 6C2), with the digit two having the lowest 267 

explained variance for the digit projection (62.5±0.1%; mean±sem) and eight having the highest 268 

prediction quality (75.4±0.1%). For the voice projection the model explained 75.6 – 83.3% of the 269 

variance across participants (80.2 ± 0.9%, mean ±sem , Fig. 6D2), with the lowest prediction 270 

quality for CF voices (73.6±0.1%) and the highest for AM voices (85.8±0.1%). 271

We also examine generalization across participants by testing whether a population average 330 

model can account for perceptual sensitivity from held-out participants. Here we find that the 331 

individual participant models are significantly better than the population average model 332 

predictions (VP: t(9) = 7.4, p < 10 -4 ; DP: t(9) = 3.78, p < 10 -2 ; AUC t(9) = 10.18, p < 10 -5 ). However, 333 

the individualized models provide relatively small improvements in prediction quality (Fig. 8B, 334

suggesting that recognition patterns are largely consistent across this sample of typically hearing 336 

participants. 337 

Finally, we tested whether glimpsing 14,15 contributed to the perceptual trends and model 338 

predictions. By design, the background mMPS used in the full model is computed for the sound 339 

segment directly overlapping each digit, so that it accounts for momentary changes in the 340 

background sound power and has the potential to account for glimpsing effects. Here, rather than 341 

using these time-localized mMPS, we consider a time-averaged mMPS for the background as input 342 

to the GPR model. This “static” version of the model does not account for momentary changes in 343 

background power and, thus, it cannot account for glimpsing effects. Here we find that the average 344 

prediction quality for the voice and digit projections and single trials of the static model is 345 

comparable to that of the full glimpsing model (Fig. 8C; VP: 82.8 ±0.8% vs. 80.2 ±0.9%, DP: 346 

70.7 ±0.8% vs. 69.4 ±0.8%, AUC: 76.6 ±1.0 vs. 74.7 ±0.4). However, notable differences in the 347 

prediction quality can be observed for the predictions within individual backgrounds (Fig. 8D). 348 

Glimpsing effects, where there is a difference between the original and static models, appear to be 349

Discussion 356 

The findings demonstrate that the spectrum and modulation statistics of speech interact 357 

with those of a competing background sound to influence word recognition accuracy. Beyond the 358 

interference effects driven by the background sound statistics 2, word-level and speaker-dependent 359 

articulatory features, including the voice fundamental frequency and formants, have a more 360 

nuanced influence on accuracy. These articulatory features vary systematically between words and 361 

speaker groups, and we demonstrate that low-dimensional auditory model embeddings capture 362 

word and voice related information. More broadly, a low-dimensional regression model that uses 363 

auditory modulation statistics produces accurate behavioral predictions across 9,900 sounds 364 

encompassing many talkers, backgrounds, and words on a trial-by-trial basis. Altogether, these 365 

results demonstrate how using modulation statistics in a generalized perceptual regression (GPR) 366 

model framework can provide a biologically interpretable account of speech recognition behavior 367 

in highly variable and realistic listening scenarios. 368 

How humans solve the cocktail party problem 1 remains an important challenge in auditory 369 

neuroscience, since everyday hearing invariably occurs in cluttered auditory scenes and such 370 

scenarios severely impair abilities for those with hearing loss 33,34 . Prior studies have focused 371 

mostly on perception of controlled speech stimuli and simplified backgrounds that lack diverse 372 

statistical structure of real-world backgrounds 22,35,36 . Although these studies have highlighted how 373 

specific foreground or background features can impact speech recognition, limited stimulus 374 

variability may make it difficult to identify the broader representations that underlie hearing any 375 

speech in any noise. 376 

Our findings here suggest that spectrum-based interference, or energetic masking 16 , alone 377 

is insufficient to fully explain voice, word, and background related effects and that spectrotemporal 378 

modulations may allow a generalizable explanation for speech perception in its full variability. Of 379 

all the factors tested, the background sound has the strongest impact on accuracy, yet word-level 380 

and speaker-level statistics interact with the background to modulate perceptual accuracy. 381 

Additionally, although speech articulatory features, such as the fundamental and formant 382 

frequencies, can account for word and voice driven recognition effects (Fig. 2), here we find that 383 

these features need not be explicitly computed to explain behavioral findings . Instead, a 384 

biologically plausible representation involving frequency and modulation selective filters that 385 

mirror the representation in subcortical regions can explain behavioral findings across diverse 386 

backgrounds, talkers, and words on a single trial basis. This is consistent with a computational 387

framework whereby interference occurs within specific subbands, with spectrotemporal filters that 388 

are jointly selective for frequency and spectrotemporal modulations. The auditory midbrain is 389 

likely a key brain region where such a decomposition and any interference would occur, since it is 390 

the first stage in the auditory system exhibiting a preponderance of modulation tuning 37 , covering 391 

an extensive range of spectrotemporal modulations (to ~500 Hz) 37,38 . This is in contrast to cortical 392 

regions, which have a more restricted range of modulation selectivity (mostly < 20 Hz) 37,39,40 and 393 

would be less likely to account for modulation-driven behavioral effects shown here. 394 

Our approach is related to several existing measures of speech intelligibility 41,42 . Although 395 

these previous measures have focused on speech quality ratings after distortion or compression, 396 

they often use similar statistical features to those used here. There are a wide variety of sound 397 

representations, and modulations are likely to play a key role in many tasks 23,43-46 . Our results also 398 

provide an alternative and complementary modeling framework to task optimized deep neural 399 

network models 47-49 . Such models can replicate some aspects of human behavior but often have 400 

tens of layers and thousands to millions of parameters. The parameters of neural network models 401 

can also be difficult to interpret and attempts to probe such networks have revealed that these 402 

models can differ substantially from human perception. For instance, task-optimized neural 403 

networks seem to use different invariances for sound recognition compared to humans 50 , and the 404 

modulation statistics influencing machine perception are not well matched to human speech-in-405 

noise behavior 2. Unlike neural networks, which are optimized to perform a particular task (e.g., 406 

predict words), GPR is optimized to predict a listener’s behavioral sensitivity (correct or incorrect 407 

judgments). In this manner, GPR takes the sound representation and predicts behavioral outcomes, 408 

requiring few dimensions (45 here) and producing linear perceptual transfer functions for each 409 

listener that directly indicate which modulation statistics impact perception. Since the GPR 410 

framework produces a listener-specific model of hearing and identifies the statistical features 411 

underlying individual behavior, it could potentially be applied to compare perception across 412 

people, animals, or machine systems and could also be used as a diagnostic for hearing disorders 44 

413 

or speech perception difficulties. 414 

Although our results here provide a new, generalizable model of speech in noise perception, 415 

there are several limitations for our approach and important open questions. First, our findings are 416 

based on semi-realistic listening conditions using natural background sounds and speech consisting 417 

of multiple words, talkers, and voice categories. While these experimental sounds are acoustically 418 

diverse, natural auditory scenes also contain variability in reverberation and spatial cues that distort 419 

spectrotemporal content and alter speech perception 18-21 . It is important to note that here we also 420 

presented sounds at a fixed SNR and intensity. Testing perception under more variable conditions 421 

could potentially alter the model performance. The model itself is also based on trial-wise 422 

prediction. Using a convolutional GPR 44 , with unknown word onsets and offsets, may better 423 

account for the temporal dynamics of continuous speech perception, such as glimpsing and co-424 

articulation. Finally, neural and behavioral measurements alongside GPR may provide a more 425 

thorough test of how spectrotemporal modulation cues are represented for sound mixtures. 426

Materials and Methods 428 

Human Psychoacoustics 429 

Experiments were approved according to the University of Connecticut Institutional 430 

Review Board (IRB). We recruited female (n=7) and male (n=5) native English speakers, ages 21-431 

47 who had normal hearing sensitivity (>20 dB HL for left and right ears, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8 kHz, 432 

tested with a GSI Audiostar Pro audiometer [Grason-Stadler], with a Radioear 3045 supra-aural 433 

headset). Participant responses were recorded with anonymized participant IDs for confidentiality. 434 

Of the (n=12) enrolled participants, (n=2) did not complete the study and were thus excluded from 435 

the data analysis. The experiments were conducted at the University of Connecticut Storrs Campus 436 

and in a sound shield room (Industrial Acoustics Co.). Sounds were delivered through a Fireface 437 

UC digital audio interface (RME Audio Inc.) amplified with a BHA-1 headphone amplifier 438 

(Bryston Ltd.) and sent to HD 820 headphones (Sennheiser Co.). All sounds were calibrated to 65 439 

dB SPL. Sound delivery and the acquisition of behavioral responses via keyboard entry were 440 

controlled using custom software using Psychtoolbox 51 in Matlab (Mathworks Inc.) on a Mac 441 

Mini M1 computer (Apple, Inc.). 442 

Participants were presented with sound mixtures consisting of foreground speech and 443 

background environmental noises to assess how both foreground and background features impact 444 

recognition performance in natural auditory scenes. Each speech sequence (Fig. 1C) consisted of 445 

a randomly chosen triplet of English digits (D1-D2-D3), delivered with a random inter-digit time 446 

interval during each trial (T1-T2-T3) and with different speakers (S1, S2, and S3). During each 447 

trial the three speakers were randomly selected from one voice group population: Adult Male 448 

(AM), Child Male (CM), Adult Female (AF), or Child Female (CF) (see Fig. 1E). Speech digits 449 

were selected from the TIDIGITS corpus 52 which contains 326 talkers (111 AM; 114 AF; 50 CM; 450 

51 CF). Each foreground triplet sequence was paired with a natural or perturbed background 451 

masker at an SNR of -9 dB. The instantaneous timing of each digit was used to calculate the SNR, 452 

such that the silent gaps (intervals between digits) did not contribute to the SNR measurement, 453 

which would create an artificially lower SNR. The onset and offset segments of all sounds were 454 

windowed with a 100 ms B-spline ramp to minimize transients at the beginning and end of each 455 

sound. For each sound, participants were tasked with reporting the three digits sequence that was 456 

presented. 457

Ten natural background sounds (speech babble, running water, traffic noise, jackhammer 459 

and factory sound, etc.) and white noise (control) were used as masker stimuli, similar to previous 460 

experiments 2. For some trials, we altered the statistical structure of the background stimuli to 461 

characterize how the spectrum and modulation statistics of the backgrounds specifically influence 462 

recognition. As a reference, natural backgrounds were presented in their unaltered original (OR) 463

Experiment Block Structure 477 

The experiment was broken up into 25 experimental blocks, each containing 25 different 478 

trials for each stimulus condition (Fig. 1E). Each participant completed two experimental blocks 479 

at a time, typically comprising 6 hours of testing split in two 3-hour sessions. In each experimental 480 

block, each of the 11 backgrounds and their 3 perturbations were tested (33 background sounds) 481 

across the 4 voice conditions shown in Fig. 1E such that there were 132 trials per block and 3,300 482 

trials across the experiment. Each foreground stimulus was constrained to a randomly selected set 483 

of 3 speakers (S1-S2-S3) from the designated speaker population (AM, CM, AF, CF), random time 484 

delays (T1-T2-T3) and a random digit triplet (D1-D2-D3). 485 

Scoring Criteria, Voice and Digit Projections 486 

Single digit recognition performance was scored according to digit identity and serial 487 

position with 9,900 single digit measurements total for each participant. On a single trial with a 488 

target foreground digit sequence of 9-8-3, for instance, a response 8-8-1 is scored as 0-1-0 489 

(1=correct; 0=incorrect). We then averaged single digit recognition accuracy across subsets of trial 490 

conditions to identify variations in accuracy (Fig. 2). First compute a “Voice Projection” (VP) by 491 

sub-selecting trials corresponding the unique combinations of Background (11 Natural 492 

Backgrounds), Perturbation (OR, SM, MM), and Voice Identity (AM, CM, AF, CF), with averages 493 

calculated for each participant averaging over digits and repeated trials. We then calculated a “Digit 494 

Projection” (DP) where single trial responses were averaged across unique combinations of 495 

Backgrounds (11 Natural Backgrounds), Perturbations (OR, SM, MM), and Digits (0-9). Note that 496 

trials are balanced across voice-background-perturbation conditions and unbalanced (but 497 

uniformly distributed and randomized) across digit-background-perturbation conditions. That is, 498 

voice-background-perturbation conditions have identical numbers of trials, but digit conditions do 499 

not. 500

To characterize the relationships between articulatory features, background conditions, and 528

judgements. Here we used the ‘fitglme’ function (MATLAB 2022b) with binomial observations 531 

and a logistic link function with random effects (intercepts) for each participant listener and each 532 

TIDIGITS speaker (n=326). 533

articulatory features F0, F1, F2, HM. We evaluated the model goodness-of-fit using the Akaike 537 

information criterion (AIC). We hypothesized that accuracy was driven by background effects 538 

interacting with the pitch of the speaker and modulated by the identity of the target word. To 539

Reduced Feature Models 647 

To explore what features may be driving variability in accuracy in both the voice and digit 648 

projections, we compared the performance of the full GPR model with several reduced models. 649 

Specifically, we varied the stimulus task features used in the model (foreground, background, 650 

foreground-background interaction) and varied the stimulus representation (spectral features alone 651 

or including spectral and temporal modulations). We fit GPR to each reduced feature set, using the 652 

method described above. Here, to prevent overfitting and ensure an equitable comparison across 653 

models, each feature set was dimensionality reduced to 15 PCs. We then evaluated the k-fold cross 654 

validated prediction sets scored according to the R 2 for the voice and digit projection average 655 

accuracy, as well as single trial accuracy. Single-trial accuracy was assessed using receiver 656 

operating characteristic (ROC) relative to the single-digit behavioral responses, resulting in a 657 

summary area under the curve (AUC) metric for each participant. Separate from the averaged VP 658 

and DP scores, the AUC allows us to observe fluctuations in performance for single trials. Thus, 659 

the AUC could be helpful in exploring the effects of individual trial differences that could be 660 

accounted by the local mMPS statistics. Reduced models were compared for each of the different 661 

model metrics, reduced model comparisons for the DP and VP were done through a paired fisher 662 

z-transform test, while the AUC was compared via paired t-test. These statistical analyses were 663 

extended to both the population model and glimpsing/static model (see below). 664 

Individual vs Population Models to Predict Within and Across-Participant Behavior 665 

While the perceptual transfer functions have the advantage that they reflect single-666 

participant behavior, we also sought to test whether a single population-level model could 667 

generalize and predict performance on unseen participants. Thus, we altered our GPR method to 668 

do leave-one-out cross validation across participants. We sub-selected an individual K-fold from 669 

the original models per participant and averaged across 9 participants to predict the remaining, 670 

held-out participant. We then compared the performance of these “population models” to the 671 

“individual models” described above. 672

Glimpsing, the idea that soft sound segments or silent gaps in a background sound produce 674 

local increases in SNR, has been proposed as one mechanism for segregating speech from noise 675 14,15 . Since our model estimates the local mMPS summary statistics overlapping each digit, it has 676 

access to stimulus power of the speech and background at a given time point during the digit 677 

duration. Thus, in its original form, the model could potentially account for “classical glimpsing”. 678 

To test whether glimpsing can be measured with the GPR model, we compared the above described 679 

GPR model (referred to here as the glimpsing model ) to a static GPR model variant which uses the 680 

time-average mMPS over the background segments that overlap the three digits in each sound to 681 

make accuracy predictions (the static model ). As with the reduced, and population models, we 682 

assessed the performance by calculating the variance explained for the VP and DP averages, in 683 

addition to calculating the AUC for both the static and glimpsing models. While the VP and DP 684 

variance explained could be less susceptible to single trial variance, the AUC represents single trial 685 

performance. To explore if certain backgrounds were prone to glimpsing, and whether specific 686 

background features contributing to glimpsing could be identified, we compared the AUC from 687 

the glimpsing and static models for each background (OR, SM and MM). Here positive AUC 688 

difference suggests that the background is sound is prone to glimpsing, while a negative difference 689 

suggests that time-averaged summary statistics better describe perception 55 .690
