Abstract 1

Predictive processing theories posit that the auditory system continuously generates 2

expectations and updates internal models when incoming evidence violates those 3

expectations. Whether such updating can be expressed as a temporally precise, 4

change-like neural response at a context-defined moment during continuous 5

stimulation––without any physical change––has remained unclear. Using a6

transitional click-train paradigm in human EEG, we show that listeners reliably detect 7

subtle midpoint transitions when click timing is fixed, whereas temporal uncertainty 8

markedly weakens this sensory-driven detection. Strikingly, even in a physically 9

unchanged stimulus, variable click timing elicits a robust response time-locked to the 10 

nominal midpoint. This endogenous change-like signal is stimulus-statistics 11 

dependent––absent in the fixed-timing no-change control––temporally confined to the 12 

early post-change window, and strongly state dependent, being amplified during 13 

active change detection relative to passive listening. Moreover, within identical 14 

no-change trials, the response tracks subjective change reports. Together, these 15 

findings show that temporal uncertainty and task engagement jointly gate predictive 16 

updating in continuous auditory streams, providing a compact assay to dissociate 17 

sensory-driven change responses from context- and state-dependent predictive 18 

signals. 19

Introduction 1

Listening is fundamentally predictive. In natural scenes, the auditory system 2

rarely waits for sounds to “arrive” before interpreting them; instead, it continuously 3

anticipates structure unfolding in time—when a footstep should land, when a syllable 4

should end, when a pattern should turn. Predictive-coding and related inference 5

frameworks formalize this intuition: the brain maintains generative models of sensory 6

causes, evaluates incoming evidence against those models, and updates them when 7

predictions fail 1-3 . One of the most robust population-level signatures of violated 8

expectation is mismatch negativity (MMN), elicited when a deviant breaks an 9

established regularity 4-6 . At the neuronal level, stimulus-specific adaptation (SSA) 10 

can likewise be interpreted as a predictive response: repeated inputs become expected 11 

and are down-weighted, whereas rare or deviant events elicit relatively preserved 12 

responses, consistent with local prediction-error signaling 7-14 . Together, these 13 

observations support the modern view that MMN-like signals reflect inference over 14 

sequence structure and the consequences of violated expectations, rather than a simple 15 

reaction to novelty 4-6,15 .16 

A striking implication of this framework is that neural updating can be aligned to 17 

expected moments even when there is little—or no—corresponding acoustic energy. 18 

In omission paradigms, the absence of an anticipated sound evokes time-locked 19 

cortical activity, demonstrating that prediction-related signals can be anchored to 20 

internal timing rather than to stimulus onset or physical change 16,17 . Hierarchical 21 

designs further show that prediction errors are sensitive to higher-order structure: 22

when the brain infers that a deviant should occur at a particular position, omitting that 1

expected deviant can elicit stronger responses than omitting a standard, consistent 2

with multi-level predictions and errors 15,18 . Related work using nonlinguistic 3

structured stimuli has likewise revealed hierarchical temporal processing across the 4

primate thalamocortical system 19 . Moreover, expectations need not be driven solely 5

by external stimulus statistics. Evidence from “phantom” or internally generated 6

percepts suggests that endogenous perceptual states can establish predictions that 7

yield deviance-like responses when violated 20 .8

Despite these advances, two gaps limit our understanding of predictive updating 9

during continuous stimulation. First, omission-related responses are most often 10 

studied in event-based or strongly rhythmic contexts, where entrainment and 11 

phase-reset can contribute to time-locked activity, complicating the question of 12 

whether prediction-related update signals can be expressed without discrete events 13 

and without overt rhythmic scaffolding 16,17 . Second, expectation-induced effects 14 

(including “phantom deviant”-like phenomena) are rarely tested when the critical 15 

update time is embedded within continuous input and defined primarily by task 16 

context—for example, an anticipated midpoint transition—rather than by the 17 

occurrence (or omission) of discrete tokens 15,20 . Thus, it remains unclear whether 18 

change-related neural machinery can be recruited as a temporally precise, 19 

context-defined update signal even when the stimulus contains no physical change, 20 

and how such updating depends on stimulus temporal statistics and behavioral 21 

engagement. 22

To address these questions, we used a transitional click-train paradigm in which 1

two continuous segments are concatenated and a nominal change point is fixed at the 2

midpoint. Within the same dataset 21 , we identified a midpoint-locked predictive 3

change-like response that emerges without any physical change: in the no-change 4

condition with variable click timing around the same mean (Irreg 4–4 ), EEG showed a 5

significant response aligned to the nominal transition, which was context dependent, 6

enhanced by active detection, and absent in the no-change control with fixed click 7

timing (Reg 4-4 ). We propose that increased temporal uncertainty reduces the precision 8

of bottom-up evidence and increases reliance on top-down priors about when change 9

is likely, enabling an internally timed update signal even when the stimulus remains 10 

constant. 11

Discussion 1

Our results identify a context-defined, predictive change-like response in human 2

EEG that can emerge in the absence of any physical stimulus transition. Behaviorally, 3

subtle timing changes were reliably detected when click timing was fixed, but 4

sensitivity was markedly reduced when click timing was variable (Fig. 1C). 5

Consistent with the expanded stimulus set and the change-prevalent trial structure, 6

participants were globally biased toward reporting “change” (Fig. 1D). At the neural 7

level, sensory-driven change responses to physical transitions were robust in the 8

fixed-timing condition and attenuated under variable timing (Supplementary Figs. 9

1–2). Crucially, during active detection the variable-timing no-change stimulus 10 

(Irreg 4–4 ) elicited a midpoint-locked response that exceeded the fixed-timing 11 

no-change control (Reg 4–4 ) (Fig. 2A–C), was amplified relative to passive listening 12 

(Fig. 3A–C), and remained absent in the fixed-timing no-change control across 13 

sessions (Supplementary Fig. 3). Moreover, when aligned to the nominal change point, 14 

a clear peak was observed only in the early post-change window, whereas the 15 

pre-change and late post-change windows showed no comparable response (Fig. 4). 16

Predictive coding frames perception as inference: the brain maintains generative 3

models of the sensory world, compares incoming evidence against those models, and 4

updates them when expectations fail 2,3 . Mismatch negativity (MMN) provides a 5

canonical population-level signature of violated expectation, reliably elicited when a 6

discrete deviant violates an inferred regularity 4-6 . Omission paradigms sharpen the 7

same logic by demonstrating that time-locked cortical responses can be evoked even 8

when an expected event is absent, indicating that prediction-related update signals can 9

be anchored to internal timing rather than stimulus energy 16,17 . However, most 10 

omission effects are demonstrated in rhythmic, event-based sequences, where discrete 11 

token timing and entrainment are integral to the paradigm. Our data extend this 12 

framework to continuous stimulation with a context-defined update timepoint—a 13 

fixed, task-relevant midpoint embedded within a change-prevalent design (Fig. 1A, 14 

Fig. 1D). In this regime, the response we observe is not best interpreted as a reaction 15 

to a missing sound token, but as an internally timed, prediction-error–like update 16 

signal to a missing structural transition at the expected midpoint of a continuous 17 

stream (Fig. 1A; Figs. 2–3). This shift matters because it implies that predictive 18 

machinery can be recruited not only by violations of what occurs, but also by 19 

violations of when an internally defined update is expected during ongoing input 2,3,15 .20 

The result also complements “phantom deviance” evidence showing that endogenous 21 

perceptual states can establish top-down predictions that yield deviance-like responses 22

Materials and methods 1

Experimental procedure and participants 2

This study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki (2013) 3

> 24

. A total of 42 participants (20 males and 22 females, mean age: 23.36 years old, 4

standard deviation: 2.55) participated in this experiment. Participants maintained a 5

stationary head position while listening to auditory stimuli and responding via 6

keyboard presses. Experiments including healthy participants were approved by the 7

from all participants. 9

The experiment included two sessions: a passive session followed by an active 10

Data Analysis 1

Preprocessing . Data analyses were performed using MATLAB R2021b (The 2

MathWorks, Inc., Natick, MA, USA) and the FieldTrip toolbox 27 . Monopolar 3

referencing was applied using the default reference electrode positioned between Cz 4

and Pz. Multichannel EEG data underwent sequential preprocessing: initial bandpass 5

filtering (two-pass Butterworth, 0.5–40 Hz), followed by epoch extraction from -1 to 6

3 s relative to trial onset. Independent component analysis (ICA) removed 7

electrooculogram (EOG) artifacts. Baseline correction subtracted the mean activity 8

from -200 to 0 ms pre-stimulus onset per trial. Motion artifacts were evaluated via a 9

relative threshold, flagging samples outside mean ± 3 × SD as "bad." Trials exceeding 10 

20% bad samples were discarded as "bad trials," and channels with >10% bad trials 11 

were labeled "bad channels." Bad channels were excluded first (based on all-channel 12 

data), followed by bad trials from remaining channels. Event-related potentials (ERPs) 13 

were computed by averaging epochs per experimental condition, channel, and subject. 14 

Prior to group-level analysis, ERPs were normalized by dividing each channel's data 15 

by the standard deviation of baseline (-200 to 0 ms pre-stimulus onset) per subject to 16 

minimize inter-channel variability. 17 

Permutation test . Permutation tests for time-point and channel-wise 18 

comparisons of ERP data between stimuli groups employed a two-tailed cluster-based 19 

approach via the 'ft_timelockstatistics' function in the FieldTrip toolbox for MATLAB. 20 

A two-tailed independent-samples t-test was conducted at each time point and channel 21 

to compute t-values, contrasting Dataset A (e.g., ERP data for Irreg 4-4 in the passive 22
