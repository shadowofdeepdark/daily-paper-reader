Abstract 16 

Background: Eye gaze provides crucial insights into perceptual and cognitive processes during 17 

naturalistic movie viewing, yet concurrent eye tracking is often unavailable in functional MRI (fMRI) 18 

research. While deep learning models can estimate gaze directly from fMRI eyeball signals, their out-of-19 

the-box generalizability across heterogeneous datasets requires empirical evaluation. 20 

Methods: We applied a specific pre-trained model from the DeepMReye framework in a zero-shot setting 21 

(without dataset-specific fine-tuning) to estimate gaze during movie watching across three independent 22 

fMRI datasets. Model accuracy was evaluated against camera-based eye-tracking data and via inter-23 

subject correlations. Furthermore, we derived eye-movement-related time series from the predicted gaze 24 

signals to map their associated brain activation. 25 

Results: At the individual level, predicted gaze showed modest correspondence with measured ground-26 

truth data ( r ≈ 0.24–0.37), yielding brain activation maps largely restricted to the visual cortex. In contrast, 27 

group-averaged gaze predictions exhibited substantially higher reliability ( r ≈ 0.73–0.84). First-level 28 

General Linear Models (GLMs) derived from group-averaged predictions successfully revealed 29 

widespread activation across established oculomotor control regions, including the frontal and parietal 30 

eye fields. Exploratory analyses of age-related effects on gaze prediction and brain activity yielded 31 

inconsistent results across datasets. 32 

Conclusions: Under a zero-shot implementation, the pre-trained model exhibits limitations for 33 

individual-level inference, likely reflecting the absence of dataset-specific training. However, group-34 

averaged fMRI-based gaze estimates successfully capture shared viewing behaviors and robustly support 35 

the investigation of eye-movement-related brain activity. These findings inform the appropriate use of 36 

fMRI-based gaze decoding for naturalistic neuroimaging datasets lacking ground-truth eye-tracking logs. 37

1. Introduction 42 

Eye movements are central to human perception and cognition. They guide visual exploration, support 43 

attentional selection, and facilitate social communication by revealing what people attend to and how they 44 

interpret dynamic visual scenes (Henderson 2003; Tatler et al. 2011). During naturalistic activities such as 45 

movie watching, gaze patterns reflect moment-to-moment fluctuations in engagement, comprehension, 46 

and social inference (Hasson et al. 2004; Richardson and Saxe 2020). Because of this richness, eye 47 

movements are widely used as behavioral markers for studying cognitive processes, including attention, 48 

memory, and theory-of-mind reasoning. 49 

Functional MRI (fMRI) provides powerful tools for studying the neural mechanisms underlying 50 

these processes, but a major limitation in naturalistic fMRI research is that most datasets do not include 51 

simultaneous eye-tracking recordings. This gap is especially evident in large open-access datasets and 52 

legacy studies collected before eye-tracking systems became widely available (Sonkusare et al. 2019). 53 

Even today, many research sites lack in-scanner eye-tracking hardware, and when such systems are 54 

available, obtaining high-quality recordings is often difficult—particularly in populations such as young 55 

children, individuals with neurodevelopmental or psychiatric conditions, or participants who have 56 

difficulty maintaining stable head position. As a result, many potentially informative fMRI datasets lack 57 

reliable gaze information, complicating the interpretation of neural activity related to visual attention, 58 

scene processing, and oculomotor control and leaving valuable datasets under-utilized. 59 

Recent advances in deep learning have introduced a possible solution. Models such as 60 

DeepMReye can infer gaze position directly from the MR signal of the eyeball region (Frey et al. 2021). 61 

These approaches build on earlier observations that motion of the eye and surrounding tissues produces 62 

systematic MR signal variations within the eyeball, likely reflecting contrast changes between internal 63 

ocular structures (Son et al. 2020). Such MR signals can be decoded using modern machine learning 64 

approaches, including convolutional neural networks. Subsequent work has further explored 65 

convolutional neural network-based approaches for MR-based gaze estimation (Wu et al. 2024), 66 

demonstrating the potential of deep architectures to capture gaze-related information from eyeball signals. 67

The ability to estimate gaze retrospectively offers a powerful opportunity: large fMRI datasets without 68 

eye tracking can be “augmented” with reconstructed gaze trajectories, enabling novel analyses of visual 69 

behavior and attention without requiring camera-based systems. 70 

Beyond methodological utility, fMRI-based gaze decoding opens new avenues for developmental 71 

and clinical research. Children and adults differ substantially in their visual exploration patterns, 72 

attentional control, and consistency of gaze behavior (Kirkorian et al. 2012). These behavioral differences 73 

correspond to ongoing maturation of large-scale cortical networks involved in visual attention, cognitive 74 

control, and social processing (Casey et al. 2005; Luna et al. 2015). Movie-watching paradigms are 75 

particularly well-suited for developmental studies because they elicit reliable neural responses across 76 

individuals while also capturing meaningful age-related differences in gaze patterns and comprehension 77 

(Cantlon and Li 2013; Petroni et al. 2018; Di et al. 2026). 78 

In this study, we applied the pretrained DeepMReye to three naturalistic movie-watching fMRI 79 

datasets to assess its ability to reconstruct gaze behavior and to characterize the neural correlates of 80 

predicted eye movements. We first evaluated prediction accuracy by correlating model-based gaze 81 

estimates with simultaneously acquired camera-based eye-tracking data in a zero-shot setting. We also 82 

leveraged the well-established phenomenon of inter-individual correlation during shared video viewing 83 

(Hasson et al. 2004; Madsen and Parra 2022; Di et al. 2025), and quantified inter-individual correlations 84 

of the DeepMReye predictions themselves. If the model accurately captures gaze behavior, we expect 85 

both strong correlations with ground-truth eye tracking and high intersubject consistency. In addition, 86 

because gaze patterns show robust inter-individual correlations, we computed group-averaged gaze 87 

predictions and compared correlations with eye-tracking data at both the group and individual levels. This 88 

approach tests the hypothesis that even when individual predictions are noisy, averaging across 89 

participants may enhance signal quality and improve correspondence with ground-truth gaze. 90 

Moreover, we examined brain activity associated with eye movements by using frame-to-frame 91 

gaze displacement (Euclidean distance) as a regressor in a conventional fMRI activation analysis. We 92 

hypothesized that we would identify regions involved in oculomotor control, such as the frontal eye fields 93

studying gaze-related brain activity in naturalistic fMRI datasets. 97

2.1. Participants and Datasets 115 

This study utilized three publicly available fMRI datasets. All datasets included fMRI scans acquired 116 

during naturalistic movie-watching tasks, along with demographic information such as age and sex. 117 

Across all datasets, participants were included based on data quality criteria. Quality control procedures 118 

were applied at both the fMRI and eye-tracking levels. For the NV dataset specifically, eye-tracking data 119 

were evaluated post-preprocessing; rather than enforcing a rigid quantitative threshold, participants were 120 

excluded only if their recordings contained a critically insufficient amount of usable data. 121 

Natural Viewing (NV) Dataset (Telesford et al. 2023): This dataset includes 22 adult participants (ages 122 

23–51 years) and was collected at the Nathan S. Kline Institute. We focused on the naturalistic viewing 123 

portion where participants watched “The Present” (TP; 4min 18s) and a clip from “Despicable Me” (DM; 124 

10min). The fMRI data was acquired with a repetition time (TR) of 2.1s. Critically, this dataset also 125 

contains simultaneously recorded eye-tracking data (EyeLink 1000 Plus, 250 Hz), which serves as a 126 

ground truth for validating the accuracy of the predicted gaze data from DeepMReye. 127 

Partly Cloudy (PC) Dataset (Richardson and Saxe 2020): This dataset provided data from two distinct 128 

age groups, comprising 53 children (ages 3–12 years) and 29 adults (ages 18–39). All participants viewed 129 

a silent version of the animated short film “Partly Cloudy” (5min 36s). The fMRI scanning TR was 2.0s. 130 

This dataset does not include concurrent eye-tracking recordings. 131 

Healthy Brain Network (HBN) Dataset (Alexander et al. 2017): This large-scale dataset includes data 132 

from children and young adults (ages 5–21 years). To avoid confounding factors from psychopathology, 133 

we included only healthy control participants. The stimuli were the same as in the NV dataset (TP and 134 

DM). The HBN data were acquired with a higher temporal resolution (TR = 0.8s), providing a more 135 

densely sampled BOLD signal. After applying quality control criteria, our final sample consisted of 82 136

2.2. fMRI Preprocessing 149 

All fMRI data were preprocessed using SPM12 in MATLAB following a standardized pipeline (Di and 150 

Biswal 2023). The procedures for the PC and HBN datasets have been detailed previously (Di and Biswal 151 

2022; Di et al. 2023); a brief summary is provided here. Structural images were segmented and 152 

normalized to MNI space. For The Present and Despicable Me datasets, the first 10 functional volumes 153 

were removed (yielding 240 and 740 volumes), whereas all volumes were retained for Partly Cloudy .154 

Functional images were realigned, coregistered to the structural scan, normalized using the segmentation-155 

derived deformation fields, and smoothed with an 8-mm FWHM Gaussian kernel. 156

reflects the intended use case of applying pretrained DeepMReye to large-scale fMRI datasets that lack 161

network. All preprocessing steps followed the official DeepMReye pipeline. Eye voxels were extracted 164

visually inspected for each dataset, and representative examples are provided in the Supplementary 168 

Materials. The model utilizes normalized eyeball voxel signals as input to estimate eye position in degrees 169 

of visual angle for each fMRI volume. The output was a time series of (x, y) gaze coordinates for each 170 

fMRI run. 171

2.4. Statistical Analysis of Gaze Movement 173 

To quantitatively evaluate model performance, we assessed the correspondence between ground-truth 174 

eye-tracking data and predicted gaze movements using Pearson correlation analysis. For each participant 175 

and each viewing task, recorded and predicted gaze time series were temporally aligned. Individual 176 

correlation coefficients were computed separately for the horizontal (x) and vertical (y) gaze dimensions 177 

across all time points within a run. For group-level evaluations, we first computed group-averaged gaze 178 

trajectories by averaging the eye-tracking and predicted gaze time series across participants at each time 179 

point, yielding a summary time series for tracking and prediction, respectively. Pearson correlation 180 

coefficients were then calculated between the group-mean tracking and group-mean prediction 181 

trajectories for the x and y dimensions. This group-averaging approach emphasizes shared, stimulus-182 

driven gaze dynamics by reducing idiosyncratic individual variability. In addition to Pearson correlation, 183

3. Results 232 

3.1. DeepMReye predictions vs. Eye-tracking in NV dataset 233 

We first validated DeepMReye predictions using the NV dataset, which included concurrent camera-234 

based eye-tracking data as ground truth. At the group level, the model performed well: group-averaged 235 

predicted gaze time series showed high correspondence with the group-averaged ground-truth eye-236 

tracking data. As illustrated in Figure 2a and d, group-averaged predicted and actual gaze trajectories 237 

were closely aligned. For the DM stimulus, correlations were r = 0.84 ( p < .001) horizontally and r = 0.73 238 

(p < .001) vertically, and mean absolute errors were MAE = 0.564 horizontally and MAE = 0.486 239 

vertically. Similarly, for TP stimulus, Pearson correlations were r = 0.81 ( p < .001) for both horizontal 240 

and vertical gaze directions, and mean absolute errors were MAE = 0.550 horizontally and MAE = 0.380 241 

vertically. These results demonstrate that DeepMReye reliably captures the group-level patterns during 242 

naturalistic viewing. 243 

However, prediction accuracy declined substantially at the individual level. Figure 2b and e present 244 

raincloud plots of individual-participant Pearson correlation coefficients between predicted and ground-245 

truth gaze for horizontal (x) and vertical (y) directions. Individual-level correlations showed greater 246 

variability and were generally lower than those observed at the group level. For the DM stimulus, median 247 

correlations were r = 0.32 (interquartile range [IQR] = 0.26–0.47) for horizontal and r = 0.28 (IQR = 248 

0.17–0.34) for vertical gaze directions. For the TP stimulus, median correlations were r = 0.37 (IQR = 249 

0.16–0.47) for horizontal and r = 0.24 (IQR = 0.15–0.33) for vertical gaze directions. While a subset of 250 

participants exhibited relatively high correlations between predicted and ground-truth gaze, many others 251 

showed substantially lower correspondence. Consistent with the correlation results, MAE values indicated 252 

moderate spatial accuracy at the individual level, with improved consistency observed after group 253 

averaging ( Figure 2c , f). At the individual level, corresponding median spatial errors were MAE = 2.75° 254

3.3. Intersubject correlations of predicted gaze in PC dataset 305 

To further examine developmental effects on gaze behavior, we analyzed intersubject similarity and 306 

variability in the PC dataset ( Figure 4 ). Correlation matrices revealed increasingly structured alignment 307 

between the same group of individual predictions with increasing age ( Figure 4a and b). The distinct 308 

high-correlation cluster among adults in the lower-right quadrant of the matrices, together with the 309 

weaker correlations involving younger participants, highlights age-related differences in gaze synchrony 310 

during movie viewing. 311 

Quantitative analyses demonstrated a significant positive relationship between age and gaze 312 

similarity for both horizontal (r = 0.352, p = 0.001) and vertical (r = 0.391, p < 0.001) components 313 

(Figure 4c and d), indicating that older participants exhibited stronger correspondence with normative 314 

gaze patterns. These findings suggest age-related increases in gaze synchrony, potentially reflecting 315

3.4. Brain activations Associated with Eye Movements 326 

To identify the core neural network associated with eye movements during naturalistic movie viewing, we 327

between consecutive time points, using either individual-level predicted gaze or group-averaged gaze 329

As illustrated in Figure 5a and c, group-averaged eye-movement regressors produced highly 331 

similar whole-brain activation patterns for the HBN dataset during the movie-watching task DM and for 332 

the PC dataset during movie-viewing task. Robust activation was consistently observed in a distributed 333 

oculomotor control network, with prominent involvement of frontal and parietal regions implicated in the 334 

planning and control of eye movements, including the frontal eye fields (FEF) and the intraparietal sulcus 335 

(IPS). These regions constitute core components of the dorsal attention and oculomotor networks that 336 

support saccade generation, visuospatial attention, and top-down control of visual exploration. 337 

In addition to this control network, widespread activation was observed in occipital visual 338 

cortices, encompassing the cuneus, lingual gyrus, and primary visual cortex, reflecting the expected 339 

engagement of early visual processing during continuous gaze shifts. Notably, despite substantial 340 

differences between the HBN and PC datasets—including movie stimuli, participant age distributions, 341 

and fMRI acquisition parameters—the oculomotor control network associated with gaze displacement 342 

was largely conserved across datasets. This robust main effect provides a consistent basis for subsequent 343 

analyses examining how age modulates gaze-related brain activity. Compared with the DM movie-344 

watching task, the TP task in the HBN dataset exhibited fewer and more spatially restricted activation 345 

clusters at the same statistical threshold ( Figure 5b ), potentially reflecting reduced statistical power due 346 

to shorter task duration and more constrained viewing dynamics. 347

significantly smaller and more localized activation clusters across all three conditions. As illustrated in 349 

Figures 5d and 5e , the DM and TP movie-watching tasks in the HBN dataset still showed positive 350

4. Discussion 421 

In this study, we demonstrate that although a pretrained DeepMReye model yields limited accuracy at the 422 

individual level, its predictions become highly reliable when aggregated across participants, providing a 423 

potential approach for extracting gaze information from large-scale naturalistic fMRI datasets lacking 424 

eye-tracking. Although fine-tuning has been shown to improve performance when ground-truth eye-425 

tracking data are available, our zero-shot evaluation highlights the extent to which a pretrained model 426 

from the DeepMReye framework can generalize across datasets without dataset-specific adaptation. At 427 

the behavioral level, model-derived predictions closely matched ground-truth eye-tracking data at the 428 

group level, both within and across datasets, while individual-level prediction accuracy was more variable. 429 

At the neural level, gaze-related activity consistently engaged a canonical oculomotor control network 430 

encompassing frontal and parietal regions, including the frontal eye fields and intraparietal sulcus, 431 

alongside expected activation in visual cortices. Importantly, age-related modulation of gaze-related brain 432 

activity differed across datasets, with non-linear developmental trajectories observed in ROI-based 433 

analyses. 434 

As established in our analyses of the NV dataset, the group-averaged predictions demonstrated 435 

substantially higher fidelity to the ground-truth data. By averaging across participants, idiosyncratic 436 

variations and model prediction noise are attenuated, thereby enhancing the shared, stimulus-driven 437 

component of the gaze signal. This improvement in signal-to-noise ratio was directly mirrored in our 438

neuroimaging results: brain activation maps derived from the group-mean regressor revealed a 439 

comprehensive and neuroanatomically plausible network, whereas maps from individual regressors 440 

yielded more restricted and variable activations. 441 

The strong performance observed at the group level likely reflects the dominant influence of 442 

shared, stimulus-driven gaze patterns in naturalistic viewing. Prior work has demonstrated that movies 443 

strongly synchronize attention and gaze across viewers, producing robust inter-subject correlations in 444 

both behavior and neural responses (Hasson et al. 2004; Dorr et al. 2010; Mital et al. 2011). By averaging 445 

predicted gaze across participants, idiosyncratic noise arising from anatomical variability, voxel-level 446 

signal heterogeneity in the orbit, and motion-related artifacts is attenuated, yielding a clearer estimate of 447 

the shared component of gaze behavior. These findings highlight a key strength of applying pretrained 448 

MR-based gaze decoding models in naturalistic paradigms: despite the relatively coarse spatiotemporal 449 

resolution of fMRI, the shared component of visual attention elicited by naturalistic stimuli can be 450 

reliably recovered under this implementation. 451 

In contrast, predictive correspondence at the individual level was markedly lower and more 452 

variable. Multiple factors may contribute to this limitation, including inter-individual differences in eye 453 

anatomy, orbit geometry, EPI distortions, and head motion, all of which can affect the extraction of 454 

reliable features from signals surrounding the eyeballs (Petit and Haxby 1999; Beauchamp 2003). In 455 

addition, because the pre-trained model was not fine-tuned on our datasets, differences in scanner 456 

characteristics, acquisition parameters, and population demographics may reduce generalizability (Tran et 457 

al. 2025; Botvinik-Nezer et al. 2020). Further, the temporal resolution of fMRI constrains the detection of 458 

rapid eye movements, making it difficult to capture saccades or subject-specific patterns of visual 459 

exploration (Engbert and Kliegl 2003). Together, these factors suggest that while the model captures 460 

shared, stimulus-driven gaze components, the reduced individual-level performance observed here likely 461 

reflects the absence of dataset-specific training and domain adaptation, rather than an intrinsic limitation 462 

of the DeepMReye framework itself. It is further supported by the weak or absent eye-movement–related 463 

activations observed when individual-level predictions were used. Eye-tracking data quality metrics were 464

that measurement noise alone does not fully account for the observed variability in individual-level 466 

performance. In addition, the relationship between predicted error (PE) and decoding performance was 467 

not consistent across tasks or evaluation metrics (Supplementary Figure. S3), indicating that PE should be 468 

interpreted cautiously as a proxy for model reliability in this zero-shot setting. Specifically, PE showed 469 

associations with MAE but not correlation in the TP task, whereas in the DM task the pattern differed, 470 

indicating task-dependent relationships between PE and performance metrics. These findings further 471 

suggest that factors such as domain shift and lack of dataset-specific adaptation may play a more 472 

prominent role in limiting individual-level performance. Importantly, the consistency of group-level 473 

predictions observed across datasets further suggests that the pretrained model captures robust and 474 

generalizable components of gaze behavior. Despite the aforementioned limitations and factors, the 475 

robustness to domain shift at the group level contrasts with the variability observed at the individual level, 476 

where subject-specific anatomical and noise-related factors play a larger role. Together, these findings 477 

indicate that while zero-shot models may be limited for individualized inference, they retain substantial 478 

utility for extracting reliable, population-level gaze signals across heterogeneous datasets. 479 

These findings have important methodological implications for fMRI studies that lack camera-480 

based eye tracking. Group-averaged gaze predictions can serve as a practical substitute for hardware-481 

based eye tracking in studies focused on population-level gaze dynamics or stimulus-driven attentional 482 

processes, particularly in large naturalistic datasets where eye tracking was not acquired (e.g., HCP, NSD), 483 

when using pretrained models in a zero-shot setting. However, the current approach is not suitable for 484 

applications requiring precise individual-level gaze measurement, such as quality control of fixation 485 

compliance, fine-grained behavioral analyses, or clinical assessments at the single-subject level, where 486 

camera-based eye tracking remains essential. 487 

It is important to acknowledge the inherent temporal limitations of fMRI when interpreting these 488 

oculomotor brain maps (the 'Nyquist problem'). Because the TRs in these datasets (800–2100 ms) are 489 

substantially longer than typical saccade durations (20–200 ms), the gaze signal is temporally aliased, 490

slow and acts as a low-pass filter, the high-frequency temporal precision of camera-based eye tracking is 493 

largely lost when convolved with the hemodynamic response function. Therefore, activation maps derived 494 

from fMRI-based gaze displacement are unlikely to differ substantially from those derived from high-495 

resolution eye tracking, as both ultimately capture the same slow, aggregate oculomotor variance and 496 

macroscopic shifts in visual attention. 497 

A central neuroscientific finding of this study is that age-related modulation of gaze-associated 498 

brain activity is non-linear and highly context dependent. In the HBN dataset, which spanned childhood 499 

through young adulthood and involved viewing a socially complex movie clip (DM), ROI analyses 500 

inverted U-shaped developmental trajectories in multiple regions within a distributed posterior–frontal 501 

network. These trajectories were characterized by increasing gaze-related activation from childhood into 502 

adolescence, followed by a subsequent decrease into young adulthood. Such patterns are consistent with 503 

prior work highlighting non-linear maturation of attentional and visual systems across development 504 

(Madden 2007). In contrast, the PC dataset exhibited a different pattern of age-related modulation, with 505 

gaze-related activity showing a predominantly decreasing trajectory across the sampled age range. Given 506 

the limited adolescent coverage in this dataset, these effects likely reflect differences between age groups 507 

rather than a fully resolved continuous developmental trajectory. Together, these findings emphasize that 508 

apparent age effects in naturalistic viewing are strongly shaped by both stimulus properties and sampling 509 

structure and are not adequately captured by simple linear models. 510 

The presence of age-related differences in group-level analyses primarily suggests that the model 511 

may be sensitive to broad developmental trends in gaze behavior (Fernandes et al. 2024), but 512 

insufficiently precise to detect subject-specific effects amid individual noise. Whether these age effects 513 

reflect true changes in visual attention or methodological artifacts remains unclear. Future work should 514 

examine how demographic variables influence model performance and whether targeted model adaptation 515 

can improve sensitivity to individual differences. 516

Our findings revealed a notable dissociation between horizontal and vertical eye movement 517 

synchronization across development. While horizontal eye movement synchrony remained relatively 518 

stable across age groups, vertical eye movement synchrony showed a significant positive correlation with 519 

age (r = 0.298, p = 0.004). This differential developmental trajectory may reflect the distinct neural 520 

substrates underlying horizontal and vertical saccade generation. The prolonged maturation of vertical eye 521 

movement control suggested by these findings aligns with previous literature suggesting that certain 522 

oculomotor functions continue to develop throughout childhood and adolescence (Grönqvist et al. 2006). 523 

These results indicate that age-related differences in gaze-related brain activity are strongly 524 

shaped jointly by the developmental stage and the stimulus context. Group-averaged model-derived gaze 525 

predictions are sufficiently robust to reveal population-level developmental patterns, whereas individual-526 

level predictions remain too noisy to support reliable subject-specific inference. This highlights an 527 

important practical boundary of current zero-shot applications of pretrained fMRI-based gaze decoding 528 

models: deep-learning–based MRI gaze decoding may be valuable for identifying population-level 529 

developmental trajectories, but its current accuracy limitations constrain its utility for fine-grained 530 

individual differences work. 531 

Several limitations should be noted. Our analyses were based on a limited number of movie 532 

stimuli and datasets, with camera-based eye tracking available only for one dataset. The use of a pre-533 

trained model without dataset-specific fine-tuning may have constrained performance particularly in 534 

populations that differ substantially from the training sample. In addition, the temporal resolution of fMRI 535 

inherently limits the model’s sensitivity to rapid eye-movement events. These limitations motivate further 536 

methodological innovation. 537 

Taken together, our findings should be interpreted in the context of a zero-shot application of a 538 

pretrained model. While this approach provides a practical solution for large datasets lacking eye tracking, 539 

it does not fully reflect the capabilities of the DeepMReye framework when combined with dataset-540 

specific fine-tuning, standardized preprocessing pipelines, and rigorous quality control. Thus, the current 541 

results should be interpreted as a lower-bound estimate of performance under zero-shot conditions. Future 542

Recent work (Park et al. 2025) has highlighted the influence of motion artifacts and preprocessing 545

correction or fine-tuning may not be feasible. In this context, the robust group-level consistency observed 549

Conclusion 563 

In summary, this study demonstrates that fMRI-based gaze prediction provides a potentially useful 564 

framework for investigating gaze-related brain activity at the group level during naturalistic viewing, 565 

while also highlighting current limitations for reliable individual-level inference. By validating a 566 

pretrained DeepMReye model in the Natural Viewing dataset using concurrent camera-based eye-tracking 567 

data, and by evaluating its zero-shot performance through inter-subject correlation and downstream 568

to move beyond static, task-isolated paradigms, offering a promising path toward understanding how 575
