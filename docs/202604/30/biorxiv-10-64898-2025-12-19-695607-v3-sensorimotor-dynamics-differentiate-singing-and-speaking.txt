# Abstract 21 

Singing and speaking often dissociate clinically—people who stutter can sing fluently, and 22 individuals with aphasia and speech output problems from stroke may express sentences fluently 23 in song—yet the neural mechanisms of this centuries-old clinical phenomenon remain unclear. We 24 recorded intracranial EEG while neurosurgical patients produced matched sentences by singing or 25 speaking, sampling millimeter- and millisecond-scale activity across both hemispheres. During 26 articulation, high-frequency activity (70–150 Hz) lateralized oppositely across behaviors, with 27 right-dominant sensorimotor cortex (SMC) activation for singing and left-dominant activation for 28 speaking. Phase-amplitude coupling revealed that mu-band (~10 Hz) phase locally organized HFA 29 in left SMC in a channel-specific, spatially interdigitated architecture preserved across both 30 behaviors. Mu-band synchrony in speaking showed an early left-led pattern, whereas singing 31 exhibited a ramping of synchrony within the left sensorimotor cortex and between the two motor 32 cortices, supporting progressive interhemispheric recruitment. Frequency-domain Granger– 33 Geweke causality revealed that the left primary somatosensory cortex drives both motor cortices 34 at speech onset. In contrast in singing, control over motor cortices relied on both hemispheres. 35 Singing and speaking engage a shared mu-coupled sensorimotor substrate through distinct 36 recruitment dynamics. 37

# Introduction 38 

For centuries, a striking dissociation has been noted between singing and speaking in many clinical 39 populations. People who stutter when they speak do not stutter when they sing 1–3 . Individuals with 40 aphasia, a language disorder occurring after brain injury, struggle to produce spoken utterances 41 but fluently produce them through song 4–6 . Even in cases of dementia, singing familiar songs can 42 induce moments of lucidity and boost cognitive function 7–9 . These observations have motivated 43 the theory that speech and song production draw on distinct neural mechanisms, yet these 44 mechanisms remain undefined. Given its central role in generating and monitoring articulatory 45 gestures 10–16 , sensorimotor activity during singing provides a window into how singing diverges 46 from speaking identical utterances. 47 48 We compared singing and speaking production in neurosurgical patients undergoing invasive 49 monitoring for treating drug-resistant epilepsy, a clinical population that allows direct observation 50 of real-time neural activity using intracranial electroencephalography (iEEG). Participants 51 produced matched sentences in both modalities (singing and speaking) while we recorded 52 millimeter-scale, millisecond-resolution activity across bilateral sensorimotor cortex (SMC) (Fig. 53 1). We quantified high-frequency activity (70–150 Hz; HFA), local phase-amplitude coupling 54 (PAC), oscillatory synchrony (1–70 Hz), and directed connectivity during articulation to probe 55 hemispheric and sensorimotor dynamics of speaking versus singing linking clinical observations 56 to circuit-level physiology. 57 58

High frequency activity and the mu rhythm in sensorimotor cortex 68 

69 

We observed higher articulation-locked HFA for singing in the right sensorimotor cortex (SMC; 70 S1 and M1) and for speaking in the left SMC. Cluster-based permutations were run to directly 71 compare singing versus speaking from 0.5 s before articulation onset throughout the first 2 seconds 72 of production. Time-varying power estimates were derived using a Morlet wavelet approach 73 during the production period following the onset of articulation, which was manually identified 74 from the audio files for each recording session. High frequency activity (HFA; 70–150 Hz) is a 75 key marker of local cortical activation 17,18 and drives the BOLD signal 19,20 . In the left SMC (S1 76 and M1), HFA power was higher in the speaking condition (Left S1: pperm =0.0004; Left M1: 77 

pperm =0.0005) (Fig. 2A,B). In the right SMC (S1 and M1), HFA power was higher in the singing 78 condition (Right S1: pperm =0.0006; Right M1: pperm =0.0002, 0.008) (Fig. 2C,D). Regions of interest 79 analyzed were these structures bilaterally: inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), superior temporal gyrus 80 (STG), postcentral gyrus (PostCG/S1), and precentral gyrus (PreCG/M1). Only significant results 81 are reported. 82 83 Information transfer in a network is supported by low frequency oscillatory activity and coherence 84 in the 1–70 Hz range 21–26 . Fluent output depends on millisecond-scale, precisely-timed motor 85 commands that recruit dozens of muscles across the respiratory, laryngeal, and supralaryngeal 86 systems to orchestrate coordinated tongue, lip, and jaw gestures that shape dynamic vocal-tract 87 constrictions. In natural speech, transitions between these gestures are rapid and seamless 10,27,28 .88 Our power analysis also revealed lower-frequency rhythms in SMC (Fig. 2). Clusters in left and 89 right SMC had higher power for the speaking condition in the 10–30 Hz range ( pperm =0.0154, 90 0.0080) (Fig. 2B,C). We used Irregular-Resampling Auto-Spectral Analysis (IRASA) 29 , to 91 separate fractal components (related to 1/f aperiodic activity) and oscillatory components in the 92 power spectrum. A ~10 Hz “mu rhythm” (with a 20 Hz harmonic) was prominent in SMC (Fig. 93 2E–H). The mu rhythm has been previously associated with speech and somatomotor activity 30– 

94 32 . Together, the findings in Fig. 2 establish two features of sensorimotor activity during sentence 95 production: HFA, which lateralizes oppositely for the two behaviors, and a prominent SMC mu 96 rhythm, which is shared bilaterally across them. 97

When MI(z) was averaged across HFA frequencies and channels within each left-121 hemisphere ROI and plotted across phase frequencies, a clear peak emerged at ~8–10 Hz in left 122 S1 and left M1, but not in other canonical left language ROIs (STG, IFG) or adjacent ROIs (MFG, 123 SMG) (Fig. 3A). The significant channels were in middle and dorsal SMC, not in ventral SMC. 130 To examine the temporal and spatial precision of this coupling, we re-estimated MI on individual 131 trials in an example participant (S6), with broad electrode coverage within the left SMC (S1: 14 132 electrodes; M1: 12 electrodes). Within left SMC, individual channels showed PAC with HFA that 133 was stable across trials and preserved across speaking and singing (Fig. 3D–E). Across left-SMC 134 channels in this participant, preferred mu phase formed a bimodal distribution with peaks near 0° 135 and 180°, a structure that was absent in other regions in canonical left language cortex (Fig. 3F). 136 Channels with opposite phase preferences (0° or 180°) were spatially interdigitated across left 137 SMC with no clear macroscopic segregation (Fig. 3G). Together with the trial-by-trial stability 138 shown in Fig. 3D–F, this pattern indicates that mu-phase preference is a precise, channel-specific 139 dynamic property of left SMC rather than a coarse anatomical gradient, consistent with prior ECoG 140 work showing functionally specific but spatially intermixed representations in sensorimotor 141 speech cortex 10,12 .142

Different mu-synchrony patterns arise in sensorimotor cortex during singing and speaking 169 

170 

The PAC results showed that mu phase locally organizes HFA within left SMC, in a manner shared 171 by speaking and singing. Fluent vocal production, however, also requires precise coordination 172 across sensorimotor sites and between the two hemispheres. We therefore examined pre- and post-173 articulatory synchrony dynamics in the left and right hemisphere for singing versus speaking. 174 Regions of interest analyzed included these structures bilaterally: IFG, STG, MFG, SMG, S1, and 175 M1. Only significant results are reported. Low frequency oscillatory synchrony further 176 distinguished singing versus speaking. Our results revealed that singing evokes mu rhythm 177 synchrony intra-hemispherically within the left SMC and inter-hemispherically between left and 178 right SMC. Synchrony of intra- and inter-hemispheric regions was measured using intersite phase 179 clustering (ISPC 34 ), a measure of phase-based connectivity that quantifies phase-locking of signals 180 over time. For each connection between two areas of interest, only subjects with electrode contacts 181 in both areas were tested for synchronization between areas. Analysis was restricted to the 182 oscillatory range (1–70 Hz) and cluster-based permutations were run on the ISPC estimates, across 183 all pairs of electrode contacts between areas, to compare synchrony in singing and speaking. To 184 capture the dynamics of preparation and articulation, we quantified the relative synchrony 185 estimates in the mu rhythm (8–12 Hz) during singing and speaking compared to their pre-186 articulatory baselines (z-scored using 0.5 s before production). 187

The left sensorimotor cortex achieves speech-like mu-synchrony through singing 189 

190 

Left SMC mu-synchrony (between left M1 and left S1) peaked at speech onset ( pperm =0.0020) but 191 continued to ramp up during singing ( pperm =0.0002), as revealed by directly comparing between 192 conditions using a cluster-based permutation approach (Fig. 4A, left panel). For speaking, we 193 observed stronger synchronization in the mu rhythm right before articulation, with an increase at 194 the point of articulation, as shown by z-scored relative synchrony changes (RSC) in the mu-band 195 (8–12 Hz), obtained by normalizing within-condition estimates using a 0.5 s pre-articulation 196 baseline (Fig. 4A, right panel). On the other hand, for singing, we observed weaker 197 synchronization at the onset, measured by RSC in the mu rhythm, however synchronization 198 ramped up throughout production. The synchrony estimates also revealed that, in the left SMC, 199 the mu rhythm is detectable in the speaking condition during the pre-articulatory period. In 200 contrast, it is not present in the singing condition during the pre-articulatory period, but synchrony 201 emerges during production. These observations indicate that singing achieves a sustained speech-202 like activity profile in the left mu rhythm without the same signatures of preparatory activity seen 203 in speaking. Right SMC mu-synchrony was more sustained throughout the speaking condition 204 (pperm =0.0020) and did not show a strong ramping up of synchrony in either condition (Fig. 4B). 205

Interhemispheric mu-synchrony increases in singing in sensorimotor cortex 223 

224 Interhemispheric communication is integral to speech motor control. Left-hemisphere stroke with 225 aphasia is often accompanied with right-sided facial drooping 35 , reflecting predominantly crossed 226 control of facial and articulatory musculature 36,37 (e.g., corticobulbar projections). In fluent 227 production, the sensorimotor system must coordinate both hemispheres to drive symmetric, 228 precisely-timed movements of lips, tongue, and jaw 10,12,27,38,39 . Differential singing modulation of 229 left–right SMC interactions may explain how song can bypass or mitigate motor deficits after left-230 hemisphere injury. 231 232 Interhemispheric synchrony between the motor cortices (left M1–right M1) at the onset of 233 articulation was initially higher for speaking ( pperm =0.0119) (Fig. 4C) but ramped up during 234 singing ( pperm =0.0060, 0.008, 0.0125). Interhemispheric synchrony between left S1 and right M1 235 also showed higher mu synchrony at the onset of articulation for speaking ( pperm =0.0004; 0.0009) 236 (Fig. 4D). However, as the sentence unfolded, there was higher synchrony for singing 237 (pperm =0.0002; 0.0006). 238 239 

Directional mu-band influences across sensorimotor cortex differ for singing and speaking 240 

241 We observed distinct mu-band synchrony profiles in SMC for speaking and singing at articulation 242 onset and later in production. Singing elicited overall higher mu-band synchrony as production 243 unfolded, with different preparatory signatures. To probe network directionality, we applied 244 Granger causality analyses to identify which SMC nodes lead during speaking vs. singing. Using 245 a frequency-domain Granger–Geweke Causality (GGC) framework 40 , we estimated directed 246 influences between areas in two windows (initiation: 0–0.5 s post-articulation onset and 247 production: 1–1.5 s post-articulation onset). We first determined the dominant directionality of the 248 influence between the two areas by testing if the net influence (area 1 → area 2 minus area 2 → 249 area 1) differed significantly from zero, separately for each condition (speak and sing). # Discussion 391 

A neural mechanism for the expression of linguistic content through song 392 

393 Intracranial EEG recordings revealed that HFA power is right-lateralized for singing sentences in 394 the sensorimotor cortex and left lateralized for speaking. We also found distinct dynamics for 395 singing versus speaking within the mu-band (~10 Hz), a prominent rhythm observed in the 396 sensorimotor cortex in both hemispheres. Phase-amplitude coupling analyses further revealed that 397 mu phase locally organizes HFA within left SMC in a channel-specific, spatially interdigitated 398 coupling architecture that is preserved across both behaviors, indicating that speaking and singing 399 share a common sensorimotor substrate that is differentially recruited across the network. During 400 singing, high-frequency activity (HFA, 70–150 Hz) was right-lateralized, and mu-band (~10 Hz) 401 synchrony ramped up over time within left SMC and between the two M1s, indicating progressive 402 recruitment of left circuitry via interhemispheric cooperation. In speaking, HFA was left-403 lateralized, and mu-band dynamics were left-led at onset with sustained right-hemisphere 404 involvement thereafter. Directional analyses further showed that speech initiation reflects left S1 405 → left/right M1 drive, whereas singing does not require left-hemisphere initiation at onset and 406 instead can proceed through right S1 → right M1 or left S1 → right M1 drive followed by right 407 M1 → left M1 influence as production unfolds. Together, these findings support a model in which 408 singing and speaking engage a shared mu-coupled sensorimotor substrate through distinct 409 recruitment dynamics: speaking via a left-led, bilaterally executed route, and singing via a right-410 hemisphere led, bilaterally-coordinated route that progressively recruits left sensorimotor nodes 411 via interhemispheric synchrony. 412 413 In our data, we observe a ~10 Hz rhythm with a ~20 Hz harmonic, classically described as a mu 414 rhythm, that coordinates left–right interactions in S1/M1. Work by Norman et al. (2025) suggests 415 that a closely related 6–10 Hz “sensorimotor theta” rhythm is an intrinsic organizing frequency of 416 the speech motor system: during articulation, bilateral SMC shows enhanced phase coherence in 417 this band, coupled to bursts of high-frequency activity and to pulse-like vocal-tract kinematics at 418 specific phases 16 . Our phase-amplitude coupling results provide direct, channel-resolved evidence 419 that concurs with the multiplexing proposed by Norman and colleagues: mu-band phase locally 420 organizes HFA in left SMC, with channel-specific phase preferences that are stable across trials, 421 spatially interdigitated, and preserved across speaking and singing. This observation, together with 422 prior ECoG work showing functionally specific but spatially intermixed representations in 423 sensorimotor speech cortex 10,12 , supports the idea that speech production depends on precise phase 424 alignment of an intrinsic SMC oscillation that multiplexes sensory feedback, motor commands, 425 and articulator dynamics. In Broca’s aphasia, where lesions typically involve left ventral peri-426 rolandic and insular cortex and their underlying white matter 44 , this intrinsic generator and its 427 coupling pathways are disrupted. The result is a system in which local rhythms can be expressed, 428 but their phases fail to align reliably across the network, consistent with halting, effortful speech, 429 variable apraxic errors, and occasional “lucky” error-free productions of complex words. 430 431 Within this framework, singing provides an alternative way to engage the same intrinsic rhythm 432 in a more stable manner. The rhythmic–melodic scaffold imposes an external temporal structure 433 that can reinforce mu/theta-band synchrony within and between hemispheres and tighten the 434 alignment between oscillatory phase, HFA bursts, and vocal-tract kinematics. Feedforward and feedback control of speech and song 443 

444 The left and right sensorimotor cortices jointly subserve planning, execution, and monitoring of 445 articulatory movements. In the Directions into Velocities of Articulators model (DIVA) 39 

446 framework, feedforward speech initiation commands are primarily routed through the left motor 447 cortex, with the right motor cortex contributing more to feedback control after movement onset. 448 Consistent with this model, and also with a recent MEG report 46 that lateralization shifts across 449 time in the mu rhythm from left to right in speaking, our intracranial data also show early left-450 biased mu-synchrony during speaking that evolves into sustained right-hemisphere involvement, 451 alongside left-lateralized HFA. By contrast, singing does not exhibit a left-initiated onset; instead, 452 mu-band coupling ramps within left S1–M1 over time while right-lateralized HFA and right-to-453 left influences support execution, suggesting a different feedforward/feedback balance for song. 454 455 

A shared sensorimotor substrate, differentially recruited 456 

457 A central implication of our findings is a distinction between the architecture of sensorimotor 458 cortex and the way it is engaged. The phase-amplitude coupling results demonstrate that mu phase 459 locally organizes HFA in left SMC in a channel-specific, spatially interdigitated fashion, and that 460 this local coupling is present in both speaking and singing. The mu-synchrony and directionality 461 results, by contrast, reveal that singing and speaking differentially recruit this substrate at the 462 network level, through distinct initiation biases, interhemispheric coupling dynamics, and 463 directional information flow. This architecture-versus-use distinction reframes the clinical 464 dissociation between singing and speaking: rather than positing entirely separate circuits, it 465 suggests that song and speech share a common sensorimotor substrate whose recruitment can be 466 flexibly modulated by the structural scaffolding of music. 467 468 

Implications for future brain-computer interfaces (BCI) 469 

470 Recent brain-computer interfaces have successfully used HFA or spiking obtained from dense 471 electrocorticographic grids or Utah arrays in the left primary motor cortex to decode speech in 472 individuals with paralysis or anarthria 11,47,48 . These approaches in left M1 capture phoneme-473 specific content that, when combined with predictive language models, can be used to decode 474 speech from an intact cortex despite paralysis of the vocal-motor system. Our results showed that 475 HFA tracks local execution of motor commands in the left SMC for speaking with an increase in 476 HFA in the right SMC for singing, supporting enhanced population activity. In addition to the 477 HFA findings, we observed differences in local and interhemispheric synchrony in singing versus 478 speaking. We find that mu-band activity indexes intra- and interhemispheric communication 479 between left and right M1 and S1 with differential pre- and post-articulatory dynamics as the 480 sentence unfolds. # Methods 502 

Patients 503 

504 We collected intracranial EEG data from 18 patients (mean age 35.2 +/- 14.3 years; 11 females) 505 undergoing intraoperative neurosurgical treatment for pharmacoresistant epilepsy. All patients 506 were implanted with stereo EEG depth electrodes to determine the site of seizure onset for surgical 507 resection. We selected 13 patients, without lesions, that had coverage in the left or right 508 sensorimotor cortex. The electrode placement was managed entirely by the neurosurgical team for 509 each patient. The recordings took place at multiple hospitals including Loma Linda University 510 Medical Center (n = 1), Barnes-Jewish Hospital in St. Louis (n = 11), and the St. Louis Children’s 511 Hospital (n = 1). All patients provided written informed consent as part of the research protocol 512 approved by each hospital’s Institutional Review Board and by the University of California, 513 Berkeley. Patients were tested when they were fully alert and willing to participate. 514 

515 

Our cohort involved individuals with epilepsy undergoing presurgical monitoring, which may 516 affect the generalizability of the findings. We minimized disease- and treatment-related confounds 517 by testing only when patients were fully alert and by excluding contacts with epileptiform activity 518 or other artifacts. Electrode implantation was dictated by clinical need rather than experimental 519 design, yielding uneven, asymmetric coverage across participants; consequently, some effects may 520 have gone undetected due to limited sampling. 521 522 

Behavioral task 523 

524 Participants were presented visually and auditorily with matched sentences and were asked to 525 repeat them once as quickly as possible. The stimuli were adapted from a standard motor speech 526 assessment battery 49 , designed to engage motor speech planning and execution skills. The stimuli 527 contained 5-7 words, some of which were multisyllabic with consonant clusters that required 528 shifting between points of articulation. For example, in the word ‘spaghetti’, there is an initial 529 consonant cluster, which demands quick movement between distant points of articulation (bilabial 530 /p/ to velar /g/ and back to apico-alveolar /t/). An example sentence from the stimuli used is 531 “prepping lobster is the best”. A block design was used for singing and speaking conditions and 532 the order of presentation was counterbalanced across participants. To optimize for a hospital 533 setting, in which time is limited and patients tire quickly, 20 sentences were presented in each of 534 the spoken and sung conditions, for a total of 40 trials. 535 536

Irregular-Resampling Auto-Spectral Analysis (IRASA) 578 

579 To estimate the fractal component of the power spectrum, we applied IRASA 29 to the broadband 580 signal by resampling the time series with a set of non-integer factors h (typically spanning 1.1– 581 1.9), using both upsampling (h) and the corresponding downsampling (1/h). For each factor, we 582 computed the auto-power spectra of the two resampled signals and combined them via their 583 geometric mean to obtain a factor-specific estimate of the fractal spectrum. Because non-integer 584 resampling shifts narrowband oscillatory power to h-dependent frequencies while leaving the 585 fractal component largely invariant, we aggregated these factor-specific fractal estimates by taking 586 the median across h at each frequency, yielding a robust estimate of the fractal PSD. The oscillatory 587 PSD was then derived by subtracting the fractal PSD from the mixed PSD; to reduce residual 588 cross-terms, PSDs were additionally averaged across multiple time segments assumed to be 589 stationary, under the assumption of no systematic phase coupling between fractal and oscillatory 590 components. 591

Granger causality analysis 641 

642 Granger causality provides an operational definition of directed functional influence between two 643 time series 56 . In this framework, activity in region X is said to causally influence activity in region 644 Y if incorporating the past of X improves prediction of the current (or future) values of Y beyond 645 what can be achieved from Y’s own past (and, when modeled, the past of other observed signals). 646 Under a linear, stochastic dynamical model, this predictive improvement corresponds to a 647 reduction in the residual variance (prediction error) of Y when X is included as a predictor, and 648 can be expressed as an asymmetric measure of directed dependence. 649 650 In the frequency domain, Granger–Geweke causality (GGC) decomposes this directed 651 predictability by frequency, quantifying how strongly fluctuations in X at each frequency 652 contribute to predicting fluctuations in Y at the same frequency 40 . Practically, the method is 653 implemented by estimating the cross-spectral density of the multichannel data and applying 654 spectral factorization to obtain the transfer function and noise covariance that define the underlying 655 linear system. This formulation is well suited for oscillatory neural signals because it allows 656 directionality to be evaluated within targeted frequency bands (here, the mu band), and it 657 distinguishes directional influence (X → Y) from the reverse direction (Y → X) within the same 658 data segment, conditional on the signals included in the model. 659 660 We performed a connectivity analysis using the Granger causality framework, to test for 661 directionality of the influences between areas of the SMC network, in two time intervals 662 (‘initiation’: 0–0.5s; ‘production’: 1–1.5s post-articulation). In each interval, we derived a 663 frequency domain measure of GGC between two areas 39 . For this, we used a multitaper-based 664 approach for cross-spectral density estimation (time-bandwidth product NW =2) and nonparametric 665 spectral factorization 57–59 . For each inter-areal connection, we compared the directed connectivity 666 between speak and sing in the mu-band (8–12 Hz) using a paired-samples t-test across all electrode 667 pairs for that connection ( df = number of electrode pairs - 1). In the same connection, the dominant 668 directionality of the influences was tested by using a t-test on the net influence (area 1 → area 2 669 minus area 2 → area 1) across all electrode pairs ( df = number of electrode pairs - 1). If the net 670 influence was significantly higher than zero it indicates that the directed influence from area 1 → 671 area 2 is stronger than the directed influence from area 2 → area 1; vice versa, when the net 672 influence was significantly lower than zero. 673 674
