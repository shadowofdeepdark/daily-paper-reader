240 41 42 Abstract 

43 Low-frequency neural oscillations contribute to the parsing of continuous speech into linguistic 

44 units. Little is known however on the coupling of brain rhythms to higher-frequencies in speech 

45 such as fundamental frequency (F0) or pitch. Using magnetoencephalography, we investigated 

46 whole-brain cortical tracking of F0 while participants listened to sentences produced at normal 

47 rate or fast rate, where pitch naturally increases, and to artificially accelerated sentences, where 

48 F0 remains unchanged. Our results revealed significant brain-to-F0 coupling across all speech 

49 rates not only in right auditory but also in right parietal, insular, and pre- and postcentral regions, 

50 likely including the ventral larynx area. Importantly, the cortico-acoustic coupling peak 

51 frequency was higher for natural fast speech to reflect the corresponding F0 increase compared 

52 to normal rate and time-compressed speech. These findings demonstrate the engagement of an 

53 auditory-somato-motor network in F0 tracking, supporting its role in facilitating phonemic 

54 processing during the perception of naturally-produced speech.

367 1 Introduction 

68 By tracking speech temporal structure, neural oscillations contribute to the parsing of the 

69 acoustic stream into hierarchically-organized linguistic constituents 1–4 . Substantial evidence 

70 from electro/magnetoencephalography (E/MEG) revealed phase-coupling of delta- (~1-3 Hz) 

71 and theta-band (~4-8 Hz) auditory cortical activity to the slow modulations of speech amplitude 

72 envelope, which mainly reflect the syllable rate 5–9 . Speech envelope has also been shown to 

73 modulate low (35-45 Hz) and high gamma (> 50 Hz) amplitude as well as theta phase/low 

74 gamma amplitude coupling 6,10–14 .

75 Besides syllabic structure, speech periodicity is another temporal cue of chief importance for 

76 speech perception and communication as it conveys segmental (voicing, manner of articulation) 

77 and suprasegmental (intonation, stress) information 15 . It results from the quasi-periodic 

78 vibration of the vocal folds during production and includes a representation of the speaker’s 

79 fundamental frequency (F0), which is the prime acoustic correlate of voice pitch. Only limited 

80 evidence exists regarding cortical tracking of F0 in naturally-produced speech. Many studies 

81 on pitch encoding measured the Frequency-Following Response (FFR), a sustained oscillatory 

82 response generally recorded by averaging responses to repeated syllables or tones that mimics 

83 stimulus periodicity and reflects precise auditory temporal processing 16–19 . Although the FFR 

84 recorded in EEG at the scalp is believed to originate primarily from the brainstem 20 ,

85 contributions from the thalamus and auditory cortex were also reported, with cortical activity 

86 peaking at the stimulus F0 21–28 . Brain coupling to F0 in continuous sound streams has been 

87 given less attention. The studies available so far focusing on neural encoding of the F0 in 

88 naturalistic speech showed responses in the auditory cortex at the F0 of isolated sentences or 

89 stories 29–32 . For instance, using a linear modelling approach in EEG, Van Canneyt and 

90 colleagues 33 reported that the F0 of stories could be reconstructed from brain activity likely 

91 arising from the brainstem and the primary auditory cortex.

492 Here, we provide a novel contribution to this line of research by assessing whether neural 

93 oscillations, in the auditory cortex but also in other cortical regions, couple their oscillatory 

94 activity with the F0 of natural speech produced at different rates. We note that the term 

95 "coupling" is used here descriptively to refer to a statistical relationship between brain activity 

96 and the stimulus F0, without assuming whether this reflects the entrainment of endogenous 

97 oscillations or stimulus-driven phase-locked responses, a distinction that remains debated in 

98 the field 34 . Besides spectro-temporal modifications (e.g., shortening of acoustic cues, enhanced 

99 coarticulation) 35 , naturally speaking faster induces changes in the phonetic realization of F0. 

100 Acoustic studies in fact reported a shift in the frequency of the pitch range with increasing 

101 speech rate, namely decreased or increased pitch depending on speakers and languages 36,37 . We 

102 aimed to characterize how cortical F0 representation changes as a function of speech rate in 

103 natural speech. 

104 Following earlier work showing that low-frequency cortical activity (e.g., 2-9 Hz) adjusts its 

105 coupling frequency to match the faster syllable rate 38,39 , we asked whether a similar frequency-

106 shift occurs at higher frequencies to track the F0 changes accompanying speech rate increase. 

107 We note that the mechanisms underlying coupling at these different frequency scales likely 

108 differ: whereas theta/delta entrainment involves endogenous oscillatory dynamics, activity in 

109 the 77-97 Hz range examined here falls within the high-gamma band, which is generally 

110 thought to reflect local population firing rates rather than narrow-band oscillations 40,41 .

111 Nonetheless, the question of whether brain-to-stimulus coupling at these frequencies exhibits 

112 a comparable rate-dependent frequency shift remains relevant and largely unexplored. 

113 Using MEG, we examined brain coupling to the F0 of sentences naturally produced at a normal 

114 or fast rate, as well as of artificially accelerated (i.e. time-compressed) sentences. Given that 

115 time-compression reduces signal duration while keeping its spectral and pitch content intact 42 ,

116 time-compressed speech has the same F0 as normal rate speech despite a faster syllabic rate.

5117 By contrast, F0 inherently increases in naturally-produced accelerated speech compared to both 

118 normal rate and time-compressed speech. Using Minimum Norm Estimation source 

119 reconstruction methods, we compared cortico-acoustic coupling and power modulations during 

120 sentence perception in the three speech rate conditions. In keeping with previous studies 7,38 , we 

121 analysed coupling in two frequency ranges centred on the mean F0 of our sentence material 

122 (77-84 Hz for normal rate/time-compressed sentences and 90-97 Hz for natural fast speech). In 

123 addition, a whole-brain analysis allowed us to investigate whether coupling to sentences’ F0 

124 only occurs in the auditory cortex or whether it involves a more distributed cortical network, 

125 noting that previous work used region-of-interest based analyses, mostly in the primary auditory 

126 cortex e.g.23,32 . We are aware of only one stereotactic EEG / MEG study showing FFR in a 

127 temporo-parieto-frontal cortical network in response to musical tones 43 .

128 Our results reveal significant cortico-acoustic coupling in frequency ranges that are specific to 

129 the sentences’ fundamental frequency. Coupling indeed occurred in the higher frequency band 

130 (90-97 Hz) for naturally-produced fast speech compared to normal rate speech, mirroring the 

131 natural increase in speech F0. No change in coupling frequency (77-84 Hz) was observed for 

132 time-compressed sentences which have similar F0 to normal rate sentences. Crucially, brain-

133 to-F0 coupling was found in a right-lateralized network encompassing temporal, parietal, 

134 precentral and postcentral cortices, known to contribute to speech perception as well as to 

135 production. Such findings suggest that by adjusting to the rate of vocal fold vibration, neural 

136 oscillations in sensorimotor regions contribute to the encoding of pitch and of phonemic 

137 information conveyed by the periodicity of the speech signal.

6142 2 Results 

143 Twenty-four French native participants listened to natural normal rate (mean = 6.76 + 0.57 

144 syllables/s), natural fast rate (9.15 + 0.60 syllables/s) and time-compressed sentences (9.15 + 

145 0.60 syllables/s) while their MEG brain activity was recorded. The mean F0 of the natural 

146 normal rate and time-compressed sentences was 81.3 Hz (SD 3.3) as calculated from the peaks 

147 of the power spectrum of the sentences using Python. It increased to 91.9 Hz (SD 9.8) for 

148 naturally accelerated sentences. Amplitude-modulated noise stimuli, generated with the 

149 envelopes of normal rate and fast rate stimuli, and which lack pitch and verbal information, 

150 were included as control conditions. We assessed neural tracking of changes in fundamental 

151 frequency by computing cortico-acoustic coupling between the acoustic signals and source-

152 localized MEG time-series using phase-lag index (PLI) and, as a complementary approach, 

153 coherence measures (see Methods for more details about the selected coupling metrics). 

154 Because sentences’ F0 in the different speech rate conditions overlapped to some extent, we 

155 analysed speech-brain coupling in subsets of sentences for which the F0s of the normal and 

156 time-compressed conditions were most distant from the F0s of the fast condition (see Methods 

157 and Supplementary Figure S1 and Table S1). The F0 in these subsets peaked at 80 Hz for natural 

158 normal rate and time-compressed sentences, and at 93 Hz for natural fast rate sentences. We 

159 therefore conducted the coupling analysis in two frequency bands of interest centred on these 

160 values, respectively, 77-84 Hz and 90-97 Hz. For statistical analysis, PLI and coherence 

161 measures for actual stimulus encoding were compared with measures obtained on surrogate 

162 data using a shuffling procedure and correction for multiple comparisons using, respectively, 

163 cluster-based correction and FDR (see Methods).

7167 2.1 Cortico-acoustic phase coupling shifts with speech F0 

168 PLI analyses revealed significant phase-locking of neural oscillations to speech fundamental 

169 frequency, with a shift in coupling frequency that mirrored the changes in F0 following natural 

170 syllable rate acceleration (Figure 1). Cortico-acoustic coupling was found at 77-84 Hz for 

171 normal rate sentences mainly in bilateral Heschl’s, superior temporal, supramarginal and pre-

172 /postcentral gyri as well as in the insula (see Supplementary Table S2 for more details). For the 

173 natural fast condition, PLI increased in the higher 90-97 Hz range in a right-lateralized network 

174 mostly including the same regions, namely Heschl’s, superior temporal, supramarginal and 

175 postcentral gyri (note from Figure 1A that coupling, albeit not significant, was also observed in 

176 the left hemisphere). As previously outlined, time-compressed sentences had the same F0 as 

177 normal rate sentences. This was well reflected in the brain by a significant PLI increase for this 

178 condition in the lower, normal rate pitch range only (77-84 Hz), in a similar network as the one 

179 found for normal rate speech. No significant phase-coupling was on the contrary observed for 

180 naturally accelerated sentences in this frequency range, stressing the specificity of the effect. 

181 Additional coherence analyses corroborated this pattern of results (see Figure 2). Indeed, 

182 coherence significantly increased bilaterally in the lower frequency range for normal rate and 

183 time-compressed sentences, but in the higher range for natural fast sentences. As expected, for 

184 the amplitude-modulated noise conditions, neither PLI nor coherence measures showed any 

185 significant cortico-acoustic coupling increase in any of the two frequency bands of interest 

186 (Figures 1 and 2).

250 251 2.3 Power modulations mirror coupling findings 

252 We finally examined whether brain coupling to speech F0 is also reflected in the amplitude of 

253 the cortical responses by analyzing source power modulations in each experimental condition 

254 and frequency band of interest. To this end, we contrasted power during the stimulus encoding 

255 period with power measured during the pre-stimulus baseline, using cluster-based statistical 

256 correction. Results showed a specific increase of power as a function of sentences’ F0 (Figure 

257 4). In the normal and time-compressed conditions with F0 peaking around 80 Hz, brain activity 

258 significantly increased in the 77-84 Hz range in the right (and most of the time left for time-

259 compressed speech) Heschl’s, superior temporal, supramarginal, insular and postcentral gyri. 

260 For natural fast rate sentences, the power increase was found at 90-97 Hz in a similar right-

261 lateralized network also encompassing the precentral gyrus. No significant increase of power 

262 was found for amplitude-modulated noise stimuli in any of the frequency ranges.

272 273 3 Discussion 

274 Our results revealed brain coupling to speech fundamental frequency, with a shift in coupling 

275 frequency and power increase that echoed the natural F0 increase with natural speech rate 

276 acceleration. Whereas phase-coupling (PLI), coherence and amplitude were enhanced at 77-84 

277 Hz for normal rate and time-compressed speech (F0 peak at 80 Hz), coupling shifted to 90-97 

278 Hz for naturally produced fast speech (F0 peak at 93 Hz). Crucially, this coupling was not 

279 restricted to the auditory cortex but encompassed a network distributed over sensorimotor 

280 regions. Overall, these findings therefore provide novel evidence that high-frequency cortical 

281 oscillations couple with the fundamental frequency of speech in the case of natural speech rate 

282 variations. 

283 Coupling and power modulations to F0 were mainly found in the right or bilateral superior 

284 temporal (including the primary auditory cortex), supramarginal, pre-/postcentral gyri and

14 285 insula, in line with previous work on pitch processing and voicing perception 45–49 . FFR studies 

286 also reported responses in the right or bilateral auditory cortex to syllables or continuous 

287 speech 23,26–28 . The lack of coupling to amplitude-modulated noise further suggests that phase-

288 locking does not reflect responses to the temporal envelope of sentences but to their high-

289 frequency carrier information. Importantly, our thorough whole-brain analysis using three 

290 different cortical parcellations highlighted the right Heschl’s gyrus, ventral postcentral gyrus 

291 and subcentral area (BA 43, at the inferior edge of the postcentral gyrus) as showing maximal 

292 coupling to sentences’ F0. Note the close spatial proximity of the latter two regions (14.7 mm 

293 based on a computation of distance between their centroids) which may correspond to the same 

294 somatosensory area. Each of these three regions furthermore showed up among the top eight 

295 regions with strong cortico-acoustic coupling across the three parcellations, indicating the 

296 reliability of the results. Other right-lateralized regions such as the insula and the supramarginal 

297 and precentral gyri also consistently coupled their oscillatory activity with speech F0, 

298 irrespective of the parcellation used. Interestingly, these regions are part of a dorsal network 

299 underlying speech auditory-motor integration 50,51 . The somatosensory cortex has also been 

300 advocated to contribute to speech perception by closely interacting with auditory regions 52 . In 

301 this regard, the subcentral gyrus (BA 43) identified in our study, owning both sensory and motor 

302 properties 53,54 , is seen as the ventral larynx motor area activated for the production of 

303 vocalizations and pitch control 55–59 . High-gamma activity in this region has been reported to 

304 increase before and during phoneme articulation 60 and for voiced consonant production 61 ,

305 suggesting its role in the control of vocal fold movements. This area furthermore increases its 

306 connectivity with the auditory cortex during mere voicing perception 48 , and it can decode 

307 articulatory features of heard phonemes 62–64 . Our results concur with a functional role of this 

308 ventral somatosensory area in speech perception as it is one of the regions that best couple its 

309 high-frequency oscillatory activity with speech F0.

15 310 A dorsal larynx motor area, located in the precentral gyrus, is also thought to be involved in 

311 voicing and pitch production and perception 56,57,65–69 . In fact, and although this should be further 

312 tested, the precentral region coupling to speech F0 in our study may correspond to this dorsal 

313 motor region. This could reflect some simulation of the laryngeal gestures underlying pitch 

314 control during speech perception 70 . We also found reliable coupling to F0 in the supramarginal 

315 gyrus and insula. This agrees with the inferior parietal cortex acting as a sensorimotor interface 

316 to coordinate speech perception and production 51,71,72 as well as with its role in phonological 

317 processing 73 . The insula is also assumed to contribute to auditory-motor speech integration 74 .

318 It is indeed engaged in speech production and the control of laryngeal muscles and of F0 

319 modulations 75–77 , but also in prosodic perception and auditory temporal processing 78–81 . In 

320 keeping with our work, high-gamma activity in the posterior insula was shown to track the F0 

321 of a repetitive vowel similarly as in Heschl’s gyrus 82 . The insula moreover shares connections 

322 with auditory 83 and motor regions, including the laryngeal motor cortex 84,85 . Altogether, our 

323 findings thus putatively suggest that besides the primary auditory cortex targeted by previous 

324 work 23,24,32,86 , a dorsal sensorimotor cortical network involved in speech auditory-motor 

325 integration syncs to the periodicity of vocal fold vibration in perceived speech. 

326 327 Whether and how our findings relate to the cortical FFR to repetitive syllables 21,23,24,27,28 is not 

328 straightforward. One difference is that the FFR is averaged over several hundreds of the same 

329 stimulus, whereas we assessed cortico-acoustic coupling and power modulations to a set of 

330 varying sentences. Still, the FFR is thought to indicate phase-locked neural activity to the 

331 stimulus F0. The different measures may therefore reflect at least some common processes. As 

332 previously mentioned, a FFR source in the right posterior auditory cortex has been described 

333 and would be involved in pitch computation 23,24 . Among the few studies that investigated F0 

334 coupling to sentences or continuous speech, some focused on the brainstem 87–89 , while others

336 occasionally including regions extending slightly beyond primary auditory cortex (e.g.,

339 and power modulations at high frequencies across widespread cortical regions, we bring new 

340 support for brain coupling to F0 in natural speech that occurs well beyond the auditory cortex, 

341 in regions that are involved in auditory-somato-motor integration (in agreement with 43 for 

342 tones). 

343 344 Although the increase in coherence we found might be explained, at least partly, by power 

345 increases at the same frequencies, this cannot hold for the phase-lag index which is insensitive 

346 to amplitude modulations. Cortical coupling to speech F0 may therefore capture the periodic 

347 nature of the phenomenon. Coffey and collaborators 86 likewise proposed that their auditory 

348 cortex FFR entailed oscillatory entrainment since the brain response outlasted syllable offset 

349 (see also 43 ). Along with this view, oscillatory coupling to F0 in our study could reflect a 

350 predictive coding mechanism comparing speech input periodicity with internal temporal 

351 expectations. Such an interpretation, although still speculative, would support the idea that 

352 high-gamma activity mediates pitch-level prediction errors and their feedforward propagation 

353 up to higher integrative areas in order to update predictions 91–93 . Cortico-acoustic coupling to 

354 F0 in (pre)motor regions furthermore agrees with their known role in predictive coding, despite 

355 this being usually observed in lower (delta and beta) frequencies 94–96 . Further cross-frequency 

356 coupling and functional connectivity analyses between auditory and motor regions are needed 

357 to fully substantiate the predictive coding interpretation and further delineate the role of speech 

358 F0 coupling at the cortical level. As speech periodicity conveys segmental information, phase-

359 locking to F0 might contribute to phoneme identification by encoding the articulatory feature

362 scene 29 and decrease sensitivity to environmental noise or competing speakers 86 .

363 Another question relates to the upper limit of F0 cortical tracking. In our study and previous 

364 work 23,27,86 , phase-locking was examined for typical male voices with F0 not exceeding 100-

365 110 Hz. It has been proposed that the auditory cortex is not able, or hardly tracks frequencies 

366 beyond 100 Hz 20 , for pure tones 97,98 or for syllables produced by males 21 or females 33,90 . Some 

367 intracranial recordings still showed phase-locked responses up to 200 Hz 19,26,99,100 . In the 

368 present study, cortical tracking of natural fast speech was significant but less widespread than 

369 for normal rate and time-compressed speech, although the three conditions highlighted a 

370 comparable cortical network. This may relate to the general decrease in cortical frequency 

371 representation towards higher frequencies and/or the greater variance in F0 for rapidly-

372 produced sentences. Part of the F0 distribution in this condition indeed lied beyond 110 Hz (up 

373 to ~120 Hz) even after the selection procedure (Figure S1, panels B and D), which may have 

374 decreased the tracking capacity of cortical oscillations. Speech F0 therefore appears as an 

375 important parameter for the emergence of cortical tracking of speech periodicity, since phase-

376 locking seems to decline with increasing F0. Alternatively, greater variability in natural fast 

377 speech F0 may have reduced statistical power, explaining the globally weaker effects in this 

378 condition. Further examining F0 tracking in different voices and under various speech rate or 

379 noisy conditions, will help better understanding this cortical contribution. 

380 As in previous work in this domain, several critical aspects need to cautiously be taken into 

381 account when interpreting our speech-brain oscillatory coupling. An important interpretive 

382 caveat concerns whether the brain-to-F0 coupling we report reflects the entrainment of 

383 endogenous cortical oscillations to the stimulus periodicity, or rather stimulus-driven evoked 

384 responses phase-locked to each glottal pulse cycle 34 . Our data do not allow us to conclusively

409 characterize the cortical encoding of F0 dynamics.
