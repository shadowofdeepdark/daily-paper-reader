Abstract 20 

Neural tracking of slow temporal modulations in speech supports extraction of 21 

prosodic and syllabic structure critical for speech comprehension, yet whether these 22 

automatic cortical tracking mechanisms are altered in children with developmental 23 

language disorder (DLD) remains unclear. We recorded MEG while children with and 24 

without DLD listened to a story, and quantified source-level lagged speech-brain 25 

coherence and frequency-specific cortical functional connectivity across bilateral 26 

cortical regions. Children with DLD showed significantly reduced coherence in the 0.9– 27 

2.5 Hz range associated with prosodic information in the story, spanning bilateral 28 

auditory and speech-related cortex. In the 2.5–5 Hz range, linked to syllabic-rate 29 

modulations in the story, group differences were right-lateralised. No reliable 30 

differences were observed at higher modulation rates (5–9 Hz or 12–40 Hz). These 31 

coherence reductions were accompanied by altered functional connectivity between 32 

cortical regions across all frequency bands, indicating disrupted large-scale 33 

coordination within speech-processing networks in DLD. 34

Introduction 36 

Studies in auditory neuroscience show that the brain transforms continuous acoustic 37 

speech input into meaningful linguistic structure by using neural mechanisms that 38 

operate across multiple temporal scales. Converging evidence from adult studies 39 

employing electroencephalography (EEG), electrocorticography (ECoG) and 40 

magnetoencephalography (MEG) demonstrates that cortical activity aligns with the 41 

temporal dynamics of the speech amplitude envelope (AE), enabling the extraction of 42 

prosodic, syllabic and segmental information through hierarchical temporal sampling 43 

(multi-time resolution models: Giraud & Poeppel, 2012; Poeppel & Assaneo, 2020). 44

Adult data suggest that delta-band oscillations (0.5–4 Hz) are preferentially coupled to 45 

slow modulations in speech, yielding information about prosody, stress patterns and 46 

phrasal boundaries (Giraud & Poeppel, 2012; Gross et al., 2013). Theta-band (4–8 47 

Hz) oscillations synchronise with syllable-rate acoustic fluctuations, facilitating the 48 

parsing of continuous speech into syllabic units (Doelling et al., 2014; Ding & Simon, 49 

2014; Luo & Poeppel, 2007). Faster oscillatory activity in gamma band is thought to 50 

encode finer-grained phonetic and acoustic detail, supporting rapid analysis within 51 

shorter temporal integration windows (Giraud & Poeppel, 2012). This multiscale 52 

alignment between neural activity and speech dynamics – often referred to as speech-53 

brain entrainment or cortical tracking – appears critical for speech perception and 54 

language processing under naturalistic listening conditions. 55 

Disruptions to these multiscale speech-brain alignment mechanisms may 56 

therefore be implicated in developmental language difficulties. Developmental 57 

Language Disorder (DLD) is a common neurodevelopmental condition, affecting 58 

approximately 7% of children, and is characterised by persistent impairments in 59 

language comprehension and production despite adequate hearing, and sufficient 60 

language exposure (Leonard, 2017). These linguistic difficulties occur in all languages, 61 

and are comprehensive. The CATALISE multi-national study of DLD identified 62 

linguistic processing problems with syntax and grammar, a limited understanding of 63 

word meanings, impairments in verbal short-term memory, and difficulties with 64 

phonology (word sound structure) as core linguistic deficits (Bishop et al., 2017). Given 65 

the wide range of linguistic difficulties, the aetiology is likely to involve impairments in 66 

fundamental aspects of acoustic processing of the speech signal. An early influential 67 

acoustic account proposed that DLD arises, at least in part, from atypical auditory-68 

temporal processing at rapid timescales >40 Hz related to phonemes. Rapid Auditory 69

Processing (RAP) theory (Tallal & Piercy, 1973; Tallal, 1980, 2004) was based on tasks 70 

using discrete nonspeech (tone) stimuli rather than continuous speech. Temporal 71 

order judgement (TOJ) and other tasks indicated that children with DLD have difficulty 72 

processing brief, rapidly successive acoustic events. This was proposed to affect 73 

perception of features such as formant transitions and voice onset time that are critical 74 

for phoneme identification and categorical speech perception. However, interventions 75 

based on RAP such as FastForWord TM have produced mixed results in randomised 76 

control trials (Gillam et al., 2008), and have not improved language skills in children 77 

with DLD. 78 

By focusing on the phoneme level, RAP may be omitting to consider a more 79 

foundational linguistic level regarding language processing. Infants are known to begin 80 

the task of acquiring a linguistic system by using speech rhythm rather than by using 81 

phonemes (Mehler et al., 1988; Nazzi et al., 1998). Recent neural data suggest infants 82 

do not begin to encode phonetic detail until later in the first year of life, and that this 83 

encoding develops quite slowly (Di Liberto et al., 2023). Accordingly, investigation of 84 

how children with DLD process the low-frequency temporal rates in the speech AE 85 

relevant to extracting prosodic structure may be useful. Indeed, recent cortical tracking 86 

studies with neurotypical infants aged 4, 7 and 11 months have shown that delta-band 87 

cortical tracking is a significant predictor of later language outcomes measured at 2 88 

years (Attaheri et al., 2024). Accordingly, a focus on the accuracy of the alignment of 89 

slow cortical oscillations <10 Hz during natural speech processing may offer a 90 

complementary framework to RAP for understanding DLD. Such a framework is 91 

offered by Temporal Sampling (TS) theory (Goswami, 2011; 2015; 2022). TS theory 92 

adopts an acoustic perspective on rhythm based on the speech AE and on the 93 

discrimination of amplitude rise times (ARTs). ARTs are rates of transition from lower 94

temporal modulations in speech in children with DLD may be informative. 105 

Although RAP and TS theories are not mutually exclusive and may reflect 106 

disruptions at different levels of temporal integration within the auditory system, to date 107 

only TS theory has been tested using natural speech (ENGLISH: Attaheri et al., 2022, 108 

2024; Power et al., 2016; Keshavarzi et al., 2022; Mandke et al., 2022; SPANISH: 109 

Molinaro et al., 2016; FRENCH: Destoky et al., 2020, 2022). Most TS studies to date 110 

are focused on explaining developmental dyslexia, a disorder of written language 111 

processing that presents with phonological (speech-sound) difficulties. As noted 112 

earlier, phonological difficulties are also a core feature of DLD (Bishop et al., 2017). 113 

The prior child dyslexia studies revealed less accurate cortical speech tracking in both 114 

the delta (English, Spanish) and theta (French) bands. In the infant cortical tracking 115 

studies, individual differences in the accuracy of delta-band tracking were predictive 116 

of both vocabulary and phonology (measured by a non-word repetition task). By 117 

contrast, infants with more theta power spectral density (PSD) during natural speech 118 

listening or with a higher ratio of theta power to delta power had worse language 119

Further, a growing body of behavioural and sensory evidence indicates 123 

substantial overlap in sensory and linguistic processing difficulties between children 124 

with dyslexia and those with DLD (Goswami, 2022). Both developmental disorders are 125 

characterised by impairments in the accurate discrimination of ARTs (for a summary 126 

of dyslexia data, see Goswami, 2015). ARTs signal rhythmic prominence in speech 127 

and both children with DLD and children with dyslexia exhibit atypical perception of 128 

syllable stress patterns in words and phrases in experimental tasks (Richards & 129 

Goswami, 2015; Cumming et al., 2015a, 2015b). Recently, it has been shown that 130 

both groups of children are also characterised by the atypical production of syllable 131 

stress patterns when copying aloud multisyllabic words (Keshavarzi et al., 2024a; 132 

Parvez et al., 2024). These convergent sensory and behavioural findings indicate 133 

possible overlapping origins in low-frequency temporal sampling mechanisms, which 134 

are investigated here in DLD. 135 

Surprisingly, direct neurophysiological evidence for atypical cortical tracking of 136 

continuous naturalistic speech by children with DLD remains limited. A recent MEG 137 

study using spoken word stimuli reported altered temporal dynamics of speech 138 

tracking in children with DLD, characterised by reduced maintenance of speech 139 

envelope representations at longer latencies relative to typically developing peers 140 

(Nora et al., 2024). Importantly, this study focused on isolated words rather than 141 

continuous speech, and interpreted the observed effects in terms of impaired retention 142 

of acoustic-phonetic information rather than deficits in early stimulus-locked encoding. 143

Accordingly, motivated by TS theory, we here recorded MEG in children with 160 

and without DLD during a story listening task and focused our analyses on a set of a161 

priori defined bilateral auditory and speech-language regions of interest (ROIs). Our 162 

selection of ROIs was based on a recent sEEG (stereoelectroenchephalography) 163 

study of cortical processing of prosody by adults (Gnanateja et al., 2025) and the adult 164 

MRI data regarding prosody reported by Sammler et al. (2015). The ROIs included 165 

Heschl’s gyrus (HG), superior temporal gyrus (STG), middle temporal gyrus (MTG), 166 

superior temporal sulcus (STS), planum temporale (PT), inferior frontal gyrus pars 167 

opercularis (IFGop) and pars triangularis (IFGtri), as well as supramarginal gyrus 168 

(SMG), angular gyrus (AG) and inferior parietal lobule (IPL). To examine cortical 169

The participating children listened to an extended spoken narrative (10 minutes) 184 

under natural listening conditions while MEG was recorded (Fig. 1a). MEG data were 185 

reconstructed at the cortical level using individual structural MRI (Fig. 1b). Source-186 

level lagged speech-brain coherence was quantified between cortical activity and the 187 

speech amplitude envelope across multiple temporal modulation ranges, as well as 188 

frequency-specific functional connectivity between cortical regions (Fig. 1c). By 189 

identifying multiple ROIs a priori , this approach enabled us to assess both local cortical 190 

tracking of speech rhythms and the large-scale coordination of distributed speech-191 

processing networks across temporal scales. 192

Syllabic-rate tracking differences are restricted to right temporal cortex 275 

In the 2.5–5 Hz band (Fig. 2b), corresponding to syllabic-rate temporal structure in our 276 

story, Wilcoxon rank-sum tests indicated reduced lagged coherence in children with 277 

DLD in several right-hemisphere ROIs, including R-STG ( z = 2.37, p = 0.018), R-MTG 278 

(z = 2.23, p = 0.026), R-STS ( z = 2.41, p = 0.016) and R-PT ( z = 2.55, p = 0.011). 279 

However, when benchmarked against the permutation-derived null model, only the 280 

reductions in R-STG, R-STS and R-PT exceeded chance-level coherence. The effect 281 

in R-MTG did not exceed the null distribution and was therefore not interpreted further. 282 

No significant effects were observed in left-hemisphere ROIs. Together, these findings 283 

indicate a right-lateralised but spatially restricted alteration of syllabic-rate speech 284 

tracking in DLD (see Fig. 4a–b). 285

No reliable group differences at higher modulation frequencies 298 

Finally, we examined the 5–9 Hz and 12–40 Hz bands, which corresponded to 299 

segmental and higher-frequency AMs in our story. Although a Wilcoxon rank-sum test 300 

identified a group difference in R-PT ( z = –2.14, p = 0.033) in the 5–9 Hz band, this 301 

effect did not exceed the permutation-derived null model and was therefore not 302 

interpreted further. As shown in Fig. 2c–d, no ROIs showed reliable group differences 303 

after benchmarking against the permutation-derived null distributions. These results 304 

indicate that neural tracking impairments in children with DLD are selective to slower 305 

temporal rates, particularly those that encode prosodic and phrasal-level structure, 306 

with weaker and more variable differences at syllabic timescales and no evidence for 307 

systematic disruptions at faster temporal modulations. 308 

Altered functional connectivity across frequency bands in children with DLD 309 

We next examined frequency-specific functional connectivity during continuous 310 

speech listening by computing the phase-locking value (PLV) between pairs of 311 

regional time series (Fig. 5a–d). In contrast to the speech–brain coherence analyses, 312 

group differences in functional connectivity were evident across all frequency bands, 313 

indicating alterations in inter-regional phase synchronisation in children with DLD that 314 

extended beyond low-frequency stimulus tracking. 315

In the 0.9–2.5 Hz band, significant group differences were observed across 16 316 

ROI pairs, forming a distributed network spanning bilateral temporal and temporo-317 

frontal regions. The most connected node was R-HG, which participated in five altered 318 

connections, indicating hub-like involvement at prosodic timescales. Additional 319 

prominent nodes included R-STG, R-STS, and L-MTG (four connections each), with 320 

further contributions from bilateral superior temporal and parietal regions. This pattern 321 

reflects a bilateral and integrative network architecture, with a right-temporal emphasis 322 

but substantial engagement of left temporal cortex, consistent with a large-scale 323 

functional difference when encoding prosodic and phrasal-level speech structure. This 324 

functional difference appears to originate in primary auditory cortex. 325 

In the 2.5–5 Hz band, group differences involved 18 ROI pairs and exhibited a 326 

more focal and hierarchical network organisation. The highest degree of involvement 327 

was observed in R-HG and R-STG, each participating in six altered connections, 328 

indicating hub-like involvement of right auditory and superior temporal cortex at 329 

syllabic rates. In addition, L-MTG emerged as a prominent secondary node with five 330 

connections, suggesting bilateral engagement of middle temporal regions. Other 331 

areas showed substantially fewer connections, resulting in a tiered network structure 332 

rather than the distributed pattern observed at the slower temporal rate (0.9–2.5 Hz). 333 

In the 5–9 Hz band, group differences were observed across 24 ROI pairs, 334 

representing the most extensive connectivity reorganisation among all frequency 335 

ranges examined. The network was characterised by multiple hub-like nodes, 336 

including R-HG, R-STG, and L-MTG (six connections each), with additional prominent 337 

involvement of R-MTG and R-STS (five connections each). Compared with lower-338 

frequency bands, altered connectivity at 5–9 Hz engaged a broader set of temporal 339 

regions and showed a pronounced right-hemisphere bias, while retaining strong 340

The present study provides source-level neurophysiological evidence that children 369 

with DLD exhibit selective impairments in the cortical tracking of slow temporal 370 

structure (<5 Hz) in naturalistic speech. No impairments were detected in cortical 371 

tracking at higher frequencies above 5 Hz. Reduced cortical tracking at lower 372 

frequencies could be expected to affect the extraction of both prosodic and syllable-373 

level information in the children’s story used as the stimulus. Reduced tracking was 374 

accompanied by frequency-dependent alterations in large-scale cortical functional 375 

connectivity which spanned both hemispheres and all frequency bands. These 376 

coherence deficits coupled with widespread atypicalities in inter-regional functional 377 

connectivity indicate that atypical speech processing in DLD reflects not only local 378 

impairments in speech-brain coupling but also altered coordination within distributed 379 

cortical speech networks. 380 

The most important finding of the present study is the reduction in speech-brain 381 

coherence in the 0.9–2.5 Hz range across bilateral auditory and speech-related cortex 382 

in children with DLD. This frequency range corresponds to the slow amplitude 383 

modulations that carry information about prosodic phrasing, intonational contours, 384 

speech rhythm and hierarchical grouping in speech (Greenberg, 2012; Ghitza & 385 

Greenberg, 2009; Giraud & Poeppel, 2012; Gross et al., 2013). This is also the 386 

frequency range most affected in children with developmental dyslexia (Goswami, 387 

2022, for review), as well as the frequency range showing the highest levels of tracking 388 

accuracy in infants aged <12 months (Attaheri et al., 2022, 2024). At least for a stress-389 

timed language like English, therefore, the efficiency of cortical tracking at ~2 Hz 390 

appears to be foundational in developing a well-functioning linguistic system. Reduced 391 

coherence in the 0.9–2.5 Hz range was observed in bilateral HG, STG, PT and IFGop, 392 

indicating that atypical processing at this timescale is not confined to early auditory 393

frequency-specific differences in functional connectivity may help to explain why 395 

children with DLD experience difficulties across all the linguistic domains of syntax, 396 

phonology and semantics (Bishop et al., 2017). In addition to these prosodic-rate 397 

effects, group differences were also observed at the rates denoting syllabic information 398 

in the story (2.5–5 Hz). Interestingly, these differences were more spatially restricted 399 

and were largely confined to right-hemisphere temporal regions. In the 2.5–5 Hz band, 400 

reduced coherence in children with DLD was evident primarily in R-STG, R-STS and 401 

R-PT, consistent with some of the prior fMRI data indicating right-hemisphere neural 402 

processing atypicalities (Parker et al., 2022). 403 

Together, these findings indicate a graded impairment in low-frequency 404 

speech-brain alignment in DLD, with the strongest and most spatially extensive effects 405 

found at prosodic timescales and weaker, right-lateralised effects found at syllabic 406 

rates. These findings provide direct neurophysiological support for the TS framework, 407 

which proposes that atypical language development arises from impaired cortical 408 

encoding of the slow temporal modulations in speech that support both prosodic and 409 

syllabic structure (Goswami, 2011, 2015, 2022). The graded nature of these effects – 410 

widespread and bilateral at prosodic rates but right-lateralised at syllabic rates – 411 

suggests that TS impairments in DLD are not limited to a single temporal scale but 412 

span multiple levels of the speech rhythm hierarchy. The right-hemisphere 413 

predominance of the syllabic-rate effects observed in the 2.5–5 Hz band are also 414 

consistent with the hemispheric asymmetries in temporal integration proposed by the 415 

Asymmetric Sampling in Time (AST) hypothesis (Poeppel, 2003; Boemio et al., 2005), 416 

by which slower-rate temporal information is preferentially processed by the right 417 

hemisphere. However, AST did not consider rates slower than the syllabic rate. 418

Poeppel, 2012). On a developmental analysis, this may not be surprising, as 422 

behavioural studies suggest that children’s knowledge about segmental units 423 

develops relatively slowly and is related to learning to read (Ziegler & Goswami, 2005). 424 

In the current study, faster cortical tracking rates did not differentiate children with DLD 425 

from control children. Regarding RAP theory, these findings suggest that neural 426 

tracking of gamma-band acoustic fluctuations (>40 Hz, the frequency rate 427 

foregrounded by RAP theory as relevant for phonemic knowledge) is not impaired in 428 

children with DLD. Neural tracking of more rapid speech information appears largely 429 

preserved during naturalistic speech listening, at least on the basis of the methods 430 

used in our study. This result requires replication, but may indicate that the origins of 431 

DLD lie not with the phoneme but rather with the syllabic rhythm patterns that comprise 432 

prosody. 433 

In contrast to the speech-brain coherence results, functional connectivity 434 

analyses revealed group differences across all frequency bands. This finding is 435 

consistent with the elevated EEG band-power that appears to characterise the DLD 436 

brain when listening to repetition of the syllable “ba” (Keshavarzi et al., 2024b), and 437 

implies widespread alterations in inter-regional phase synchronisation. With respect 438 

to the slowest temporal rate (0.9–2.5 Hz), children with DLD showed reduced 439 

functional connectivity across a distributed bilateral network encompassing auditory 440 

and temporal regions, with additional involvement of temporo-parietal and inferior 441 

frontal cortex. Connectivity differences were evident in both hemispheres and included 442 

a mixture of intra-hemispheric and inter-hemispheric connections, consistent with 443

cortical systems is inefficient in the DLD brain. 452 

At syllabic rates (2.5–5 Hz), functional connectivity differences were more focal 453 

and hierarchically organised than at the slowest timescale. Altered connectivity was 454 

dominated by right auditory and superior temporal cortex, with R-HG and R-STG 455 

emerging as the most connected nodes and L-MTG acting as a prominent secondary 456 

contributor. Compared with the distributed pattern observed at 0.9–2.5 Hz, this tiered 457 

organisation suggests a sharpening of network disruption at syllabic timescales, 458 

centred on regions critical for processing speech rhythm and timing. The close 459 

correspondence between these network-level alterations and the right-lateralised 460 

coherence differences observed at 2.5–5 Hz supports the interpretation that atypical 461 

syllabic-rate processing in DLD reflects both reduced local neural alignment and 462 

weakened inter-regional coordination within temporally specialised speech networks. 463

frequencies (5–9 Hz and 12–40 Hz) despite the absence of corresponding group 465 

differences in speech–brain coherence. At these frequencies, connectivity alterations 466

faster speech modulations does not necessarily entail efficient large-scale 470 

coordination across cortical networks. One implication is that, while neural encoding 471 

of segmental and high-frequency acoustic information may be largely intact in DLD, 472 

the integration of these representations across distributed cortical systems remains 473 

atypical. Such a decoupling between local stimulus tracking and inter-regional 474 

synchronisation accords with frameworks emphasising communication through 475 

coherence (Varela et al., 2001; Fries, 2005, 2015), in which phase alignment across 476 

cortical areas regulates the efficiency and stability of information transfer during 477 

complex cognitive tasks, including naturalistic speech processing. The atypical 478 

network-level organisation demonstrated here may contribute to variability in language 479 

outcomes and help to explain why difficulties in speech comprehension and language 480 

use persist in DLD despite relatively intact performance on some basic auditory 481 

psychophysical measures with tones (e.g., backward masking and frequency-482 

modulation detection) in quiet listening conditions (Bishop et al., 1999). 483 

In summary, here we show that DLD is associated with selective impairments 484 

in slow-timescale cortical speech tracking and frequency-dependent disruptions of 485 

cortical functional connectivity during continuous speech listening. By linking reduced 486 

prosodic- and syllabic-rate neural alignment to altered large-scale network 487 

coordination – particularly within right-hemisphere temporal and temporo-frontal 488 

systems – the findings provide converging mechanistic support for some prior fMRI 489 

data (Helenius et al., 2014, 2009; Asaridou & Watkins, 2022). More broadly, the results 490 

highlight slow temporal sampling as a feature of DLD, supporting TS theory, and 491 

identifying novel neurophysiological targets for future interventions. 492

Materials and Methods 494 

Participants 495 

Twenty-eight children took part in the study, including 14 typically developing control 496 

children (mean age = 9.31 years, S.D. = 0.47) and 14 children with DLD (mean age = 497 

9.60 years, S.D. = 1.02). All participants were native English speakers with no history 498 

of neurological disorders. All participants were taking part in an ongoing study of 499 

auditory processing in DLD, and those children with suspected language difficulties 500 

were nominated by the special educational needs teachers in their schools. Children 501 

in the TD group were nominated by classroom teachers as being typically-developing. 502 

All participants exhibited normal hearing when tested with an audiometer. In a short 503 

hearing test across the frequency range 0.25 – 8 kHz (0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8 kHz), all 504 

children were sensitive to sounds within the 20 dB HL range. Language status was 505 

ascertained by a trained speech and language therapist, who administered two 506 

subtests of CELF-V (Wiig et al., 2017) to all the participants: recalling sentences and 507 

formulating sentences. Those children who appeared to have language difficulties 508 

then received (depending on age) two further CELF subtests drawn from word 509 

structure, sentence comprehension, word classes and semantic relationships. 510 

Children who scored at least 1 S.D. below the mean (7 or less when the mean score 511 

is 10) on at least 2 of these 4 subtests were included in the DLD group. MRI scans 512 

were available for all individuals except five participants, for whom the FreeSurfer 513 

fsaverage template was used during source modelling. All participants and their 514 

parents provided informed consent to take part in the study, in accordance with the 515 

Declaration of Helsinki. The study was reviewed by the University of Cambridge, 516 

Psychology Research Ethics Committee and received a favourable opinion. 517

MEG Data Acquisition 525 

MEG data were acquired using a 306-channel Elekta Neuromag system (102 526 

magnetometers and 204 planar gradiometers) in a magnetically shielded room. 527 

Signals were sampled at 1000 Hz with an online band-pass filter (0.1–330 Hz). Head 528 

position indicator (HPI) coils were used to track head position, and standard fiducials 529 

(nasion and left/right preauricular points) and scalp points were digitised for co-530 

registration. Two-minute empty-room recordings were acquired for noise covariance 531 

estimation using the same acquisition parameters as the task recordings. To ensure 532 

attention to the narrative, children were informed prior to testing that they would 533 

answer simple comprehension questions following each story section. 534 

Comprehension was assessed using four questions in total (score range: 0–4). Mean 535 

scores (± S.D.) were 3.64 ± 0.50 for the TD group and 3.57 ± 0.65 for the DLD group. 536 

A Wilcoxon rank-sum test revealed no significant group difference ( z = 0.11, p = 0.91), 537 

indicating comparable comprehension performance across groups. 538

Stimulus envelope calculation 570 

Speech stimuli consisted of ten ~1-minute segments from a narrated “Iron Man” story. 571 

For analysis, speech stimuli were converted to 64-bit floating-point format and 572 

concatenated to form a single continuous waveform sampled at 44.1 kHz. We first 573 

computed a broadband amplitude envelope by taking the magnitude of the analytic 574 

signal obtained via the Hilbert transform. The resulting envelope was normalised by 575 

its maximum value. To align the speech envelope with the MEG data and reduce 576 

computational load, the envelope was then resampled to 200 Hz using Fourier-based 577 

resampling, which includes appropriate anti-alias filtering. This envelope was 578 

subsequently band-pass filtered into four modulation-frequency ranges using a fourth-579 

order, zero-phase Butterworth filter: 0.9–2.5 Hz, 2.5–5 Hz, 5–9 Hz and 12–40 Hz. 580 

These ranges were chosen to probe distinct temporal scales of speech information 581 

determined by prior computational modelling of the story content (Mandke et al., 582 

2022): prosodic and phrase-level structure (0.9–2.5 Hz), syllabic timing (2.5–5 Hz), 583 

onset/segmental dynamics (5–9 Hz) and higher-frequency amplitude modulations 584 

(12–40 Hz). The resulting band-limited envelopes were stored and used as the speech 585 

regressors for all subsequent lagged coherence analyses. 586
