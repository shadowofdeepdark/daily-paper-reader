13 A central question in visual word recognition concerns whether orthographic and phonological 14 codes are coordinated sequentially or in parallel during lexical access. Korean Hangul, an alpha-15 syllabic writing system with morphophonemic spelling principles, allows independent 16 manipulation of orthographic and phonological syllable overlap. In a masked priming lexical 17 decision task with EEG ( N = 30), we contrasted orthographically identical primes (e.g., 식-식량 ), 18 phonologically overlapping primes (e.g., 싱-식량 ), and unrelated primes. Event-related 19 potentials and time-frequency representations (theta: 4–8 Hz, lower beta: 13–20 Hz, upper beta: 20 20–30 Hz) were analyzed to capture both evoked and oscillatory neural dynamics. Orthographic 21 priming produced a cascade of facilitative effects: early fronto-central P200 enhancement (150– 22 250 ms) with upper beta synchronization (30–290 ms), followed by centro-parietal N400 23 reduction (350–550 ms) with frontal theta suppression (400–730 ms), and behavioral facilitation. 24 Phonological priming, by contrast, elicited an inhibitory behavioral effect and sustained lower 25 beta activity over central regions (310–590 ms) but produced no early electrophysiological 26 modulation, consistent with lexical competition. This spatiotemporal dissociation suggests that 27 orthographic syllable processing can emerge at early stages and cascades into later lexical-level 28 processing, whereas phonological syllable effects are confined to later processing stages. These 29 findings provide support for a sequential or cascaded account of orthographic-phonological 30 coordination, as predicted by dual-route models, while challenging strong forms of parallel 31 activation, and suggest that the alpha-syllabic structure of Korean may enable a processing 32 strategy in which orthographic parsing serves as an efficient entry route to the lexicon. 33

1. Introduction 34 Understanding how orthographic and phonological codes are coordinated during lexical access 35 has been a long-standing question in visual word recognition research. Dual-route models such 36 as the Dual Route Cascaded (DRC) model posit distinct processing pathways—a direct 37 orthographic route that maps visual input onto whole-word representations and an indirect 38 phonological route that assembles phonological codes prior to lexical access (Coltheart et al., 39 2001; Perry et al., 2007)—predicting that orthographic analysis precedes phonological assembly. 40 Interactive activation frameworks such as the Bi-Modal Interactive Activation Model (BIAM; 41 Diependaele et al., 2010; Grainger & Holcomb, 2009), by contrast, propose that orthographic and 42 phonological codes are activated rapidly and in parallel, building on the foundational Interactive 43 Activation Model (IAM; McClelland & Rumelhart, 1981). Sublexical units occupy a central role 44 in this debate, as they mediate between orthographic input and lexical access and represent the 45 earliest processing level at which the two codes interact. Two related questions have motivated 46 much of this research: whether sublexical effects reflect orthographic or phonological 47 representations, and whether they originate at early form-encoding stages or at later lexical 48 stages during competitive activation. However, since alphabetic writing systems inherently 49 confound orthographic and phonological syllable structure, independent manipulation of the two 50 has remained elusive, leaving both questions yet to be resolved. 51 52

Phonological variables, by contrast, tend to produce 59 inhibitory effects: phonological neighborhood density slows lexical decisions as the number of 60 phonologically similar words increases, consistent with lexical competition driven by 61 phonological activation (Grainger et al., 2005; Luce & Pisoni, 1998). 62 The representational distinction intersects with a longstanding debate about the role of 63 phonological codes in visual word recognition. According to dual-route and cascaded 64 architectures (e.g., Coltheart et al., 2001), orthographic form analysis provides a fast, direct 65 pathway to the lexicon, with phonological assembly proceeding through a slower indirect route. 66 An alternative class of accounts maintains that phonological codes are computed rapidly and 67 obligatorily, serving a functional role in lexical access regardless of task or orthographic system 68 (Brysbaert, 2001; Lukatela et al., 1998, 2001). Evidence from masked priming studies has been 69 equivocal: early demonstrations of phonological effects in French (Ferrand & Grainger, 1992, 70 1993) were not consistently replicated in deeper orthographies such as English (see Rastle & 71 Brysbaert, 2006, for review). However, subsequent work has shown that null results reflected 72 inadequate control of orthographic neighborhood competition rather than a genuine absence of 73 early phonological activation (Davis & Lupker, 2006; Rastle & Brysbaert, 2006). The debate has 74 accordingly shifted from whether phonological codes are activated early to the conditions under 75 which such activation becomes detectable, a question that remains open. 76 Within this broader debate, the syllable is of particular interest as a sublexical unit that 77 straddles both orthographic and phonological dimensions. 1.3. Previous Korean Syllable Processing Research 126 A central debate in Korean visual word recognition concerns whether phonological information 127 is automatically activated or whether recognition proceeds primarily through orthographic 128 processing. Orthographic syllables have emerged as robust processing units across paradigms. 129 Park (1996) found no facilitation from pseudohomophone primes relative to orthographic 130 controls, suggesting that phonological information is not automatically activated. Bae and Yi 131 (2010) compared orthographically identical first-syllable primes (숙소-숙녀 ) with 132 phonologically identical primes ( 숭배 -숙녀 ) and found facilitation only for orthographic overlap. 133 Similarly, Tae et al. (2015) manipulated prime duration (60 ms, 150 ms) and found significant 134 priming effects only for orthographically matched primes. This orthographic dominance 135 extended to cross-modal priming with auditory primes (Tae et al., 2017), and a recent meta-136 analysis further confirmed the dominant role of orthographic syllables across paradigms (Lim et 137 al., 2022). 138 However, the role of phonological syllables remains contested. Some studies reported 139 inhibitory effects of phonological syllables, consistent with lexical competition driven by 140 syllable frequency (Choi et al., 2015; Y. Kwon, 2012; Y. Kwon et al., 2011; Y. Kwon & Lee, 141 2017). Other studies found null effects (Jin et al., 2018; Y. Kwon & Lee, 2015), and more recent 142 work has reported facilitatory effects, particularly in morphologically complex words, attributed 143 to a fast-guess mechanism (Kim et al., 2023; S. Kwon et al., 2023; S. Lee et al., 2023). 144

1.4. ERP Evidence: Processing Stage and Temporal Dynamics 160 Event-related potentials provide the temporal resolution necessary for investigating whether 161 sublexical effects arise at early form-encoding or later lexical processing stages. The P200 162 component (150–250 ms), characterized by a fronto-central scalp distribution, has been 163 interpreted as an index of early sublexical form encoding and has been associated with early 164 orthographic–phonological processing, where high-frequency syllables consistently produce 165 reduced P200 amplitudes, interpreted as reflecting more efficient sublexical activation (Barber et 166 al., 2004; Hutzler et al., 2004). The N400 component (300–500 ms), with its characteristic 167

This P200-170 facilitation/N400-competition dissociation has been interpreted in terms of a distinction between 171 early sublexical form encoding and later lexical processing. However, several studies have 172 reported SFEs across both time windows simultaneously (Chetail et al., 2012; Goslin et al., 173 2006), leaving the neural locus unresolved in alphabetic languages. 174 Korean ERP studies have paralleled these findings while providing additional leverage 175 through the orthographic-phonological dissociation inherent in the writing system. Phonological 176 syllable frequency modulated P200 amplitudes (150–250 ms) in pseudoword recognition, with 177 high-frequency phonological syllables producing greater negativity, suggesting early inhibitory 178 activation (Y. Kwon et al., 2011). The N400 (300–500 ms) was primarily influenced by syllable 179 neighborhood density, with denser neighborhoods producing enhanced negativity reflecting 180 lexical competition (Y. This dissociation was further supported by masked 183 priming evidence (Y. Together, Korean ERP 186 evidence suggests an early orthographically driven advantage alongside a delayed phonological 187 competition effect, providing support for a temporal dissociation consistent with distinct 188 processing stages for the two syllable types, though one that warrants further examination with 189 respect to the temporal locus of these effects. 190

1.5. Time-Frequency Analysis: Beyond ERPs 192 While ERPs reveal the temporal dynamics of word recognition at discrete latency windows, 193 time-frequency analysis exposes oscillatory mechanisms that are not visible in conventional ERP 194 averaging. Distinct frequency bands have been linked to specific cognitive operations: theta 195 oscillations (4–8 Hz) have been associated with lexical retrieval and semantic integration 196 (Bastiaansen et al., 2005, 2008), and beta oscillations (13–30 Hz) have been implicated in top-197 down prediction and syntactic integration (Lewis & Bastiaansen, 2015; Weiss & Mueller, 2012). 198 Beta oscillations can be further decomposed into lower beta (13–20 Hz), associated with 199 sustained cognitive processing (Kilavik et al., 2013; Pfurtscheller & Lopes da Silva, 1999), and 200 upper beta (20–30 Hz), reflecting faster coordination dynamics during perceptual and 201 representational processing (Engel & Fries, 2010). 202 Crucially, oscillatory dynamics reflect the coordination and updating of distributed neural 203 representations rather than facilitation per se, providing access to computational processes that 204 are orthogonal to amplitude-based ERP measures (Lewis et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2012). Given 205 that orthographic facilitation and phonological competition are hypothesized to rely on distinct 206 computational mechanisms—rapid perceptual grouping versus sustained assembly and conflict 207 resolution—oscillatory dynamics may offer diagnostic signatures capable of dissociating these 208 processes beyond what ERPs alone can reveal. 209 210

2.2. Procedure 263 A visual masked priming lexical decision task was used (see Figure 1 ). First, a fixation point 264 (“+”) appeared at the center of the screen for 500 ms, followed by a forward mask (“####”) 265 presented at the same location for 500 ms. After the mask, a prime stimulus was briefly 266 presented for 50 ms, and the target stimulus appeared immediately after prime offset. During 267 target presentation, participants were instructed to decide whether the visual stimulus was a real 268 word or not by pressing a designated response key. Participants were instructed to minimize eye 269 blinks during stimulus presentation and response execution. After the target presentation, an 270 inter-trial interval (ITI) was presented for 1000, 1100, or 1200 ms, during which a visual cue 271 (>__<) was displayed, instructing participants to blink if needed. Each participant completed 24 272 practice trials and proceeded to the main experiment only after achieving an accuracy of at least 273 70%. All visual stimuli were presented in white Malgun Gothic font (size 40) on a black 274 background and were displayed on a 24-inch LG monitor, with responses collected using a Serial 275 Response Box, controlled using E-Prime 3.0 Professional (Psychology Software Tools, 276 Pittsburgh, PA, USA). 277 278

4. Discussion 591 The present study employed masked priming with lexical decision and high-density EEG to 592 simultaneously address two questions regarding sublexical processing in Korean visual word 593 recognition: (1) whether syllabic priming effects reflect orthographic or phonological syllable 594 overlap, and (2) whether these effects originate at early form-encoding stages or at later lexical 595 stages during competitive activation. Exploiting Korean alpha-syllabic structure, in which 596 morphophonemic spelling principles allow systematic dissociation of orthographic from 597 phonological syllable representations, we contrasted orthographically identical and 598 phonologically overlapping primes while recording both event-related potentials and time-599 frequency representations. Our findings revealed a clear spatiotemporal dissociation: 600 orthographic priming produced early fronto-central P200 enhancement (150–250 ms) 601

The early ERP effects observed here could therefore reflect, at least in part, 618 low-level visual repetition rather than purely abstract orthographic processing. 4.5. Limitations and Future Directions 878 Several limitations warrant consideration. First, our masked priming design with a brief 50-ms 879 prime duration may have preferentially engaged orthographic processing due to limited time for 880 phonological assembly. Future studies employing longer prime durations or prime awareness 881 manipulations could assess whether phonological effects emerge more robustly under conditions 882 that afford greater processing time, as suggested by the SOA-dependent facilitation observed in 883 French by Ferrand et al. (1996). Notably, Hangul's orthographic blocks provide immediate 884 syllabic form information, and a brief 50-ms prime may be sufficient to saturate orthographic 885 facilitation while leaving phonological assembly to later stages. Future studies employing 886 systematically varied prime durations (e.g., 50, 100, 150 ms) would help determine the temporal 887 threshold at which phonological priming effects become detectable in Korean. Second, the 888 present study employed visual-visual masked priming exclusively, leaving open the question of 889 whether the observed orthographic facilitation and phonological inhibition pattern generalizes 890 across input modalities. Tae et al. (2017) demonstrated that orthographic priming effects 891 persisted even with auditory primes in a cross-modal paradigm, suggesting that orthographic 892 representations may be activated regardless of input modality. Future studies employing 893 auditory-visual and auditory-auditory priming conditions with concurrent EEG recording could 894 determine whether the temporal dissociation between orthographic and phonological processing 895 observed here reflects a modality-general property of Korean syllable representations or is 896 specific to the visual input channel.
