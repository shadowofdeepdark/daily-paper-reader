Abstract 1

2A central question in psycholinguistics in visual word recognition is whether morphologically 3complex words are obligatorily decomposed into stems and affixes during visual word 4recognition or whether whole ‐word access can occur when forms are frequent and familiar. The 5present study investigated how morphological complexity and lexical frequency jointly shape 6neural responses by leveraging Korean nominal inflection, whose transparent stem–suffix 7structure permits a clean dissociation between base (stem) frequency and surface (whole ‐word) 8frequency. Twenty-five native Korean speakers completed a rapid event-related fMRI lexical 9decision task involving simple and inflected nouns that varied parametrically in both frequency 10 measures. Representational similarity analysis (RSA) revealed robust encoding of surface 11 frequency—but not base frequency—in the inferior frontal gyrus (IFG) pars opercularis and 12 supramarginal gyrus (SMG), with significantly stronger correlations for inflected than simple 13 nouns. Univariate analyses converged with this result: surface frequency selectively increased 14 activation for inflected nouns in inferior parietal regions, whereas base frequency showed no 15 reliable effects in any ROI. These findings challenge models positing obligatory pre-lexical 16 decomposition, instead supporting accounts in which morphological processing is shaped by 17 post-lexical, usage-driven lexical statistics. Taken together, our findings shed light on a 18 distributed perspective on morphological processing, suggesting that structural and statistical 19 factors jointly constrain access to morphologically complex forms. 20 21

INTRODUCTION 1Human language comprehension requires efficient recognition of morphologically complex 2words. A central question in psycholinguistics in visual word recognition is whether the 3cognitive system obligatorily decomposes these words into morphemes before meaning access or 4whether whole-word representations can be accessed directly for familiar forms. 5Evidence for obligatory decomposition comes primarily from masked-priming studies, 6where briefly presented primes like hunter facilitate recognition of hunt , even when semantically 7opaque (e.g., corner–corn ) relative to orthographic controls (Longtin et al., 2003; Rastle et al., 82004). Such priming, with prime durations as brief as ~50 ms, suggests rapid morpho-9orthographic segmentation that precedes semantics. ERP and MEG work corroborates this view, 10 showing early negativities before semantic divergence (Lavric et al., 2011; Lewis et al., 2011). It 11 is important to note that the empirical and theoretical frameworks reviewed here pertain 12 specifically to visual word recognition, where orthographic structure provides the primary cues 13 to morphological segmentation. Phonological processing can exhibit different morphological 14 dynamics, particularly in languages with rich morphophonology, and therefore lies outside the 15 scope of the present investigation. 16 In contrast, distributional perspectives argue that processing depends on lexical statistics, 17 transparency, and predictability (Baayen et al., 2011; Bybee, 1995; Milin et al., 2017). High-18 frequency or predictable forms may be accessed holistically, whereas rare or novel forms are 19 decomposed. In lexical decision and naming tasks, surface frequency, the token frequency of the 20 whole form, often better predicts recognition than base frequency, the cumulative frequency of 21 words sharing a stem (Bertram et al., 2000; Kuperman et al., 2009). Morphological priming 22 effects are likewise modulated by transparency and familiarity (Feldman et al., 2015; Jared et al., 23

Together, these 2findings suggest morphological processing may reflect both automatic segmentation and flexible, 3frequency-driven representations. 4Another critical distinction in morphological processing concerns the timing of 5decomposition relative to lexical access. Pre-lexical decomposition models propose that complex 6words are obligatorily parsed into constituent morphemes before whole-word meaning is 7accessed (Rastle et al., 2004; Taft & Forster, 1975). Under this view, recognition of hunter 8necessarily involves early access to hunt + -er , regardless of whole-word frequency. In contrast ,9post-lexical decomposition accounts propose that morphological structure is analyzed after initial 10 whole-word recognition (Giraudo & Grainger, 2000, 2001). Here, frequent derived forms like 11 

hunter may be accessed directly as whole words, with morphological decomposition occurring 12 later for semantic integration or production. Critically, these accounts make different predictions 13 about which frequency measure should modulate neural responses: pre-lexical models predict 14 that base (stem) frequency should drive activation patterns because stems are accessed first, 15 whereas post-lexical models predict that surface (whole-word) frequency should dominate 16 because initial access relies on whole-word representations. Notably, surface frequency itself can 17 be interpreted differently across frameworks: in pre-lexical models, it reflects the frequency of 18 recombining morphemes, whereas in post-lexical models, it indexes the frequency of stored 19 whole-word forms. The present study leverages fMRI to test these competing predictions by 20 examining whether sustained neural activation patterns are better explained by surface or base 21 frequency. 22

5Neuroimaging evidence has shown mixed results regarding whether morphological 1processing reflects categorical decomposition or graded statistical effects. fMRI studies report 2that complex forms engage a left-lateralized network including the inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), 3inferior parietal lobule (IPL), and ventral occipitotemporal cortex. Some findings have been 4interpreted as supporting categorical decomposition, which showed selective IFG activations to 5inflected or transparent derivations in English and Finnish (Bozic et al., 2007; Lehtonen et al., 62006), and distinct activation patterns for regular versus irregular inflections in the left 7frontotemporal network in German and English (Beretta et al., 2003; Tyler et al., 2004). 8However, other work suggests that these effects reflect graded sensitivity to phonological 9complexity, semantic factors, and lexical statistics rather than a unique decomposition stage 10 (Devlin et al., 2004; Joanisse & Seidenberg, 2005). When phonological complexity is controlled, 11 categorical differences between regular and irregular forms often disappear (Desai et al., 2006), 12 and when differences do emerge, they are modulated by semantic variables such as semantic 13 transparency (Bozic et al., 2013), suggesting continuous rather than discrete processing 14 mechanisms. While much of this work has focused on the regular versus irregular distinction in 15 verb inflection, a complementary question remains: at what level—whole-word or stem—does 16 the brain encode lexical frequency during morphological processing? 17 Findings on word frequency also remain inconsistent, with some studies reporting 18 stronger activation for low-frequency words (Fiebach et al., 2002; Fiez et al., 1999), and others 19 showing divergent patterns depending on task and subregion (Carreiras et al., 2009; Graves et al., 20 2010). Critically, few studies have manipulated base frequency directly, and most report limited 21 independent effects once surface frequency is controlled (Lehtonen et al., 2006; Pliatsikas et al., 22

Its agglutinative 2morphology yields transparent stem–suffix structure, stems remain invariant across inflections, 3and orthographic conventions (i.e., spacing and syllable blocking) clearly indicate morphemic 4boundaries, all of which permit precise frequency estimation. Crucially, inflected forms exhibit a 5natural dissociation between surface frequency (i.e., the frequency of the whole form) and base 6frequency (i.e., the frequency of the bare stem). For example, the stem 까닭  /kkadak/ "reason" 7can combine with different particles (e.g., 까닭은 , 까닭이 , 까닭을 ); each inflected form has its 8own surface frequency, but they all share the same base frequency — the cumulative count of the 9stem 까닭 . If morphological parsing occurs at the pre-15 lexical stage, then neural activation patterns should be more strongly explained by base 16 frequency, which indexes the frequency of accessing the stem independent of surface form. In 17 contrast, if inflected nouns are accessed primarily as whole-word units, consistent with post-18 lexical accounts, then surface frequency should provide a better account of both amplitude-based 19 responses and the representational geometry captured by RSA. The present study tests whether 20 neural patterns align more closely with stem-driven decomposition or whole-word-driven 21 statistical access by directly contrasting surface and base frequency in Korean nominal inflection. 22

METHODS 2

Participants 3Twenty-five healthy adults (15 female; age: 26 ± 3.83 years) recruited from Korea University 4participated in the experiment. They were all native Korean speakers, right-handed (confirmed 5by the scores on the Edinburgh Handedness Test (Oldfield, 1971): M = 7.76, SD = 0.28) and had 6normal or corrected-to-normal vision. Participants were informed and provided written consent 7prior to the experiment and were monetarily compensated for their participation. One participant 8withdrew before completing the experiment, and two others were excluded due to poor 9behavioral performance (accuracy < 60%). One additional participant completed the experiment 10 with adequate behavioral performance, but their fMRI data could not be analyzed due to file 11 corruption resulting from hardware failure. Behavioral data from this participant were retained. 12 Final sample sizes were N = 22 (11 female; age: 26.3 ± 3.97 years) for behavioral analyses and N 13 = 21 (11 female; age: 26.4 ± 4.07 years) for all neuroimaging analyses. The sample size was 14 determined based on prior fMRI studies using similar multivariate and univariate ROI-based 15 approaches in the language domain (Bozic et al., 2007; Carota et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2024), 16 which typically report medium-to-large effects in comparable tasks with sample sizes ranging 17 from 16 to 24 participants. Given the within-subjects design and the use of trial-wise beta 18 estimation, this sample size affords adequate power to detect region-level effects of morphology 19 and lexical frequency using both traditional and multivariate analyses. The study was conducted 20 in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and approved by the Institutional Review Board 21 of Korea University (protocol code KUIRB-2021-0427-01, 21 December 2021). 22 23

Stimuli 1A total of 120 Korean nouns were selected from the Korean Sejong corpus (Kang & Kim, 2009), 2which contains approximately 15 million words. Korean is an agglutinative language with a 3transparent morphological system, in which grammatical relations are marked by attaching case 4particles or topic markers to noun stems. Furthermore, the Korean writing system (Hangul) 5encodes syllables both orthographically and phonologically, enabling precise control of 6sublexical units. These features make Korean particularly well-suited for isolating effects of 7morphological complexity, stem-level access, and syllable-level frequency during visual word 8recognition. 9Morphological complexity was orthogonally manipulated such that half of the stimuli were 10 morphologically simple nouns, and the other half were inflected nouns. Each condition included 11 an equal number of bi-syllabic and tri-syllabic words, ensuring that word length was fully 12 balanced across conditions. Simple nouns consisted of bi- or tri-syllabic stems, while inflected 13 nouns were composed of mono- or bi-syllabic stems followed by a monosyllabic inflectional 14 suffix (e.g., case or topic marker). For example, 숭어  (trout) and 뱀은  (snake + TOPIC) illustrate 15 the contrast between simple and inflected forms, with 옥수수  (corn) and 까닭은  (reason + 16 TOPIC) showing the contrast between a tri-syllabic simple noun and a tri-syllabic inflected noun 17 consisting of a bi-syllabic stem ( 까닭 ) with the topic particle -은 . Whole-brain analysis 2All neuroimaging analyses were conducted on 21 participants who met behavioral criteria and 3had valid fMRI data (see Participants section for exclusion criteria). First-level analyses modeled 4four contrasts for each participant: Inflected > Simple, Inflected > Baseline, Simple > Baseline, 5and Word > Baseline. The Inflected and Simple contrasts included only real word trials from 6their respective morphological conditions. The Word > Baseline contrast collapsed across all real 7word trials (both Inflected and Simple conditions), excluding pseudowords. Events were 8modeled with durations of 2 s and convolved with the canonical hemodynamic response 9function (HRF), and six motion parameters were included as nuisance regressors. At the second 10 level, one-sample t-tests were performed on first-level contrast images. DISCUSSION 1The present study investigated how morphological complexity and lexical frequency jointly 2shape neural responses during visual word recognition, focusing on the dissociation between 3surface (whole-word) frequency and base (stem) frequency in Korean nominal inflection. Korean 4provides an interesting test case because its transparent agglutinative structure allows stem-based 5and surface-based statistics to be cleanly separated. By combining univariate mixed-effects 6modeling with representational similarity analysis (RSA) across six left-hemisphere language 7regions, we tested competing predictions of pre-lexical decomposition and post-lexical, whole-8form access accounts. Three main findings were demonstrated. First, RSA revealed robust 9representational encoding of surface frequency in IFG pars opercularis (IFGoper) and 10 supramarginal gyrus (SMG), with encoding significantly stronger for inflected than simple nouns. 11 Second, base frequency did not show positive encoding in any ROI; instead, simple nouns 12 produced negative RSA correlations with the base-frequency RDM in IFGoper and SMG. Third, 13 univariate analyses in the inferior parietal lobule (IPL) revealed significant interactions of 14 morphology and surface frequency, with activation increasing with surface frequency for 15 inflected but not simple nouns. Collectively, these findings indicate that sustained neural 16 activation patterns primarily reflect whole-form lexical statistics rather than stem-based 17 morphological structure. 18 These results are in line with prior work in both Indo-European and Korean languages, 19 while extending this literature by comparing surface- and stem-based frequency within a single 20 inflectional system. Consistent with masked-priming and ERP evidence showing that high-21 frequency complex forms can be accessed more holistically (Kim et al., 2022; Lehtonen et al., 22 2007), our RSA and univariate findings demonstrate that surface frequency reliably modulates 23

Behavioral processing advantages for high-1frequency inflected forms have been widely documented in lexical decision and naming tasks 2(Bertram et al., 2000; Kuperman et al., 2009), and our data converge with these findings by 3showing that surface frequency, but not stem frequency, shapes neural similarity and activation 4magnitude. 5Neuroimaging studies have similarly implicated graded lexical-semantic and statistical 6influences on morphological processing. In languages such as English, Hebrew, Finnish, and 7Italian, fMRI work has shown that putative morphological effects often reduce to or interact with 8frequency, predictability, or semantic transparency (Bozic et al., 2013; Desai et al., 2006; Devlin 9et al., 2004). Our results extend this observation to Korean nominal inflection; despite its 10 transparent morphological structure, the neural similarity in IFGoper and SMG was more 11 strongly associated with whole-word frequency than with stem-based factors. 12 These findings refine our understanding of Korean morphological processing and situate 13 it within broader cross-linguistic patterns. Prior fMRI research on Korean verb inflection has 14 consistently shown overlapping activation for regular and irregular verbs in left IFG and 15 temporal regions (Kim et al., 2024; Yim et al., 2006), suggesting that the processing of Korean 16 inflectional morphology may not solely depend on categorical dichotomy. At the same time, 17 these studies observed graded modulations by semantic homonymy and morphological 18 complexity in occipitotemporal and angular gyrus regions (Kim et al., 2024), indicating 19 sensitivity to experience-based properties. Our findings parallel this pattern in showing that 20 although morphological complexity (simple vs. inflected) modulates representational strength, 21 the representational structure was shaped primarily by surface-level lexical statistics. 22

31 The behavioral results complement the neural findings, further specifying how lexical 1statistics shape recognition of inflected forms. Both surface and base frequency facilitated lexical 2decisions, and neither interacted significantly with morphological complexity. This aligns with 3large lexical-decision megastudies showing that whole-word frequency is the primary predictor 4of behavioral performance (Keuleers et al., 2011; Yap & Balota, 2009; Yi et al., 2017). 5Importantly, the absence of a morphology × frequency interaction behaviorally parallels the 6pattern in the fusiform and MTG ROIs, which showed no reliable modulation by frequency. 7The neural data, however, reveal distinctions that remain behaviorally silent. Whereas 8both frequency measures facilitate decision speed, only surface frequency shapes 9representational similarity in IFGoper and SMG, and only surface frequency interacts with 10 morphology in parietal univariate activation. This dissociation suggests that behavioral measures 11 and neural representational measures capture distinct aspects of word recognition; behavioral 12 responses index the efficiency of the recognition and decision processes at the later stage of 13 lexical access, whereas RSA and ROI activation reveal the internal representational architecture 14 supporting this process. Inflected nouns with higher surface frequency may be accessed more 15 efficiently behaviorally as they possess more stable or more distinct neural representations in 16 IFGoper and IPL. Thus, the convergence between behavioral facilitation and neural 17 representational structure strengthens the interpretation that surface frequency indexes a 18 privileged pathway for accessing inflected forms. 19 A central theoretical question concerns what surface frequency encoding indeed 20 represents neurally. In pre-lexical decomposition accounts, lexical access is assumed to proceed 21 via early and obligatory segmentation into morphemic units, such that base frequency — 22 reflecting the resting activation of stored stem representations — serves as a primary determinant 23

Surface frequency, in this framework, is not 1expected to exert a dominant influence during initial processing stages; rather, its effects would 2emerge only at later stages where decomposed morphemes are recombined and matched against 3whole-word representations. In contrast, post-lexical or full-form access accounts hold that 4surface frequency directly indexes the strength of stored whole-word lexical entries, with higher-5frequency forms benefiting from more robust representations and thus more efficient retrieval. 6Two aspects of our findings support the latter interpretation. 7First, when examined within the inflected-noun stimuli, surface and base frequency 8demonstrated only moderate correlation and produced clearly distinct representational 9geometries (see Fig. S1 ). If surface-frequency effects were driven by stem-level statistics, one 10 would expect base frequency to exhibit parallel RSA encoding, which it did not. Second, 11 surface-frequency encoding localized primarily to IFGoper and SMG, regions implicated in 12 lexical retrieval, semantic integration, and competition resolution (Binder et al., 2009; Lau et al., 13 2008; Thompson-Schill et al., 1997), rather than in occipitotemporal regions, which has been 14 functionally linked to early morpho-orthographic segmentation (Gold & Rastle, 2007; Solomyak 15 & Marantz, 2010). This spatial dissociation implies that the RSA patterns reflect downstream 16 lexical-semantic processes tied to whole-form access rather than sublexical parsing. 17 Another interpretation can be derived from the Augmented Addressed Morphology 18 model (AAM; Caramazza et al., 1988) proposes that lexical access can proceed via both whole-19 word representations and morphological decomposition, with the relative contribution of each 20 route depending on factors such as frequency and representation strength. CONCLUSION 2In conclusion, the present findings provide the first fMRI evidence comparing surface and base 3frequency effects on neural representational structure in morphologically complex Korean words. 4The results demonstrate that morphological complexity modulates neural encoding of surface 5rather than base frequency and that morphological condition does not yield discrete category 6representations in classic language areas.
