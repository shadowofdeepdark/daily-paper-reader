Working memory (WM) representations that are distributed across the brain can be 2

flexibly recruited to best guide behavior 1–4 . For instance, information may be 3

represented relatively more strongly in visual cortex when a WM task requires fine 4

visual detail, or more strongly in motor cortex when a specific response can be 5

prepared 5–10 . If WM drives goal-oriented actions, we might also expect such task-6

dependent signals to propagate to the peripheral effectors that realize WM commands. 7

Accordingly, oculomotor signatures like gaze biases can track simple visuo-spatial WM 8

features 11,12 , but their functional flexibility is unclear. Here, we test the idea that WM 9

content is adaptively distributed across the nervous system according to behavioral 10 

demands. We ask whether patterns in both eye and hand movements can express 11 

visual WM stimulus features, and whether the distribution of such activity shifts with the 12 

task context. In a delayed recall task, we manipulated how human participants reported 13 

their memory: they would either draw a line or adjust a visible response wheel to match 14 

a remembered orientation. Via continuous eye- and stylus-tracking, we found that 15 

remembered orientations were decodable from small inflections in both gaze and hand 16 

movements during the WM maintenance period. Moreover, this decoding strength 17 

varied by response format. Gaze patterns tracked memorized features relatively better 18 

in the wheel condition (vs. draw ), while hand movements were better in the draw 19 

condition (vs. wheel ). Therefore, visually encoded WM contents may be adaptively 20 

allocated to task-relevant motor effectors, balancing WM representations across 21 

peripheral activity according to behavioral needs. 22 

23 RESULTS 24 

The crux of working memory (WM) lies in its flexibility to apply mental representations 25 

toward myriad current goals. Likewise, WM content may be held in different neural 26 

response patterns depending on how it will be used 4,6,9,13,14 . Rather than sustain a 27 

precise copy of encoded sensory features, an adaptive WM code may incorporate 28 

critical features of the task context 15 . Therefore, while the quality of visual WM is often 29 

tested by having participants adjust a visible prompt to match their memory (e.g., click 30 

locations on a wheel, continuously adjust the angle of a bar) 16–19 , this test format may 31 

shape the memory itself. Here, to examine how motor response demands influence 32 

visual WM maintenance, we had participants report their memory using a digital stylus 33 

and tablet. The critical manipulation was whether they were asked to freely recall the 34 

remembered stimulus by drawing it from memory, or whether they used a more 35 

conventional response wheel to make a continuous adjustment report. We recorded 36

76 Gaze and hand motion patterns maintain WM features 77 

WM is tightly intertwined with action, and neural signatures of WM content can often 78 

reflect features of an impending motor response 8,9,24–27 . Simple features of remembered 79 

information can also now be detected in oculomotor signatures outside the brain, like 80 

gaze biases that track the remembered location or orientation of a relevant WM 81 

stimulus 11,12,28,29 . WM may therefore engage distributed structures across the nervous 82 

system that are relevant for current goals. If so, in addition to oculomotor activity, we 83 

would expect manual motor effectors to be modulated by corresponding WM demands. 84 

Here, we tested whether memorized orientation features could be detected in both gaze 85 

and hand movement patterns during a WM delay (first collapsed across the draw/wheel 86 

condition; Figure 2 ). From eye movement recordings, we extracted distributions of gaze 87 

offset angle relative to each individual’s baseline gaze location ( Figure 2a ); From stylus 88 

recordings, we extracted the distribution of movements along each direction ( Figure 2b ;89 

see Quantification and Statistical Analysis ). During the blank WM delay interval, we 90 

observed that small inflections in both gaze offsets and hand trajectories appeared to 91 

vary systematically with the memorized orientations ( Figure 2a-b ), suggesting that 92 

motor activity tracks WM content information. We next formally quantified these 93 

patterns. 94

To gauge the quality of WM stimulus information in eye and hand movement patterns, 95 

we applied two multivariate techniques that are commonly used for neuroimaging 96 

analysis. First, we used representational similarity analysis (RSA 30 ; see 97 

Representational similarity analysis ) to assess whether more similar orientations 98 

evoked more similar eye or hand movement patterns during WM 12 . This would indicate 99 

that peripheral motor signatures discriminate between remembered stimuli. To test this, 100 

we correlated the stimulus similarity matrix (pairwise similarity between stimulus 101 

orientations) with the similarity matrix for the peripheral motor signatures (pairwise 102 

cosine similarity between movement feature vectors across remembered orientations). 103 

We assessed the timecourse of this correlation across the trial ( Figure 2c ). 104 

Shortly after encoding, stimulus-specific information emerged in both gaze and hand 105 

movement patterns for stimuli that were cued to remember (‘cued’), and it remained 106 

above chance throughout the WM delay. Critically, no above-chance information 107 

emerged for stimuli that were perceived but then cued to drop (‘uncued’). Therefore, 108 

both manual and oculomotor signatures reflect only the task-relevant mnemonic content 109 

– they are explained by neither lingering sensory-evoked activation nor a reflexive motor 110 

response to items that are seen but not remembered. This remained the case whether 111

While RSA can tell us that activity patterns reflect the relational structure in the stimulus 114 

space, the analysis does not derive the specific item being remembered. So we also 115 

applied inverted encoding modeling (IEM 31,32 ) to reconstruct an estimate of the 116 

remembered orientation from aggregate motor patterns. IEM learns stimulus-activity 117 

associations across feature-selective information channels during training, then predicts 118 

what information is reflected in untrained channel responses. The approach is used 119 

widely to estimate WM representation quality from population-level neural activity (e.g., 120 

fMRI/EEG) 33–39 , and here we apply it to peripheral motor activity to approximate the 121 

information in those patterns (see Inverted encoding modeling ). We trained and 122 

tested models using leave-one-subject-out cross-validation ( across-subject ) which we 123 

report in the main text. We report 5-fold cross-validation ( within-subject ) results in 124 

Supplemental Results 2.3 (Figure S2d ). 125 

We were able to reliably reconstruct relevant stimulus orientations during key trial 126 

epochs ( Figure 2d ; where stimulus evidence is the sharpness of the model prediction 127 

error distribution). Right after the first sample stimulus, that orientation was reliably 128 

reconstructed (  0.001  for Stim1, gaze and hand). Right after the second stimulus, 129 

that stimulus was reliably reconstructed, whereas the first stimulus remained above 130 

chance but became relatively dampened (  0.001  for Stim1 and Stim2, gaze and 131 

hand). During the blank WM delay, both relevant stimulus orientations could be reliably 132

stimuli were remembered (i.e., load of 1 or 2; Figure S2c ), further suggesting that these 135 

peripheral activity patterns can code for multiple memory representations. 136 

To summarize, we used two multivariate approaches to quantify the WM information 137 

content in gaze and hand movement patterns, across encoding and maintenance 138 

intervals. We found robust evidence for task-relevant mnemonic content in both motor 139 

measurements, suggesting that visual WM content is expressed in multiple effectors 140 

during maintenance. Stimulus information within individuals could also be reconstructed 141 

from models trained on other subjects’ data, indicating that these motor WM signatures 142 

may share properties across people. 143

Here, we focus on gaze during the 500ms after probe onset, for trials when both stimuli 151

165 Motor signatures of WM shift with expected response format 166 

Cortical signatures of WM content can adapt to how WM will be tested 3–6,14 . For 167 

instance, visuo-spatial WM content may be offloaded to a more motor-like neural 168 

representation when a specific response can be prepared 9. This suggests that motor 169 

intentions might mold how or where visual WM content is maintained. In other words, 170 

the same visual WM content might be represented differently when it precedes different 171 

actions. Our theoretical framework predicts that WM may also flexibly recruit the 172 

peripheral nervous system according to the task context. To test this, we next asked 173 

whether the WM signals in gaze and hand movements vary when a draw or wheel recall 174 

response is required. 175 

Both draw and wheel tasks tested memory for the same stimulus feature (angle), and 176 

used the same response implement (stylus). Nonetheless, we found that the relative 177 

strength of WM stimulus information in gaze and hand signals was modulated by the 178 

response format condition ( Figure 3 ). Namely, the correlation in the gaze RSA was 179 

stronger in the wheel condition compared to draw . Conversely, the correlation in the 180 

hand motion RSA was stronger in the draw condition compared to wheel (Figure 3a ). 181 

Therefore, we might infer that the motor effectors are differently task-relevant in the two 182 

conditions – while the eyes appear to ‘prefer’ the wheel condition, the hands appear to 183 

prefer the draw condition. 184

220 Competition and cooperation between gaze and hand codes 221 

If the different response demands modulate which effectors are most relevant for WM 222 

maintenance, we might expect to observe a hand-eye trade-off in stimulus evidence 223 

between the draw and wheel conditions ( Figure 4a ). For instance, when the response 224 

format changes from wheel to draw , a greater increase in an individual’s hand evidence 225 

should be accompanied by a corresponding decrease in gaze evidence. To test this, we 226 

calculated the difference in IEM evidence between the draw and wheel conditions, 227 

separately for gaze and hand data, for each individual ( Figure 4b ). We then examined 228 

the relationship between the gaze and hand difference scores (linear regression). 229 

We found a negative relationship across subjects (    . ,   . ; Figure 4c ), 230 

consistent with the idea that the gaze and hand WM representations trade off with each 231 

other. For instance, individuals with a relatively better average hand-based 232 

reconstruction during draw (vs. wheel ) also showed a relatively worse gaze-based 233 

reconstruction during draw (vs. wheel ). This suggests that when the balance of 234 

maintenance activity shifts towards the hands, it is correspondingly dampened in the 235 

eyes (and vice versa). The system may adaptively distribute limited maintenance 236 

resources across the relevant peripheral structures. 237

260 DISCUSSION 261 

Here, we found that patterns in both eye and hand movements can express visual WM 262 

stimulus features, but the distribution of such peripheral motor activity shifts with the 263 

task context. Given identical visual stimulus content, distributed WM maintenance 264 

activity may tailor to which effectors are most relevant for an upcoming task. 265 

The hands showed stronger WM information preceding a drawing response (vs. wheel ), 266 

while the eyes showed stronger WM information preceding a wheel adjustment (vs. 267 

draw ). A draw response can be freely executed without visual input, but may require a 268 

relatively elaborated motor plan. A wheel response is prompted by a visible probe, and 269 

may require continuously adjusting the probe to match memory. These subtle 270 

differences in impending task demand may shape how WM activity is distributed across 271 

the nervous system. 272
