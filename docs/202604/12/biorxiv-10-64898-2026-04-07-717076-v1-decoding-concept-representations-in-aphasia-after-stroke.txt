Abstract 16 

17 Many stroke survivors with aphasia struggle to map their thoughts into words or motor plans. 18 Neuroprostheses that decode concept representations could help these individuals communicate by 19 predicting the words, phrases, or sentences that they are struggling to produce. Here we decoded concept 20 representations measured using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) from participants with 21 different aphasia profiles. The decoders generated continuous word sequences that could describe the 22 concepts that the participants were hearing about, seeing, or imagining. To forecast how this approach 23 would generalize across the heterogeneity of aphasia profiles, we characterized how stroke affects the 24 anatomical organization and information capacity of conceptual processing. Mapping how concepts are 25 organized across the brain, we found that conceptual tuning during non-linguistic processing was largely 26 consistent between the participants with aphasia and neurologically healthy participants. Comparing 27 information processing between the participants with aphasia and neurologically healthy participants, we 28 found that both groups processed similar amounts of non-linguistic information. Our findings indicate 29 that concept representations can be largely spared in individuals with aphasia and demonstrate how these 30 representations can be decoded to support communication. 31

Introduction 32 

33 Aphasia is a communication disorder caused by damage to the language regions of the brain. The most 34 common cause of aphasia is stroke, and it is estimated that nearly a third of stroke survivors experience 35 aphasia 1. Strokes can affect multiple brain regions that serve different linguistic functions, so individuals 36 with post-stroke aphasia can exhibit a variety of language production and comprehension impairments 2–4 .37 However, these individuals often perform well on non-linguistic tasks 5,6 and report knowing what they 38 want to say but not knowing how to say it 7. This suggests that their conceptual knowledge is often spared 39 even when their access to lexical or phonological knowledge is disrupted 8–10 . Consequently, 40 neuroprostheses could help these individuals communicate by decoding spared concept representations 41 into intended language output. 42 43 Concepts appear to be encoded in a widely distributed network of brain regions known as the semantic 44 system 11 . To measure concept representations in neurologically healthy participants, we previously 45 recorded brain activity using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) while each participant 46 listened to many hours of narrative stories 12 . We then used voxelwise modeling to estimate how the 47 activity in each brain region is related to semantic features of the stimulus words 13 . This produces an 48 encoding model that can take any word sequence and predict how the participant’s brain would respond. 49 To decode new brain responses, we generated word sequences to make the predicted responses under the 50 encoding model as similar as possible to the actual responses 14 . This produces a natural language 51 approximation of the concepts that the participant was thinking about. 52 53 While this semantic decoding approach can successfully approximate the meaning of perceived and 54 imagined stimuli, it has previously only been demonstrated in neurologically healthy participants. 55 Individuals with aphasia often have comorbidities that make it difficult for them to tolerate the many 56 hours of scanning required to train decoders 15 . Moreover, these individuals often have language 57 comprehension impairments that could make it difficult to train decoders on brain responses to linguistic 58 stimuli 3. To simultaneously address these barriers, we developed an approach for transferring semantic 59 decoders across participants by aligning their brain responses using a small set of common stimuli 16,17 .60 Because concept representations are shared across cognitive processes, the common stimuli can be either 61 linguistic or non-linguistic, making this transfer learning approach robust to language comprehension 62 impairments 18–21 .63 64 Here we used these approaches to decode concept representations in participants with post-stroke aphasia. 65 We trained encoding models on brain responses from neurologically healthy reference participants, 66 transferred them to the participants with aphasia, and used the aligned models to decode new brain 67 responses. We found that the decoder predictions could describe the concepts that the participants with 68 aphasia were hearing about, seeing, or imagining. Because semantic decoding requires spared concept 69 representations, we assessed the generality of this approach by characterizing how stroke affects 70 conceptual processing. We found that the anatomical organization and information capacity of conceptual 71 processing were largely spared even in participants with extensive lesions and severe speech and language 72 and impairments. This demonstrates the potential for semantic decoding to support individuals with post-73 stroke aphasia regardless of their lesion profiles, speech and language impairments, or intended concepts. 74 75

Participants with aphasia 76 

77 We enrolled three participants with post-stroke aphasia ( Figure 1 ). P1 was a right-handed man who 78 suffered a left intraparenchymal hemorrhagic stroke resulting from an arteriovenous malformation 8 years 79 prior to participation in the study. P2 was a right-handed man who suffered a left middle cerebral artery 80 infarction resulting from an embolism 2 years prior to participation. P3 was a right-handed woman who 81 suffered bilateral middle cerebral artery infarctions resulting from bilateral pulmonary emboli 4 years 82 prior to participation. We manually delineated lesions on T1-weighted images using ITK-SNAP. We 83 characterized speech and language processing using the Quick Aphasia Battery 22 , auditory verbal 84 comprehension using the Peabody Picture Vocabulary Test 23 , and conceptual processing using the 85 Pyramids and Palm Trees Test 24 (Table 1 ). 86 87 Structural imaging indicated that the participants had different lesion profiles. P1 had an extensive left 88 hemisphere lesion impacting lateral and ventral temporal cortex. P2 had an extensive left hemisphere 89 lesion impacting lateral frontal cortex, inferior parietal cortex, lateral temporal cortex, and insular cortex. 90 P3 had an extensive left hemisphere lesion impacting lateral frontal cortex, inferior parietal cortex, lateral 91 temporal cortex, and insular cortex, as well as a less extensive right hemisphere lesion impacting lateral 92 frontal cortex, lateral temporal cortex, and insular cortex. 93 94 Speech and language testing indicated that the participants had impairments to different aspects of 95 language production. P1 predominantly struggled with word finding; his spoken output was fluent with 96 frequent pauses and circumlocutions. P2 struggled with grammatical construction and word finding; his 97 spoken output was largely limited to short phrases and written output was largely limited to single words. 98 P3 struggled with speech motor programming and grammatical construction; her spoken output was 99 largely unintelligible due to severe apraxia of speech and written output was largely limited to short 100 phrases. The participants also had different degrees of language comprehension impairment. P1 and P2 101 could comprehend conversational speech but struggled with more grammatically complex sentences, 102 while P3 struggled to comprehend any spoken language but demonstrated much greater sparing of written 103 language. 104 105 Despite their different speech and language impairments, participants showed relative sparing of 106 nonverbal semantic processing on the Pyramids and Palm Trees–Pictures Test, a picture association task 107 (45/52 for P1, 45/52 for P2, 46/52 for P3). The scores were close to the cutoff for neurologically healthy 108 performance (47/52), indicating that non-linguistic conceptual knowledge was relatively spared. All 109 participants demonstrated understanding of task instructions and complied with the behavioral 110 assessments and neuroimaging experiments. 111

Semantic decoding of perceived and imagined concepts 123 

124 The goal of semantic decoding is to generate word sequences using only brain responses as input. To 125 model the relationships between word sequences and brain responses, we trained encoding models on 126 fMRI responses collected from four neurologically healthy reference participants while they listened to 10 127 h of narrative stories 12,13,25,26 (Figure 2a ). To transfer these encoding models to the participants with 128 aphasia, we trained cross-participant converters to align fMRI responses between the reference 129 participants and the participants with aphasia 16,17,27 . Converter training data collected from both groups 130 comprised 3 h of narrative stories, as well as 1 h of silent movies to account for comprehension 131 impairments. The participants with aphasia were given the option to listen to the stories with subtitles 132 (Methods ). To decode new brain responses from a participant with aphasia, we can generate word 133 sequences using a language model, predict brain responses to the word sequences using the aligned 134 encoding models, and identify the word sequences that are most consistent with the recorded brain 135 responses 14 (Figure 2b ). 136 137 To evaluate the semantic decoders, we first decoded single-trial brain responses collected while the 138 participants listened to novel test stories. We found that the decoder predictions could successfully 139 approximate the gist of the story events for P1 and P2 ( Figure 2c ; see Supplementary Table 1 for all 140 participants). We quantified decoding performance by comparing the predictions to the story words using 141 BERTScore, which is a metric designed to measure the similarity of meaning between two word 142 sequences 28 . Story decoding performance for all participants with aphasia was significantly higher than 143 expected by chance (q(FDR) < 0.05; one-sided non-parametric test) but lower than that of neurologically 144 healthy controls ( Figure 2d ). In particular, P3 had the lowest decoding performance consistent with her 145 severe language comprehension impairment, which may have prevented her from accessing the 146 conceptual meaning of the stories. 147 148 To determine whether the lower story decoding performance reflects conceptual processing impairments 149 or language comprehension impairments, we next decoded single-trial brain responses collected while the 150 participants watched novel test movies that were presented without sound. Our previous work showed 151 that word sequences describing silent movie stimuli can be decoded from neurologically healthy 152 participants using semantic decoders trained only on linguistic stimuli 14 . Here, we found that the decoder 153 predictions could successfully approximate the gist of the movie events for all participants with aphasia 154 (Figure 2e ; see Supplementary Table 2 for all participants). We quantified decoding performance by 155 comparing the predictions to official verbal descriptions of the movies. Movie decoding performance for 156 all participants with aphasia was significantly higher than expected by chance (q(FDR) < 0.05; one-sided 157 non-parametric test) and approached that of neurologically healthy controls, indicating that non-linguistic 158 conceptual processing was relatively spared ( Figure 2f ). 159 160 Language neuroprostheses should be able to decode internally generated concepts, so we decoded single-161 trial brain responses collected while the participants silently imagined three topics in the MRI scanner. 162 We prompted the participants to imagine traveling to the scanning session, eating breakfast, and getting 163 dressed. The participants then described what they had imagined for each topic outside of the scanner. We 164 found that the decoder predictions often matched the prompted topics for all participants with aphasia 165

Anatomical organization of conceptual processing after stroke 191 

192 The decoding results demonstrate that spared concept representations in the participants with aphasia can 193 be decoded into continuous language. We can forecast how these results would generalize to other 194 concepts from other individuals with aphasia by determining the extent to which concept representations 195 are spared in stroke. We first mapped how stroke affects the anatomical organization of the semantic 196 system. In neurologically healthy participants, concepts are encoded by distributed patterns of brain 197 activity that are largely consistent across individuals 13 , stimulus modalities 20,21,29 , and languages 30 . One 198 possibility is that spared brain regions in individuals with aphasia are selective for the same concepts 199 before and after stroke, which would indicate that the semantic system can be robust to focal damage. 200 Another possibility is that spared brain regions acquire selectivity for new concepts to compensate for 201 damaged regions, which would indicate that the semantic system can reorganize in response to focal 202 damage. 203 204 To evaluate these possibilities, we used encoding models to map the conceptual tuning of each brain 205 region. We trained encoding models for the neurologically healthy reference participants using a lexical 206 semantic embedding space derived from word co-occurrence statistics 13 . We transferred the encoding 207 models to the participants with aphasia by aligning brain responses using either narrative stories or silent 208 movies 17 . For each participant with aphasia, this produced a story-aligned encoding model that maps 209 linguistic conceptual tuning and a movie-aligned encoding model that maps non-linguistic conceptual 210 tuning. We then compared conceptual tuning between the participants with aphasia and the reference 211 participants at every location on a cortical surface atlas 31 . Because the encoding models were transferred 212 across participants using only functional correspondences, any anatomical correspondences between the 213 encoding models indicate shared conceptual tuning. 214 215 To visualize conceptual tuning, we assigned a color to every location on the cortical surface atlas by 216 projecting the encoding model weights onto the three dimensions of the lexical semantic embedding space 217 that explained the most variance in a separate group of neurologically healthy participants 13 (Figure 3a ). 218 The story-aligned encoding models showed that P1 shared linguistic tuning with the reference participants 219 outside of damaged regions while P2 and P3 had larger differences in their linguistic tuning. However, the 220 differences could result from impaired linguistic access to the concept representations rather than 221 reorganization of the concept representations themselves. This was confirmed by the movie-aligned 222 encoding models, which showed that all participants with aphasia shared non-linguistic tuning with the 223 reference participants outside of damaged regions. For instance, concrete concepts (e.g. visual , tactile ,224 

body part ) were represented near visual cortex and somatosensory cortex, while abstract concepts (e.g. 225 

social , mental , time ) were represented in medial parietal cortex, inferior parietal cortex, and lateral 226 temporal cortex 13 .227 228 To quantify these results, we operationalized the conceptual tuning at every location on the cortical 229 surface atlas by using the movie-aligned encoding models to simulate brain responses to 10,470 230 concepts 13 . We next measured how stroke affects the information capacity of the semantic system. Even if each brain 256 region processes the same concepts after stroke as it did before stroke, the semantic system as a whole 257 might not process information to the same extent (e.g. only processing concepts from certain categories) 258 or granularity (e.g. only processing more superordinate concepts). Because semantic decoders operate on 259 spared concept representations, the information capacity of the semantic system determines the extent and 260 granularity of the concepts that can be decoded. 261 262 While we cannot measure the premorbid capacity of the semantic system in a participant with aphasia, we 263 can approximate it using brain responses from neurologically healthy reference participants. We can then 264 quantify the post-stroke capacity in the participant with aphasia by assessing how well their brain 265 responses can predict brain responses from the reference participants. Here we separately trained cross-266 participant converters on brain responses to narrative stories and silent movies, and evaluated converter 267 performance using the linear correlation between the predicted and actual responses from the reference 268 participants ( Figure 4a ). High converter performance in a reference participant brain region indicates that 269 the information processed in the region is still processed somewhere in the participant with aphasia 32 .270 Low converter performance indicates that the information may no longer be processed anywhere in the 271 participant with aphasia. We obtained a ceiling on converter performance by training cross-participant 272 converters between pairs of reference participants. 273 274 We visualized converter performance on a cortical surface atlas after averaging across reference 275 participants 31 (Figure 4b ). Story-based converter performance was modest outside of auditory and 276 articulatory regions, which indicates that some of the conceptual information in the narrative story stimuli 277 was not fully processed by the participants with aphasia, consistent with the language comprehension 278 impairments that we documented. Conversely, movie-based converter performance approached the 279 ceiling in most brain regions, which indicates that most of the conceptual information in the silent movie 280 stimuli was fully processed by the participants with aphasia. These results demonstrate that the 281 information capacity of the semantic system can be largely spared even in participants with extensive 282 strokes and severely impaired language processing. Consequently, semantic decoding may generalize far 283 beyond the concepts and participants that were assessed in this study. 284

Factors affecting semantic decoding performance 296 

297 There are several remaining barriers to using semantic decoders as language neuroprostheses. One major 298 barrier is that the decoders are not consistently accurate. Another major barrier is that semantic decoding 299 from neurological patients has only been demonstrated using fMRI, which requires a large, expensive, 300 and non-portable scanner that is unsuitable for most practical applications. To determine potential 301 avenues for translating semantic decoders into practical language neuroprostheses, we assessed the factors 302 that affect mental imagery decoding performance for the participants with aphasia. While advances in brain recording 309 technologies and decoding algorithms are difficult to forecast, many studies have found that increasing 310 the amount of training data consistently improves decoding performance 14,33–35 . Discussion 346 

347 This study demonstrates that concept representations in individuals with post-stroke aphasia can be 348 decoded into continuous language with a reasonable degree of accuracy. In practice, semantic decoders 349 could be used as language neuroprostheses to predict the words, phrases, or sentences that these 350 individuals are struggling to produce. Existing augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) 351 devices allow individuals with aphasia to retrieve words or phrases linked to images, but they are limited 352 in real-time responsiveness and in the scope and richness of concepts they can convey, falling short of 353 natural communication 39 . By directly predicting what individuals are thinking about based on their brain 354 activity, language neuroprostheses could be substantially more effective at translating rich internal 355 thoughts into natural language. 356 357 Most existing language neuroprostheses decode motor representations to help individuals with dysarthria, 358 who struggle to map motor representations into muscle movements 34,35,38 . However, these motor decoders 359 are unsuitable for individuals with aphasia, who are often impaired on earlier stages of language 360 production such as lexical access or phonological encoding. Because all stages of language production are 361 thought to be downstream of conceptual processing, semantic decoders that target concept representations 362 should be highly robust to the wide range of linguistic and co-occurring motoric impairments in aphasia 8– 

363 10 . Our decoding results generalized across participants with different behavioral profiles, demonstrating 364

Previous studies have approximated this extent using behavioral assessments 40–43 and controlled 369 neuroimaging experiments 44 , but these studies lacked the precision and coverage required to characterize 370 the anatomical organization and information capacity of the semantic system. By using naturalistic 371 neuroimaging experiments to measure thousands of concept representations, we found that conceptual 372 processing can be largely spared in individuals with post-stroke aphasia despite extensive lesions 373 impacting different regions of the semantic system. These results demonstrate that semantic decoding can 374 be robust to the heterogeneity of lesion profiles in aphasia 3,45 .375 376 Our results point toward several scalable avenues for improving the accuracy of the decoder predictions. 377 It may also be possible to increase the functional utility of the decoder predictions by integrating them 378 with residual language output, displaying them in real-time, and personalizing them for each individual 379 with aphasia. For instance, many individuals with aphasia can produce some words despite struggling 380 with others, so the successfully produced words could be used as linguistic context to inform the decoder 381 predictions 46,47 . Similarly, many individuals with aphasia can successfully produce words after receiving 382 semantic cues, so it could be helpful to display multiple sets of decoder predictions even if none are 383 completely accurate 48 .384 385 While this study decoded brain activity measured using fMRI, our results demonstrate that semantic 386 decoding from individuals with aphasia does not require the full brain coverage of fMRI. Consequently, 387 semantic decoding could be adapted for wearable technologies such as functional near-infrared 388 spectroscopy 37 or electroencephalography 33 . Semantic decoding using fMRI could also help plan implant 389 locations for intracranial neuroprostheses targeting conceptual information 49,50 . Taken together, our results 390 suggest several potential avenues for using semantic decoding to support communication in individuals 391 with aphasia. 392

502 GPT (also known as GPT-1) is a 12-layer transformer neural network trained to predict the next word in a 503 sequence given the previous words 57 . Decoding performance 631 

632 

Decoder predictions from the story and movie experiments were compared to target words from the 633 stimulus transcripts using the BERTScore metric 28 . BERTScore was computed between the predicted and 634 target words within a 20 second window around each second of the stimulus. BERTScore uses a 635 bidirectional transformer language model to represent the meaning of each predicted and target word as a 636 contextual embedding, and scores each target word based on its maximum cosine similarity across all of 637 the predicted words. These scores are averaged across the target words to quantify the similarity of 638 meaning between the predicted and target word sequences. 639 

640 

Because BERTScore values are not inherently interpretable, they were normalized with respect to a null 641 distribution 28 . Null sequences were generated by sampling from the language model without using any 642 brain data except to predict word times 14 . The null model maintained a beam of 10 candidate sequences 643 and generated continuations from the language model nucleus at each predicted word time 63 . The only 644 difference between the actual decoder and the null model was that, instead of ranking the continuations by 645 the likelihood of the fMRI data, the null model randomly assigned a likelihood to each continuation. For 646 each participant with aphasia, this process was repeated 200 times to generate 200 null sequences. This 647 process was as similar as possible to the actual decoder without using any brain data to select words, so 648 these sequences reflected the null hypothesis that the decoder does not recover meaningful information 649 about the stimulus from the brain data. The null sequences were scored against the target words to 650 produce a null distribution of decoder scores for each participant with aphasia. 651 652

681 Whole brain MRI data were partitioned into 5 functional networks using a clustering approach 26 .682 Encoding models were trained to predict brain responses from the neurologically healthy participants 683 using lexical semantic features of the stimulus words 13 . 704 Statistical significance of decoder scores was assessed using a one-sided non-parametric test. Prediction 711 performance for each voxel was computed as the linear correlation between the predicted and actual 712 response time-courses. A null distribution for each voxel was obtained by randomly resampling (with 713 replacement) 10-TR blocks from the voxel’s actual response time-course. Resampling contiguous blocks 714 preserves the auto-correlation structure of the voxel’s responses. The null prediction performance was 715 then computed as the linear correlation between the predicted and permuted response time-courses. 716 Repeating this process for 1,000 trials provided a null distribution of prediction performance for each 717 voxel. p values were computed for each voxel as the fraction of permutations with prediction performance 718 greater than or equal to than the observed prediction performance. 719 720 Statistical significance of tuning correlation was assessed using a one-sided permutation test.
