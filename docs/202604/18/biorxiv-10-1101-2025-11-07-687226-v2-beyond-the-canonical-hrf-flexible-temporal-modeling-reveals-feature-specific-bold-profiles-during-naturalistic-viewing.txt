Abstract 19 

Naturalistic stimuli, such as movies and narratives, are increasingly used in cognitive 20 

neuroscience to map cognitive and affective processes onto brain activity measured with 21 

functional MRI (fMRI). Features extracted from movies span multiple levels, from computational 22 

descriptions of visual and auditory input to physiological signals and subjective ratings. However, 23 

the temporal alignment between these features and the blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) 24 

response can vary considerably, and the commonly used canonical hemodynamic response 25 

function (HRF) with temporal derivatives may not adequately capture these delays. In this study, 26 

we analyzed three movie-watching datasets and examined a range of features, including visual 27 

luminance and contrast, auditory pitch, a physiological measure of pupil size, and subjective 28 

ratings of theory of mind. We performed cross-correlation analyses and finite impulse response 29 

(FIR) deconvolution between feature time series and fMRI responses across the whole brain. 30 

Our results show that the canonical HRF captures sensory features well, but misaligns with 31 

slower signals, such as pupil size and subjective ratings. Notably, raw pupil size and subjective 32 

ratings exhibited stronger correspondence with delayed hemodynamic responses. Lastly, using 33 

FIR deconvolution, we systematically characterized the true BOLD response profiles of these 34 

diverse features, revealing distinct inter-regional temporal hierarchies and peak latencies that 35 

systematically deviate from canonical assumptions. These findings highlight the critical role of 36 

temporal alignment in mapping naturalistic features to brain activity. Accounting for feature-37 

specific delays using flexible temporal modeling may improve the interpretability and accuracy 38 

of fMRI studies using complex, real-world stimuli. 39

1. Introduction 44 

Naturalistic stimuli — such as movies or spoken narratives — have become increasingly 45 

popular in functional MRI (fMRI) research because they offer tools to investigate brain function 46 

under ecologically valid conditions (Di et al., 2026; Hasson et al., 2004; Vanderwal et al., 2019). 47 

Unlike traditional, highly controlled experimental paradigms, naturalistic stimuli capture the 48 

richness and temporal continuity of real-world experiences, thereby engaging a wide range of 49 

perceptual, cognitive, and affective processes simultaneously. This shift has opened new 50 

avenues for studying how the brain integrates complex, dynamic information over time. 51 

A seminal line of work demonstrated that when multiple individuals watch the same 52 

movie or listen to the same story, their brain activity exhibits significant inter-subject correlations 53 

(ISC), revealing shared neural responses to naturalistic events (Hasson et al., 2004). Such 54 

data-driven analyses have been instrumental in identifying brain regions involved in high-level 55 

perception, attention, and social cognition (Kauppi et al., 2010). However, while these 56 

approaches effectively capture synchronized patterns of activity across individuals, they do not 57 

directly reveal which specific features of the stimulus drive these neural responses. To address 58 

this, researchers have increasingly focused on extracting quantitative features from naturalistic 59 

stimuli and mapping their temporal dynamics onto brain activity measured by fMRI (Bartels et al., 60 

2008; Bartels and Zeki, 2004; Huth et al., 2016; Lahnakoski et al., 2012a; Raz et al., 2016). 61 

A wide variety of stimulus-derived features can be used for such mapping. These range 62 

from low-level sensory features — such as visual luminance, contrast, and auditory pitch — to 63 

higher-level variables derived from physiological signals (e.g., pupil size) or subjective 64 

behavioral ratings (e.g., perceived emotional valence or theory of mind, ToM). In recent years, 65 

feature extraction has also benefited from advances in deep neural networks, where model-66 

based representations capture complex visual, auditory, or semantic content (McMahon et al., 67 

2023; Samara et al., 2025; Sohn et al., 2024). These features can be analyzed individually 68

Crucially, naturalistic stimuli are not monolithic; they contain deeply nested, hierarchical 71 

temporal structures. For example, visual streams progress from rapid, high-frequency shifts in 72 

luminance to slower transitions between objects and scenes, while auditory streams build from 73 

transient phonemes to extended narratives. A prominent theoretical framework posits that the 74 

brain processes these nested features through a topography of hierarchical cortical timescales, 75 

or temporal receptive windows (TRWs) (Lerner et al., 2011; Meer et al., 2020). In this framework, 76 

primary sensory regions possess short TRWs to process rapid physical properties, whereas 77 

higher-order associative networks possess long TRWs to integrate semantic and narrative 78 

information over extended durations. 79 

Beyond extrinsic stimulus features, naturalistic viewing also engages internally 80 

generated, observer-dependent dynamics. Physiological responses, such as pupil size, act as 81 

markers of autonomic arousal and cognitive load, reflecting an integration of sensory input and 82 

internal state (Joshi and Gold, 2020). Similarly, subjective behavioral ratings—such as ToM 83 

annotations—capture conscious, high-level social-cognitive evaluations of characters' mental 84 

states over time (Saarimäki, 2021). As illustrated in our conceptual framework (Figure 1), 85 

appropriately mapping naturalistic viewing to brain activity requires integrating three distinct 86 

temporal levels: 1) the nested intrinsic hierarchies of the stimulus features; 2) the biological and 87 

cognitive integration delays inherent to the observer's physiological and behavioral responses; 88 

and 3) the region-dependent hemodynamic brain responses. 89

A critical challenge in linking these features to fMRI data lies in the temporal mismatch 117 

between stimulus features and the hemodynamic response. The fMRI signal—via the blood-118 

oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) effect—typically lags behind underlying neural activity by about 119 

5–6 seconds (Friston et al., 1998; Handwerker et al., 2004). To account for this, most studies 120 

either apply a fixed delay (e.g., 4 s) or convolve the feature time-series with a canonical 121 

hemodynamic response function (HRF). Some approaches further include temporal derivatives 122 

of the HRF to allow limited flexibility in response timing (i.e., ≈1 s adjustment). However, the 123 

shape and latency of the HRF vary considerably across brain regions, across individuals, and 124 

even within the same region under different physiological conditions (Aguirre et al., 1998; 125 

Handwerker et al., 2004). Such intrinsic biological variability complicates efforts to accurately 126 

align continuous stimulus features with corresponding neural activity. 127

This temporal uncertainty becomes especially pronounced when analyzing multiple 128 

types of features that occupy different levels of the naturalistic processing hierarchy. Applying a 129 

uniform canonical HRF across all feature classes fundamentally ignores the diverse intrinsic 130 

frequencies and delays inherent to these distinct signals. Fast sensory inputs, delayed 131 

physiological signals, and slow cognitive annotations possess vastly different temporal 132 

relationships to neural and vascular processes. Because pupil size and subjective behavioral 133 

ratings are themselves delayed responses relative to the eliciting neural activity, convolving 134 

them with a standard HRF risks compounding delays. This conventional approach can result in 135 

"double-smoothing" temporal artifacts that obscure true brain-behavior coupling (Lloyd et al., 136 

2023). 137 

To overcome the limitations of the canonical HRF and accurately map hierarchical 138 

processing timescales, more flexible temporal modeling is required. Finite Impulse Response 139 

(FIR) deconvolution provides a robust, non-parametric alternative (Glover, 1999). Rather than 140 

imposing a predetermined temporal shape and fixed latency, FIR models utilize a set of time-141 

lagged predictors to directly estimate the true, feature-specific system response function from 142 

the continuous fMRI data. By allowing the response shape and peak latency to vary freely, FIR 143 

deconvolution can capture the diverse temporal profiles of naturalistic processing—ranging from 144 

rapid sensory responses to the extended, diffuse hemodynamic delays that characterize slower, 145 

higher-order cognitive features. 146 

In the present study, we analyzed three movie-watching fMRI datasets to systematically 147 

characterize how different classes of stimulus, physiological, and behavioral features are 148 

expressed in their true BOLD response shapes. We extracted continuous features spanning the 149 

hierarchical levels of naturalistic processing: low-level sensory features (frame luminance, frame 150 

contrast, and auditory pitch), a physiological measure (pupil size), and a subjective behavioral 151 

measure (ToM ratings). Moving beyond conventional cross-correlation analyses, we applied 152 

formal FIR deconvolution to estimate the BOLD system response functions across the whole 153

2.1. Movie-watching fMRI Data 160 

We analyzed three movie-watching fMRI datasets previously used in our work (Di et al., 2023; 161 

Di and Biswal, 2022): two from the Healthy Brain Network (HBN) project (Alexander et al., 2017), 162 

and one from the Partly Cloudy dataset (Richardson et al., 2018). 163 

2.1.1. Healthy Brain Network (HBN) Dataset 164 

The HBN dataset was obtained from the project repository 165 

(http://fcon_1000.projects.nitrc.org/indi/cmi_healthy_brain_network/). For the current study, we 166 

included a subset of participants without diagnosed psychiatric disorders who underwent fMRI 167

2.3. Subjective Rating Data Collection 217 

Subjective rating data were collected from a separate group of participants at the New Jersey 218 

Institute of Technology (NJIT) to capture continuous, moment-to-moment fluctuations in 219 

perceived mental states of movie characters. A total of 34 participants (age range: 18–27 years, 220 

mean ± SD = 20.9 ± 2.3 years; 17 female) took part in the study. All participants reported normal 221 

or corrected-to-normal vision and hearing, and none had a history of neurological or psychiatric 222 

disorders. Informed consent was obtained from all participants in accordance with the 223 

Institutional Review Board (IRB) of NJIT. 224 

Participants viewed the same three video clips used in the fMRI experiment. Each 225 

session took place in a quiet, darkened room to minimize visual and auditory distractions. 226 

Stimuli were presented on a computer monitor with synchronized sound through a speaker. 227 

Continuous subjective ratings were acquired using CARMA software (Continuous Affect 228 

Rating and Media Annotation; (Girard, 2014) with a gaming joystick as the input device. 229 

Participants were instructed to continuously indicate the extent to which they could perceive the 230 

characters’ emotions, thoughts, or intentions while watching each clip. This measure was 231

2.7. Temporal characteristics of multimodal features 283 

Across the five selected features, we observed distinct temporal and frequency characteristics 284 

that correspond to their respective levels in the naturalistic processing hierarchy. Low-level 285 

sensory features, such as frame luminance and auditory pitch, exhibited highly transient, high-286 

frequency fluctuations (sampled at video frame rates or 10 Hz, respectively) reflecting rapid, 287 

instantaneous changes in the external physical stimulus. In contrast, frame contrast exhibited 288 

slightly slower dynamic shifts, corresponding to sustained visual scene characteristics. Finally, 289 

the physiological (median pupil size) and behavioral (median ToM rating) features operated on 290 

markedly slower, lower-frequency timescales. These differences highlight that higher-level 291

2.8. MRI Data Processing 298 

Preprocessing of the fMRI data was performed separately for the HBN and Partly Cloudy 299 

datasets, following procedures described in previous studies. Here, we provide a brief overview. 300 

Structural MRI images for each participant were first segmented and normalized to the standard 301 

Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) space. For The Present and Despicable Me datasets, the 302 

first 10 functional volumes were discarded, resulting in 240 and 740 volumes, respectively. In 303 

contrast, all functional volumes were retained for the Partly Cloudy dataset. The remaining 304 

functional images were then realigned to the first retained image, coregistered to the 305 

participant’s structural image, normalized to MNI space using the deformation fields obtained 306 

during segmentation, and spatially smoothed with an 8 mm full-width-at-half-maximum (FWHM) 307 

Gaussian kernel. 308 

Finally, a general linear model (GLM) was constructed to regress out head motion 309 

effects using Friston’s 24-parameter motion model, which also incorporated an implicit high-310 

pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 1/128 Hz. The resulting residual time series were used in all 311 

subsequent analyses. 312 

We adopted a region-of-interest (ROI)–based approach to achieve a balance between 313 

spatial specificity and temporal flexibility. Cortical regions were defined using the Schaefer 100-314 

region parcellation (Schaefer et al., 2018), and subcortical regions were defined using the 315 

Automated Anatomical Labeling (AAL) atlas (Tzourio-Mazoyer et al., 2002). Fourteen bilateral 316 

subcortical nuclei were included: hippocampus, parahippocampus, amygdala, caudate, 317

2.9. Cross-correlation Analysis 326 

Each feature time series from the three movie clips was first convolved with the canonical HRF 327 

implemented in SPM12 (using the default parameters: peak delay = 6 s, undershoot delay = 16 328 

s, and ratio = 6). The convolved feature time series were then resampled to match the temporal 329 

resolution of the corresponding fMRI data (TR = 0.8 s for The Present and Despicable Me ; TR = 330 

2.0 s for Partly Cloudy ). 331 

Subsequently, cross-correlation analyses were performed between the HRF-convolved 332 

feature time series and the mean fMRI time series from each ROI for each participant. 333 

Correlations were computed across a lag range of ±16 seconds (i.e., ±20 TRs for HBN data and 334 

±8 TRs for Partly Cloudy ), resulting in a lag-by-ROI correlation matrix for each participant. For 335 

visualization and group-level interpretation, these matrices were averaged across participants 336 

after Fisher’s z transformation of the correlation coefficients. 337 

In addition, a regression analysis was conducted using the HRF-convolved feature time 338 

series as predictors in a general linear model (GLM) framework for each participant. Group-level 339 

statistical significance of the resulting parameter estimates was assessed using one-sample t-340 

tests across participants, which corresponds to testing the cross-correlation at zero lag. Multiple 341 

comparisons across the 114 ROIs were controlled using the false discovery rate (FDR) at p <342 

0.05. 343

2.10. Finite Impulse Response Deconvolution 350 

We performed FIR deconvolution to estimate the system response function between an input 351 

signal ( x(t) ) and an output signal ( y(t) ). Specifically, we first examined the pupillary system 352 

response using frame luminance as the input ( x(t) ) and pupil size as the output ( y(t) ). 353 

Subsequently, we applied the same approach to estimate the HRFs in different brain regions 354 

using the various video-related features described above as input signals and fMRI time series 355 

from each ROI as outputs. 356

an unknown system response function: 358

To improve numerical stability and reduce overfitting, we first applied an autoregressive 367

For the pupil–luminance analysis, FIR deconvolution was performed on the median pupil 370

baseline offsets and normalize amplitudes. 372 

For the fMRI data, FIR analysis was conducted separately for each participant and ROI. 373 

Prior to deconvolution, input features were resampled to match the fMRI sampling rate, and 374 

both input and output signals were z-scored. 375 

To determine the statistical significance of the estimated FIR models, we utilized an F-376 

test at the individual participant level to evaluate the overall variance explained by the full set of 377 

time-lagged predictors. To perform group-level statistical inference, we applied Fisher's method 378 

to aggregate the individual-level p-values across all participants for each ROI (Fisher, 1925). 379 

The resulting group-level p-values were then corrected for multiple comparisons across the 114 380 

ROIs using the FDR at p < 0.05, allowing us to systematically identify regions with significant 381 

FIR effects. 382 

For regions demonstrating significant group-level responses, we obtained group-level 383 

response functions by averaging the individual FIR estimates after temporal alignment. To 384 

accurately quantify temporal hierarchies, we characterized the temporal dynamics by extracting 385 

the delay of the peak response (time-to-peak) for each ROI. To ensure that this metric captured 386 

primary stimulus-driven activations rather than secondary signal dips or undershoots, the peak 387 

delay was identified by extracting the maximum amplitude strictly from the positive values of the 388 

estimated FIR response function. These resulting temporal profiles and peak latencies were 389 

then evaluated to compare physiological and neural dynamics across brain systems. 390

4. Discussion 607 

In the present study, we systematically investigated the temporal dynamics of BOLD responses 608 

to continuous naturalistic features during movie watching. By integrating multi-level features— 609 

ranging from low-level sensory properties (luminance, contrast, pitch) to physiological (pupil size) 610 

and subjective cognitive (ToM) signals—we evaluated how well conventional hemodynamic 611 

modeling captures the brain's nested temporal hierarchies. To overcome the limitations of fixed-612 

delay assumptions, we utilized FIR deconvolution alongside traditional cross-correlation 613 

analyses to systematically map the true BOLD response profiles across the whole brain. 614 

Our results demonstrate that different classes of naturalistic features exhibit distinctly 615 

different temporal BOLD profiles that reflect their position in the cortical processing hierarchy. 616 

Our main findings are threefold. First, while low-level sensory features generally aligned with the 617 

temporal expectations of the canonical HRF, slower physiological and subjective cognitive 618 

features systematically deviated from these standard models. Second, convolving inherently 619 

delayed, low-frequency signals (such as pupil size and ToM ratings) with a standard HRF 620 

introduces severe temporal artifacts—such as the "double-smoothing" effect—which led to 621 

misalignments and even inverted zero-lag correlations. Finally, by employing flexible FIR 622 

deconvolution, we successfully uncovered the feature-specific BOLD system response functions. 623 

This approach allowed us to explicitly quantify peak response latencies, revealing extensive 624 

inter-regional temporal hierarchies. These empirically derived maps demonstrated a clear 625 

progression of processing delays from primary sensory cortices to higher-order associative 626 

networks. Together, these findings underscore the necessity of feature-specific temporal 627

4.1. Sensory features 631 

The present study demonstrated that for low-level sensory features—specifically frame 632 

luminance, frame contrast, and acoustic pitch—the canonical HRF implemented in standard 633 

fMRI analysis tools (e.g., SPM) effectively captured the temporal delay between stimulus 634 

dynamics and BOLD responses. Cross-correlation analyses revealed that the strongest 635 

correlations between these features and regional BOLD signals occurred at lags consistent with 636 

the canonical HRF peak (approximately 4–6 seconds). Similarly, FIR deconvolution yielded 637 

impulse response functions that closely mirrored the canonical HRF in modality specific brain 638 

regions, exhibiting the characteristic rise, peak, and undershoot phases. These results indicate 639 

that the temporal characteristics of the BOLD response to continuously varying visual and 640 

auditory inputs are largely consistent with those reported in conventional event-related 641 

paradigms, even under naturalistic viewing conditions. 642 

This finding aligns with classic event-related fMRI studies showing that BOLD responses 643 

in early sensory regions are highly stereotyped in both shape and latency (Boynton et al., 1996; 644 

Dale and Buckner, 1997; Glover, 1999; Handwerker et al., 2004). It also resonates with more 645 

recent research using naturalistic stimuli, which has shown that BOLD activity in visual and 646 

auditory cortices remains tightly coupled to sensory fluctuations over time (Bartels and Zeki, 647 

2004; Hasson et al., 2004; Nishimoto et al., 2011). Together, these studies support the notion 648 

that the canonical HRF offers a robust approximation of average neurovascular coupling in 649 

primary sensory areas across diverse experimental contexts. 650 

Nonetheless, subtle deviations in the FIR-estimated responses across cortical regions 651 

were evident, suggesting regional variability in HRF dynamics. Some of these differences likely 652 

stem from intrinsic vascular properties, such as baseline cerebral blood flow and local 653

4.2. Pupil size 675 

Previous fMRI studies have often modeled pupil diameter as a physiological regressor by 676 

convolving it with a canonical HRF before correlating it with BOLD signals (Schneider et al., 677 

2016; Yellin et al., 2015). This approach assumes that pupil size reflects instantaneous neural 678 

or arousal-related processes that are temporally blurred by the hemodynamic response. 679

approximately 5-second delay of the pupil response to frame luminance, which directly 681

When comparing correlations between BOLD activity and both HRF-convolved and raw 683 

pupil size time series, clear differences emerged. In visual cortical areas, where BOLD activity 684 

positively tracks luminance, pupil size is expected to show an inverse relationship due to the 685 

pupillary light reflex (Joshi and Gold, 2020; Mathot, 2018). Consistent with this prediction, 686 

negative correlations were observed between raw pupil size and visual cortex BOLD signals 687 

near zero lag. In contrast, when using the HRF-convolved pupil signal, these expected negative 688 

correlations disappeared and instead became artifactually inverted (positive) at zero lag, 689 

demonstrating a severe temporal misalignment between modeled and actual pupil–BOLD 690 

dynamics. Furthermore, the spatial extent of correlated regions differed markedly between the 691 

two approaches. While both revealed significant correlations in regions such as the insula and 692 

temporoparietal junction, the raw pupil signal additionally showed broader associations 693

decreased thereafter. This immediate peak strongly validates the use of the raw pupil trace, as 701 

the physically delayed peripheral signal naturally aligns with the rapid neural dynamics of these 702 

autonomic centers without the need for additional temporal shifting. 703

central arousal and neuromodulatory activity, particularly reflecting the influence of the locus 705

4.3. Theory of Mind Rating 735 

In this study, an independent group of participants provided continuous ratings reflecting their 736 

perceived understanding of the characters’ thoughts, feelings, and intentions while viewing short 737 

movie clips. These ratings are intended to index moment-to-moment engagement of ToM—the 738 

ability to attribute mental states to others (Frith and Frith, 2006; Schurz et al., 2021). Unlike low-739 

level sensory or physiological features, however, the temporal coupling between such 740 

introspective, socially grounded judgments and underlying neural activity is far less constrained 741 

and may vary with narrative structure, attention, and individual interpretation (Jacoby et al., 742 

2016; Richardson et al., 2018). 743 

Continuous introspective ratings inherently integrate information over several seconds 744 

due to cognitive evaluation and motor execution delays (Nummenmaa et al., 2012; Zahn et al., 745 

2009). Because the generation of a continuous behavioral rating is intrinsically a temporally 746 

smoothed process, applying an additional HRF convolution to these time series is 747 

mathematically redundant. This secondary convolution compounds the temporal dynamics, 748 

creating a "double-delay" that fundamentally shifts the regressor out of phase with true neural 749 

activity, while also over-smearing the signal variance. The improved spatial consistency 750 

observed across videos when using raw (unconvolved) ToM ratings is thus likely attributable to 751 

both the adjustment of this temporal misalignment and the avoidance of this redundant 752 

smoothing. Furthermore, because the raw ToM time series already represents a delayed 753 

behavioral output relative to the initial neural event, performing FIR deconvolution directly on 754 

this trace logically yields an accelerated estimated hemodynamic peak (as the behavioral 755 

latency accounts for a portion of the typical neurovascular lag). This accelerated response 756 

profile—as empirically confirmed by our FIR estimations—explicitly demonstrates why the 757

Furthermore, our FIR deconvolution results demonstrate that ToM processing dynamics 760 

are fundamentally stimulus- and context-dependent. While low-level sensory features—such as 761 

frame contrast and auditory pitch—exhibited a clear temporal hierarchy with processing delays 762 

propagating sequentially from primary sensory cortices to higher-order associative networks, 763 

ToM processing did not follow this pattern. Specifically, the peak lag distributions for ToM during 764 

Despicable Me did not exhibit a clear feed-forward cortical hierarchy; instead, the temporal 765 

delay distributions were remarkably similar and widespread across all the functional networks. 766 

This simultaneous, distributed temporal profile likely reflects the highly multimodal and 767 

continuous nature of the mentalizing required for this specific film, which demands the 768 

concurrent integration of rapid dialogue, abstract social rules, and dynamic multi-character 769 

interactions across the brain, rather than a simple sequential processing stream. 770 

These complexities highlight the profound challenges of mapping continuous, high-level 771 

social cognition onto temporally sluggish BOLD signals. Our results converge with recent efforts 772 

advocating explicit examination of temporal relationships between behavioral regressors and 773 

neural responses in naturalistic paradigms (Di and Biswal, 2022; Lahnakoski et al., 2012b). 774 

Rather than assuming a canonical HRF—or relying solely on raw time series—data-driven 775 

approaches such as FIR deconvolution are strictly essential to capture the highly context-776 

dependent and feature-specific speeds of social cognition. Future work could further disentangle 777 

these overlapping temporal profiles by integrating synchronized physiological measures (e.g., 778 

facial EMG or reaction-based time stamping) to refine lag estimation, or by employing advanced 779 

regularization techniques to account for the inherent collinearity of cinematic features. 780

A striking observation in our analysis was the substantial variability in brain responses across 783 

the three films. While sensory features generally exhibited more consistent activations across 784 

datasets, there were notable exceptions—such as widespread cortical responses to frame 785 

contrast in The Present and to auditory pitch in Despicable Me . This variability was even more 786 

pronounced for high-level cognitive features like ToM. Specifically, ToM processing did not 787 

uniformly engage classical mentalizing or default mode network regions across all datasets. 788 

This divergence likely stems from profound differences in narrative complexity and the specific 789 

nature of the ToM content elicited by each film. For example, Despicable Me involves complex, 790 

dialogue-driven social rules and dynamic multi-character interactions, which manifested as 791 

widespread, distributed cortical engagement. In contrast, short animations like The Present or 792 

Partly Cloudy rely heavily on non-verbal, visually driven social cues, which may engage a 793 

different, more restricted sub-network of social-perceptual regions. Thus, the neural correlates 794 

of "ToM" during naturalistic viewing are not monolithic; they are highly context-dependent and 795 

intrinsically tied to the statistical and narrative structure of the specific stimulus. 796 

Beyond narrative content, inter-stimulus variability is also driven by fundamental 797 

differences in data acquisition and technical parameters. The statistical power and temporal 798 

resolution varied considerably across datasets: Despicable Me contained 740 time points (TR = 799 

800 ms), The Present contained 240 time points (TR = 800 ms), and Partly Cloudy was 800 

acquired with a significantly slower temporal resolution (TR = 2000 ms) and only 168 time points. 801 

The extended data length in Despicable Me likely afforded higher statistical power, which 802 

partially explains the more widespread network recruitment observed for both ToM and sensory 803 

features in this video. Furthermore, differences in TR inherently impact the temporal precision of 804 

FIR deconvolution, as higher temporal resolution allows for more accurate estimation of 805 

response peaks compared to slower acquisitions. 806

during naturalistic viewing is the inherent collinearity of stimulus features. Unlike highly 808

4.5. Broader implications for hemodynamic response 829 

The present analysis provides robust empirical evidence that the HRF can be estimated directly 830 

from continuous naturalistic data using FIR modeling. Traditional HRF estimation approaches 831 

have primarily relied on event-related or block designs, where each stimulus or trial is modeled 832 

as a discrete impulse (Dale and Buckner, 1997; Glover, 1999). Although such methods have 833 

been instrumental in characterizing canonical HRF properties, they inherently simplify the 834

temporal and cognitive structure of real-world experiences. Event-based paradigms constrain 835 

the complexity and duration of modeled processes and often assume that cognitive or affective 836 

responses occur as isolated, time-locked events rather than as continuously evolving states. 837 

In contrast, naturalistic paradigms offer an alternative framework by leveraging time-838 

varying sensory, behavioral, or physiological features as continuous inputs to estimate neural 839 

response functions. This approach captures more ecologically valid dynamics of neural activity, 840 

encompassing sustained attention, emotional engagement, and social inference—processes 841 

that unfold gradually rather than discretely (Hasson et al., 2004; Nastase et al., 2019). Although 842 

continuous designs face challenges such as limited variability in specific features and uneven 843 

temporal sampling across frequency ranges, they allow HRF estimation in higher-order cortical 844 

and subcortical regions that are less reliably engaged by simple, transient stimuli. 845 

The current findings demonstrate the utility of using continuous naturalistic features to 846 

perform data-driven HRF deconvolution across multiple brain regions. The FIR model, which 847 

imposes minimal assumptions about response shape or latency, revealed systematically diverse 848 

temporal profiles and hierarchical peak latencies across regions, consistent with prior evidence 849 

that HRF dynamics vary with vascular and neuronal properties (Aguirre et al., 1998; 850 

Handwerker et al., 2004; Lindquist et al., 2009). Such flexibility is valuable for uncovering 851 

region-specific or stimulus-specific variations in hemodynamic coupling that are not well 852 

captured by canonical models. 853 

However, this flexibility comes at the cost of increased variance and reduced reliability, 854 

particularly when the number of model parameters approaches the available data length (Goutte 855 

et al., 2000; Pedregosa et al., 2015). Future research should explore hybrid or regularized 856 

approaches—such as ridge-regularized FIR models, basis function modeling (e.g., gamma or 857 

spline basis sets), or parametric HRF estimation—to balance flexibility with stability and 858 

physiological interpretability (Lindquist et al., 2009; Pedregosa et al., 2015). Comparative 859

An additional priority is to assess the reliability and inter-individual variability of HRF 862

Finally, integrating FIR-based HRF estimation with analyses of sensory, physiological, 871

align well with the canonical HRF, whereas physiological (pupil) and introspective (ToM rating) 874 

signals exhibit more complex, delayed timing relationships, confirms that hemodynamic coupling 875 

differs systematically across cortical processing hierarchies and temporal receptive windows. 876 

Data-driven HRF estimation, therefore, provides not only a methodological advance but also a 877 

conceptual framework for understanding how distinct neural systems integrate information over 878 

time. Extending this approach across diverse stimulus types and participant populations could 879 

yield a more comprehensive characterization of the temporal architecture of brain function under 880 

naturalistic conditions. 881
