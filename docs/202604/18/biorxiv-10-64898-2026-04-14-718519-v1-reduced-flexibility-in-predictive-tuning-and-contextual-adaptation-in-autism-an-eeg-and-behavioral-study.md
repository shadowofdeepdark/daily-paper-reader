## 摘要
大脑会生成预测，以便为即将发生的事件做好准备。由于环境并非完全可预测，大脑同时会估计这些预测的确定性，并据此调整准备过程。鉴于自闭症个体常常对日常生活中的细微变化表现出抵触，我们假设自闭症个体在预测确定性的调节方面存在差异。为验证这一假设，我们在一项概率提示目标识别任务中记录了青少年和年轻自闭症成人（n = 20）以及与之年龄和智商匹配的非自闭症成人（n = 19）的脑电（EEG）。该任务的提示有效性系统性地分为四个水平：100%、84%、67%及33%。参与者并未被告知提示与目标的有效性，也不知道其何时改变。我们关注两种预期准备的神经标志——关联性负变（contingent negative variation，CNV）和 α 频段事件相关去同步化（α-band event-related desynchronization，-ERD），以及一种认知更新的标志——目标刺激和无效刺激（例如替换了目标的非目标刺激）引发的 P3。总体而言，随着情境确定性降低，准备活动增强，低概率情境中的目标前出现较大的 CNV 幅度和更强的 -ERD，这表明在更大不确定性下，准备性投入增强。此外，较大的 CNV 幅度预测了更快的反应时间，说明预期动态具有功能意义。然而，在自闭症组中，神经准备及反应时间随提示与目标概率变化的调节显著减弱。此外，自闭症参与者在目标和无效刺激的 P3b 反应中均表现出概率依赖性的调节减弱；在非自闭症参与者中观察到预期活动（CNV）与随后的更新（P3b）之间的耦合，而在自闭症中此耦合缺失。总体来看，这些发现表明，虽然自闭症个体的预测机制仍然存在，但其预期过程对情境不确定性的调节较不灵活，并且与后续认知更新的连接较弱。这种降低的适应性可能反映了在调整内部预测模型以应对变化环境时的困难，可能导致自闭症的核心特征，如抗拒变化和坚持一致性。

## Abstract
The brain generates predictions to prepare for upcoming events. Because the environment is not perfectly predictable, the brain also estimates the certainty of these predictions and adjusts preparatory processes accordingly. Given that autistic individuals often resist even small changes to everyday routines, we hypothesized altered tuning of prediction certainty in autism. To test this, EEG was recorded from adolescents and young autistic adults (n = 20) and from age- and IQ-matched non-autistic adults (n = 19) during a probabilistic cued target identification task during which cue validity was systematically varied across four levels: 100%, 84%, 67%, and 33%. Participants were not informed of the cue-target validity nor when it changed. We focused on two neural signatures of anticipatory readiness, contingent negative variation (CNV) and alpha-band event-related desynchronization (-ERD), and one of cognitive updating: the P3 to targets and to invalid (e.g., a non-target in place of the target) stimuli. Across groups, preparatory activity increased as contextual certainty decreased, with larger CNV amplitudes and stronger -ERD preceding targets in lower-probability contexts, suggesting enhanced preparatory engagement under greater uncertainty. Furthermore, larger CNV amplitudes predicted faster reaction times, indicating functionally significant anticipatory dynamics. However, modulation of both neural preparation and response times as a function of cue-target probability was significantly reduced in the autistic group. In addition, autistic participants showed diminished probability-dependent modulation of the P3b to both targets and invalid stimuli, and coupling between anticipatory activity (CNV) and subsequent updating (P3b) was observed in non-autistic participants whereas it was absent in autism. Together, these findings suggest that while predictive mechanisms are present in autism, anticipatory processes are less flexibly tuned to contextual uncertainty and less effectively linked to subsequent cognitive updating. This reduced adaptability may reflect difficulty adjusting internal predictive models to changing environmental contingencies, potentially contributing to core features of autism such as resistance to change and insistence on sameness.
