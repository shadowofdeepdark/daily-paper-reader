Abstract 11 

Brain–body interactions (BBIs) are fundamental to cognition and mental health, but their 12 

continuous multimodal dynamics remain difficult to extract. Previous approaches have been 13 

largely observational, and few frameworks enable these interacting processes to be modeled 14 

within an integrated generative system. Here, we applied a Predictive-Coding-Inspired 15 

Variational RNN (PV-RNN) to simultaneous EEG, ECG, and respiration recordings obtained 16 

from 33 participants during exteroceptive and interoceptive attention. The model learned a 17 

temporal hierarchy spanning modality-specific dynamics, multimodal associative integration, 18 

and sequence-level global states, and accurately reconstructed unseen physiological 19 

sequences. Specifically, the intermediate associative layer successfully captured the core 20 

complexities of BBI by extracting multiscale, nonlinear, and bidirectional coupling dynamics with 21 

variable temporal lags. Furthermore, the estimated precision (inverse variance) of latent 22 

variables representing BBI dynamics within this multimodal associative layer increased 23 

significantly during interoceptive attention. The magnitude of this condition-dependent precision 24 

enhancement correlated positively with subjective adaptive body controllability and negatively 25 

with psychiatric vulnerabilities, including rumination and trait anxiety. These findings identify a 26 

latent physiological signature of interoceptive attention and establish hierarchical generative 27 

modeling as an interpretable framework for extracting continuous BBI dynamics and linking 28 

multimodal physiology to cognitive and clinical characteristics. 29

Introduction 2

Brain–body interaction (BBI) has become a central topic in cognitive neuroscience 3

because cognition is increasingly understood as a process shaped by ongoing 4

interactions between neural and bodily activity (Buzsáki & Tingley, 2023; Ribeiro & 5

Oliveira-Maia, 2025). Physiological dynamics in visceral signals and their neural 6

processing influence a diverse range of cognitive functions, including attention 7

(Petzschner et al., 2019), emotion (Critchley & Garfinkel, 2017), memory (Messina et al., 8

2022; Umeda et al., 2016), decision-making (Fujimoto et al., 2021; Herman et al., 2021), 9

self-representation (Babo-Rebelo et al., 2019), thought (Ito et al., 2019; Sakuragi et al., 10 

2024), and behavior (Johannknecht & Kayser, 2022; Palser et al., 2021), and their 11 

dysregulation has been implicated in mental health and psychiatric vulnerability 12 

(Gregorio & Battaglia, 2024; Khalsa et al., 2018). Despite this importance, current 13 

approaches often capture only partial aspects of BBI, reducing the brain–body system 14 

to isolated indices rather than treating it as an integrated dynamical process. 15 

Existing approaches have provided important but still fragmentary insights. Event-based 16 

methods, such as heartbeat-evoked or respiration-locked neural responses, have 17 

greatly advanced interoception research by isolating afferent processes tied to specific 18 

physiological events (Poppa et al., 2022; Schandry et al., 1986; von Leupoldt et al., 19 

2011). For instance, these methods have demonstrated that cortical responses to 20 

individual heartbeats are modulated by interoceptive attention (Petzschner et al., 2019). 21 

However, these approaches reduce continuous multimodal dynamics to discrete events 22 

and are less suited for describing ongoing interactions across bodily systems. Time-23 

series approaches, including mutual information and transfer entropy (Antonacci et al., 24 

2020; Martínez Vásquez et al., 2023; Pernice et al., 2019), have overcome the need for 25 

event segmentation, revealing how directed information flow between the brain and 26 

body is modulated by changes in cognitive and affective states. While these metrics 27 

quantify directed information flow, their reliance on static pairwise summaries limits their 28 

ability to capture continuous and state-dependent nonlinear dynamics. Although a 29 

recent model incorporates physiological constraints to address non-linearity and 30

These limitations arise in part from the structure of BBI itself. Neural activity exhibits fast 3

and irregular fluctuations, whereas cardiac and respiratory signals show slower rhythms, 4

event-like morphologies, and variable timing. Their interactions are nonlinear, 5

distributed across multiple timescales, and often involve bidirectional influences with 6

unknown or variable lags (Candia-Rivera, 2022; Lin et al., 2016; Schiecke et al., 2019). 7

Given this complexity, conventional measures of brain–body coupling that focus solely 8

on statistical dependencies among observed signals leave it unresolved whether the 9

observed relationships arise from direct interactions, temporally lagged influences, or 10 

common latent drivers. Although recent multimodal deep-learning methods offer greater 11 

flexibility for integrating heterogeneous physiological data, they have largely been 12 

developed for multimodal fusion and downstream predictive tasks rather than for 13 

modeling the latent dynamical structure of those interactions (Kline et al., 2022; 14 

Pillalamarri & Shanmugam, 2025). A generative framework could address these 15 

limitations by modeling the hidden dynamical structure through which these signals are 16 

co-generated over time, allowing BBI to be examined as an inferred latent interaction 17 

process rather than only through its observable traces (Durstewitz et al., 2023). From 18 

these perspectives, a useful generative model of BBI should 1) preserve modality-19 

specific multiscale structure, 2) capture bidirectional and nonlinear cross-modal coupling 20 

with temporal lags, 3) integrate time-varying uncertainty for state-dependent and 21 

modality-specific fluctuations, and 4) yield interpretable latent representations for 22 

application to cognition and mental health. 23 

Here, we address this gap using a predictive-coding-inspired variational recurrent neural 24 

network (PV-RNN) trained on simultaneous EEG, ECG, and respiration recorded during 25 

focused attention on external or internal stimuli (Shinagawa et al., 2025). PV-RNN 26 

combines deterministic and stochastic latent variables within a temporal hierarchy 27 

(Ahmadi & Tani, 2019; Idei et al., 2022; Takahashi et al., 2025), providing the 28 

representational capacity to model BBI, where interactions unfold across multiple 29 

timescales, include substantial noise and variability, and depend on probabilistic 30

We evaluate the model in a stepwise manner that aligns with the generative 12 

requirements of BBI discussed above. First, we assess whether PV-RNN can accurately 13 

reconstruct multimodal data at the waveform level preserve physiologically meaningful 14 

structures. Second, building on this generative capability, we examine whether the 15 

intermediate associative layer captures multimodal coupling. Specifically, using ablation 16 

and dynamical analyses, we examine how these shared latent variables contribute to 17 

multimodal generation and assess whether their dynamics are consistent with key 18 

aspects of BBI, including multiscale dynamics, nonlinear interactions, bidirectionality, 19 

and temporal lags. Third, we evaluate the construct validity of the extracted latent states 20 

by assessing their correspondence with experimentally manipulated attentional 21 

conditions and known physiological coupling patterns. Finally, we investigate whether 22 

the highest-level latent representations spontaneously segregate global sequence-level 23 

information, such as distinct attentional states and individual identity. By framing BBI as 24 

hierarchical multimodal generation, this study offers a computational framework for 25 

moving beyond fragmented physiological indices. 26

Results 1

Datasets and Model Overview 2

> Training and Test

3

To establish a computational framework for investigating BBI, we required a dataset 4

providing simultaneous recordings of brain activity and autonomic signals. Furthermore, 5

to enable evaluation of the extracted BBI dynamics, the dataset needed to explicitly 6

encompass distinct cognitive states in which dynamic modulations of BBI are 7

theoretically expected. Therefore, we focused on how attentional allocation is 8

modulated between the external environment and internal bodily sensations. We trained 9

the PV-RNN using a dataset comprising the simultaneous EEG, ECG, and respiratory 10 

(RESP) recordings acquired during exteroceptive (Sound Focus; SF) and interoceptive 11 

(Breath Focus; BF) attention tasks (Shinagawa et al., 2025). In SF, participants 12 

responded as quickly as possible to randomly presented pure tones (inter-tone interval: 13 

1.2–2.0 sec), whereas in BF they responded at the end of each exhalation; the same 14 

pure tones were also presented in BF to equate the auditory environment. In both 15 

conditions, participants performed self-reports when they noticed their attention had 16 

drifted away. We extracted the periods 1–5 s preceding these reports, representing 17 

intervals when participants were presumably focused on the task (i.e., on sounds or 18 

their breath). Each sequence was 4 sec long sampled at 100 Hz (400 time points) and 19 

comprised 19 EEG channels, 2 RESP channels, and 1 ECG channel; the 19 EEG 20 

channels were selected to broadly cover the scalp. 21 

The model was trained for multimodal generation on 660 sequences from 33 22 

participants (10 SF and 10 BF per participant). After training, to verify that the capability 23 

of multimodal data generation is not driven by overfitting on training datasets, we fixed 24 

the synaptic weights and evaluated whether accurate reconstruction could be achieved 25 

solely by inferring the latent state. This generalization was tested on two distinct 26 

datasets: held-out sequences from trained participants (cohort test for within-subject 27 

generalization; n = 30, 180 sequences), and sequences from completely novel 28 

participants (unobserved participant test for across-subject generalization; n = 9, 54 29

> System Overview

4

The model has a hierarchical spatiotemporal architecture (Fig. 1a) (Ahmadi & Tani, 5

2019; Idei et al., 2022; Takahashi et al., 2025; Yamashita & Tani, 2008) designed to 6

represent the specific characteristics of the dataset across three levels: modality-7

specific modules at the lowest level capturing the independent, fast dynamics of each 8

signal (e.g., ECG, Resp, EEG), a multimodal-associative level integrating shared cross-9

modal information at intermediate timescales, and a global state level capturing 10 

sequence-level contexts, such as individual- or task-related overall dynamic changes, at 11 

the highest level. In the present study, latent variables in the global state layer were 12 

treated as sequence-specific constants (i.e., fixed within each sequence and not 13 

updated over time), so that they encode stable, sequence-level context while moment-14 

to-moment dynamics are captured by lower levels. Dynamics at each level are 15 

implemented by stochastic latent variables (    ), which encode level-specific 16 

uncertainty, together with deterministic states (  ), which carry timescale-dependent 17 

recurrent dynamics. 18 

PV-RNN is trained as a dynamic generative model (Fig. 1b). During the training phase, 19 

model parameters are optimized across sequences, while latent states are inferred to 20 

explain the observed multimodal time series. Given an observed sequence, latent states 21 

are initialized from the prior and used to generate top-down predictions for each 22 

modality. The discrepancy between predicted and observed signals is then used to 23 

iteratively refine latent states and synaptic weights, yielding posterior latent states that 24 

better explain the observed time series. In the test phase, all model parameters were 25 

held fixed, and only latent variables were optimized to reduce prediction error for each 26 

sequence, yielding posterior latent variables that better explain the observed time series. 27 

Additional details of this inference scheme are provided in Supplementary Fig. 1-2 and 28

PV-RNN accurately reconstructs multimodal EEG–ECG–respiratory time series 1

First, we assessed whether the trained PV-RNN accurately reconstructs multimodal 2

data at the waveform level and preserves physiologically meaningful structures. 3

Following training, the PV-RNN learned a stable generative model of multimodal 4

dynamics across EEG, ECG, and RESP signals in both BF and SF conditions. In both 5

datasets, the reconstructed signals appeared physiologically plausible on visual 6

inspection. Fig. 2a shows representative reconstructed sequences of unobserved 7

datasets, illustrating that the model captures diverse temporal structures across 8

modalities. The trained model accurately reconstructs both fast EEG fluctuations and 9

slower respiratory dynamics within a single hierarchical generative model. In addition, it 10 

reproduces event-like ECG morphology with a stereotyped waveform shape while 11 

allowing variability in event timing, consistent with the quasi-deterministic shape and 12 

stochastic occurrence of cardiac cycles. Remarkably, prediction errors for unobserved 13 

participants were as low as those for within-cohort held-out sequences, with error 14 

distributions showing substantial overlap at small magnitudes (Fig. 2b). These results 15 

suggest that the model captured multimodal physiological data faithfully at the waveform 16 

level and that downstream inferences on latent states and their associations are unlikely 17 

to be driven by overfitting or idiosyncratic properties of the training datasets. 18 

To further examine whether the model reconstructed physiologically meaningful 19 

structure beyond the waveform level, we compared the actual physiological signals from 20 

the training datasets with the reconstructed data pooled across both within-cohort and 21 

unobserved test sequences. The two datasets showed substantial overlap in several 22 

canonical summary properties (Fig. 2c), including the mean power spectral density 23 

across EEG channels, the distribution of mean RR interval, and the distribution of 24 

within-sequence inhalation-phase duration. These results indicate that the reconstructed 25 

data preserved meaningful statistical properties of each modality. Furthermore, 26 

heartbeat-evoked potentials, serving as a representative marker of BBI, showed similar 27 

waveform profiles between the actual and reconstructed data, with a typical early 28 

component clearly evident in the model outputs (Coll et al., 2021). Thus, the generative 29 

process reproduced not only the superficial multimodal waveforms but also their 30

Multimodal-associative layer latent variables capture multimodal coupling 12 

dynamics 13 

Second, building on this generative capability, we evaluated whether the intermediate 14 

associative layer captures multimodal coupling. We conducted the ablation study by 15 

removing each probabilistic latent variable in turn and evaluating its influence on the 16 

generation of each modality (see Methods section). Briefly, we fixed their activities to 17 

zero during the generation process and measured how the reconstruction error for each 18 

signal was increased using a paired t-test between the full model and ablation model. 19 

Fig. 3 shows the percentage of total latent variables in each layer that actively influence 20 

each modality, indicating the extent to which they represent a specific modality. The 21 

ablation of latent variables in the executive layer revealed that they play distinct 22 

functional roles in representing individual identity and global states. Contrary to the 23 

expectation of a uniform global influence, the latent variables were categorized into 24 

'EEG only' (one variable), 'EEG + ECG' (two variables), and 'None' (one variable). The 25 

category 'None' indicates that ablating the variable did not produce a significant 26 

increase in reconstruction error in any modality under the present analysis. This 27 

distribution suggests that the task-condition and individual-trait structure self-organized 28 

in the executive layer is reflected primarily in brain dynamics and brain–heart 29 

interactions, rather than in a uniform influence across all modalities. Furthermore, the 30 

absence of top-down influence on respiration implies that basal autonomic rhythms may 31 

be represented by lower-level dynamics. However, it is important to note that the lack of 32

Ablating latent variables in the multimodal-associative layer revealed that these 3

variables contributed to the generation of physiological data in three distinct ways. The 4

six variables demonstrated a global integration role, where their ablation simultaneously 5

disrupted the generation of all three modalities. The other eight variables exhibited 6

cross-modal influence, affecting pairwise combinations such as EEG–ECG, EEG–RESP, 7

or ECG–RESP. The remaining single variable impacted only the EEG signal. This 8

dominance of global and cross-modal influences—as opposed to isolated, modality-9

specific processing—indicates that this intermediate layer is highly specialized for 10 

linking brain and bodily signals. Consequently, these results show that the multimodal-11 

associative layer primarily functions to represent the BBI. 12 

Ablation of the probabilistic latent variables in the sensory modality layers had no 13 

significant impact on prediction generation, except for the EEG module. However, this 14 

result does not imply that these modalities are uninvolved in the data generation 15 

process. It is important to note that our ablation targeted only the probabilistic latent 16 

variables. In the PV-RNN architecture, predictive outputs are generated from the lowest 17 

layer via deterministic variables (  and ). The internal state   at each time step is 18 

determined by both top-down inputs (Eq. (1)) and local inputs. Therefore, if higher 19 

layers predict the signal dynamics and provide strong top-down signals, the contribution 20 

of local probabilistic states becomes negligible, leading to the ablation effect seemingly 21 

disappearing. Physiologically, respiration and heart rate are intrinsically linked (Yasuma 22 

& Hayano, 2004), and their activities are often reflected within EEG signals (Heck et al., 23 

2016; Schandry et al., 1986; Zaccaro et al., 2024). These results likely reflect the 24 

inherent characteristics of these signals: while EEG possesses unique components and 25 

complex dynamics not found in other modalities, ECG and respiration exhibit less 26 

unique fluctuation and are largely explainable by other modalities or top-down 27 

predictions. 28

To determine the directionality of interactions mediated by the multimodal-associative 4

layer, we quantified the Lagged Mutual Information (MI) between each latent variable 5

and global EEG activity, summarized as Global Field Power (GFP). By varying the time 6

lag, we identified the optimal temporal offset that maximized information sharing (Fig. 7

4b). Negative optimal lags indicated that brain activity preceded changes in the latent 8

variable. Because the ablation analysis showed that associative-layer variables are 9

necessary for the joint generation of brain and bodily signals (Fig. 3), these brain-10 

leading variables can be interpreted, with caution, as reflecting an efferent pathway from 11 

the brain to the associative layer to the body. In contrast, positive optimal lags indicated 12 

variables whose activity preceded global EEG fluctuations. As these same variables 13 

were also coupled to somatic signals, they may reflect afferent pathways from the Body 14 

to the Associative to the Brain. Although these pathway labels remain inferential, the 15 

broad distribution of optimal lags indicates that the model captured variable temporal 16 

delays characteristic of physiological loops. Together, these findings suggest that the 17 

associative layer acquired a nonlinear, multiscale, and bidirectional representation of 18 

BBI. 19

The most important mechanistic result is that the multimodal-associative layer appears 3

successful acquisition of these complex BBI dynamics can be attributed to two specific 10 

architectural properties of the PV-RNN. First, the intrinsic recurrent and state-dependent 11 

nature of the RNN enables the latent space to approximate nonlinearities that linear 12 

models fail to capture. Its memory mechanism bridges variable time lags between the 13 

brain and body, while its dynamic internal states allow the model to represent complex, 14 

context-modulated interactions. Second, the spatiotemporal hierarchy plays a crucial 15 

role in integration. The emergence of distinct oscillation peaks within the multimodal-16 

associative layer indicates that distinct timescales from the lower modality-specific 17 

layers are integrated into a single latent dynamic. This layer functions as a common 18 

source for top-down predictions. By minimizing prediction errors through predicting the 19 

future states of both brain and body from their shared past states, this hierarchical 20 

constraint effectively captures the bidirectional causality and variable lags inherent in 21 

BBI dynamics. 22

regarding physiological modulation, respiratory sinus arrhythmia—where heart rate 27

conditions having rumination as a core component of symptoms—as aberrant precision 8

weighting, wherein a failure in precision modulation amplifies interoceptive prediction 9

errors and impedes flexible adaptation to noisy ascending signals (Barrett & Simmons, 10 

2015; Paulus & Stein, 2010). While theoretical accounts posit altered BBI in these 11 

conditions, empirical evidence relying on conventional behavioral indices of cardiac 12 

interoception (e.g., heartbeat counting task) has remained inconsistent (Eggart et al., 13 

2019; Jenkinson et al., 2024). This inconsistency likely stems from the inherent 14 

limitations of static behavioral tasks, which capture generalized sensory accuracy and 15 

suffer from low construct validity. Overcoming these limitations, our generative modeling 16 

approach isolates the top-down control of BBI by extracting task-dependent precision 17 

modulation from multimodal physiological data, allowing us to translate the abstract 18 

concept of aberrant precision into a quantifiable dynamic. Specifically, individuals prone 19 

to rumination demonstrated a marked difficulty in enhancing BBI precision during 20 

interoceptive attention. This finding suggests that the altered processing of bodily 21 

signals in psychiatric conditions is not simply a static sensory deficit or constant 22 

hypersensitivity, but fundamentally a failure of context-dependent precision reallocation 23 

(Stephan et al., 2016). Adaptive regulation requires flexibly modulating precision 24 

weighting across internal and external signals according to task demands. The inability 25 

of highly ruminative individuals to make this shift indicates that their inferential system is 26 

too rigid to redistribute precision across changing contexts. For instance, when 27 

confronted with uncomfortable physical sensations or distress, individuals with such 28 

computational rigidity may fail to adaptively down-regulate their bodily signals, 29 

becoming trapped in the unpleasant state rather than flexibly shifting their interoceptive 30 

focus. This computational deficit thus provides a mechanistic explanation for the 31

It is necessary to address a methodological concern in multimodal modeling, whether 4

the shared latent space captures common noise sources, such as cardiac or respiratory 5

motion artifacts contaminating the EEG signal (Urigüen & Garcia-Zapirain, 2015). At first, 6

we implemented a preprocessing pipeline—including 0.5-Hz high-pass filtering to 7

remove low-frequency drifts and independent component analysis to eliminate cardiac 8

artifacts—which substantially attenuates the influence of these non-neural components 9

within the EEG signals. Beyond these signal-level corrections, the empirical 10 

relationships detailed above provide the strongest conceptual evidence against the 11 

artifact hypothesis. Within our model's hierarchical architecture, latent dynamics in the 12 

associative layer are governed by the higher-level global state layer, which self-13 

organizes to encode individual identity and attentional context (Fig. 3). This top-down 14 

control suggests that the extracted dynamics reflect intrinsic, context-dependent 15 

representations rather than sequence-specific noise. Furthermore, the magnitude of the 16 

precision modulation systematically correlated with subjective adaptive body control and 17 

psychiatric traits. Residual physiological noise cannot account for such coherent 18 

dependencies on hierarchical cognitive contexts and multifaceted clinical phenotypes. 19 

Therefore, these findings demonstrate that our model captures conceptually valid BBI 20 

dynamics rather than physiological signal artifacts. 21

ECG, and respiration. While our current focus was on reconstructing and interpreting 23 

these BBI dynamics, the model's inherent generative capacity provides a highly 24

spontaneously self-organized to reflect both attentional conditions and individual 27 

differences without explicit labels. By naturally segregating broader context at the 28 

highest level while preserving moment-to-moment generative BBI mechanisms at lower 29

> Analysis of Latent Representation

11 

An analysis of the organized latent representation via the ablation of latent variables 12 

was conducted (Fig. 4). First, to establish the baseline variability, we used the trained 13 

network to generate 20 simulated time series for each signal under different random 14 

samplings of the latent states. We then calculated the deviations between these 15 

generated time series and the target data. The distribution of prediction error from the 16 

20 iterations for each modality (EEG, ECG, and respiratory) was defined as the baseline 17 

difference (BD). Next, to evaluate the impact of individual latent variables, we ablated 18 

one variable at a time. Using this lesioned network, we generated 20 simulated time 19 

series via random sampling of the remaining latent states. We then calculated the 20 

prediction error between these generated time series and the target data. The 21 

distribution of prediction error from the 20 iterations for each modality (EEG, ECG, and 22 

RESP) was defined as the ablation effect (AE). We compared the distribution of BD and 23 

AE using paired t-test. If the AE for a certain modality (EEG, ECG, and RESP) was 24 

larger than the corresponding BD, we considered that the ablated latent variable 25 

represented the affected modality information. For example, if the ablation of a latent 26 

variable of the multimodal-associative module affects EEG and ECG generation, the 27 

ablated latent variable is considered to represent both EEG and ECG and is a bimodal 28
